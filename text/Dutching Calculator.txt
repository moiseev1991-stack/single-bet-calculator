## How does a Dutching Calculator work?

A Dutching Calculator allocates your total stake across two or more mutually exclusive outcomes so that your total return (and therefore profit) is the same whichever selection wins. It does this by weighting each stake inversely to the outcome’s effective payout multiplier (decimal odds adjusted for any exchange commission). This lets you plan exact stakes ahead of placing bets with UK bookmakers or betting exchanges while accounting for rounding and commissions.

### What inputs do you need (odds, stake, commission)?

To produce a precise staking plan, you should provide the following information for each bet you intend to place. These inputs ensure the calculator can equalise returns and highlight any expected loss (overround) or guaranteed profit (arb) before you commit funds.

- Total stake (S): the total amount you want to distribute across all selections (e.g., £100).
- Selections and odds: the decimal, fractional, or American odds for each mutually exclusive outcome (e.g., Home, Draw, Away in football).
- Commission per selection (optional): the exchange commission rate (as a percentage) charged on net winnings for that selection; bookmakers typically have 0% commission on winnings.
- Odds format: choose fractional or decimal (most common in the UK); American is often supported for convenience.
- Rounding mode: how you want stakes rounded (e.g., to the nearest £0.01, £0.05, or £0.10) to match operator stake limits.
- Constraints (optional): any minimum/maximum stakes or step sizes imposed by specific firms.

### How are stakes split to target equal profit?

The calculator equalises total return K (stake plus profit) across all outcomes. Without commission, stakes are proportional to 1/Oi (where Oi is decimal odds). With exchange commission ci (expressed as a decimal, e.g., 0.02 for 2%), stakes are proportional to 1/Ei, where the effective payout multiplier is Ei = Oi − ci(Oi − 1). This accounts for commission on profit only (stake is not commissioned).

Compute the effective multiplier for each selection:
 
- No commission: Ei = Oi
- With commission: Ei = Oi − ci(Oi − 1)

- Sum the reciprocals: H = Σ (1 / Ei)
- Equalised return (constant across outcomes): K = S / H
- Stake for selection i: si = K / Ei
- Resulting profit (same whichever wins): P = K − S

- Rounding to currency increments can slightly alter K; the calculator rebalances so stakes sum to S.

## Which markets can you Dutch effectively?

Dutching works best where the available selections are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive, meaning exactly one selection will win and all possible results are fully covered. In the UK, that usually includes football 1X2 (win–draw–win), tennis match winner, and horse racing win-only markets. Markets where multiple selections can win simultaneously (for example, place markets) or where your chosen set does not cover every possible outcome make strict equal-return Dutching impossible or fragile. Always check market rules, settlement, and any commission before placing bets.

### Can you Dutch football win–draw–win or correct score?

Yes. Football 1X2 (win–draw–win) is a textbook Dutching market because exactly one of the three outcomes will settle as a winner. You simply input the three prices (adjusting for any exchange commission), and the calculator weights stakes so the total return is equalised. Correct score can also be Dutched, but only if your selection set is exhaustive: you must include every listed discrete score plus any “Any Other…” selections that capture unlisted scorelines (market conventions vary by bookmaker or exchange). Missing even a single reachable scoreline invalidates the hedge.

- 1X2 checklist: cover Home, Draw, Away; confirm settlement (90 minutes only unless otherwise stated); apply commission if using an exchange.
- Correct score checklist: include all listed discrete scores and any “Any Other Home Win/Draw/Away” selections required by that market to cover high-score outcomes.
- Risk controls: place all legs quickly to reduce slippage; watch for stake limits and price changes that can shift the equalised return.

### Accumulator dutching: is it possible and how to approach?

It is possible in principle, but rarely practical. To Dutch accumulators you must cover every mutually exclusive combination of your chosen legs, then allocate stakes across those accumulator lines so the return is equal. For two 1X2 matches, that’s 3 × 3 = 9 exclusive acca lines; for three matches it becomes 27, and so on—an exponential growth that multiplies overround, liquidity risk, and operational complexity.

- Enumerate exclusive scenarios: list every combination of leg outcomes (e.g., H1/D1/A1 × H2/D2/A2).
- Price each combo: multiply decimal odds per leg (adjust for commission where applicable) to get each accumulator price.
- Allocate stakes: apply the standard Dutching formula across those acca prices.
- Execution: place all accas without delay; check max-payout caps and correlated-market restrictions (many firms disallow certain same-game combos).

- Practical alternative: Dutch singles within each event (e.g., 1X2) rather than trying to Dutch across multiple events via accas.
- Permutation bets (Trixie/Patent/Yankee) distribute risk but are not strict Dutching and will not produce equal returns across all scenarios.

### Does Dutching work for each-way horse racing?

Strict Dutching (equal return regardless of which selection wins) does not work for each-way as a single combined bet. An each-way bet splits your stake into a win part and a place part; if your horse wins, both parts pay, creating overlapping returns that break the “one winner only” requirement. Place markets also allow multiple runners to succeed simultaneously, so backing several runners to place cannot be Dutched to an exact equal return.

- Win-only Dutching: valid. Choose multiple runners in the win market and equalise returns normally.
- Approximating each-way coverage: treat the win and place components separately—Dutch the win market; then size place bets for coverage, accepting that returns will vary if more than one selection places.
- Place terms: bookmakers commonly quote place fractions such as 1/5 or 1/4 of the win odds and pay a set number of places depending on race type and field size (terms vary by race and firm).
- Converting to place odds (for planning): decimal place odds = 1 + (Owin − 1) × f, where f is the place fraction (e.g., 0.20 for 1/5).
- Dead-heats and reductions: settlement rules can alter returns; always check race terms, potential Rule 4 deductions, and maximum-payout limits.

If your objective is a guaranteed equal return, stick to win-only Dutching on horse races. If you use each-way for coverage, model scenarios and aim for an acceptable minimum return rather than an exact equalised figure.

### How to use or build a Dutching Excel template?

The core of a Dutching sheet is straightforward: compute an effective payout multiplier for each selection (odds adjusted for commission), sum their reciprocals, solve for the constant equalised return, and allocate stakes accordingly. The steps below outline a transparent, auditable template using decimal odds and per-selection commission.

Set up headers in row 1:
 
- A1: Selection
- B1: Decimal Odds (O)
- C1: Commission (c)
- D1: Effective Multiplier (E)
- E1: 1/E
- F1: Stake (£)
- G1: Return if Wins (£)

Inputs:
 
- Enter each selection’s decimal odds in column B (e.g., rows 2–6).
- Enter per-selection commission c in column C as a decimal (e.g., 0.02 for 2%; use 0 for bookmakers).
- Set total stake S in a dedicated cell, e.g., H2 (label H1 as “Total Stake”).
- Optional: Set rounding increment in H3 (e.g., 0.01 for penny rounding) and label H3 as “Rounding Inc.”

Formulas (copy down as needed):
 
- D2 (Effective multiplier): =B2 - C2*(B2-1)
- E2 (Reciprocal): =1/D2

Harmonic sum and equalised return:
 
- H4 (label “H = Σ(1/E)”): =SUM(E2:E6) [adjust E6 to your last row]
- H5 (label “K = Equalised Return”): =H2/H4
- H6 (label “Profit = K − S”): =H5 - H2

Stake allocation:
 
- F2 (stake for row 2, with rounding): =ROUND($H$5/D2, ROUND(LOG10(1/$H$3),0)) if H3 holds a power-of-10 increment (e.g., 0.01). Simpler: =MROUND($H$5/D2, $H$3) in Excel supporting MROUND.
- Copy F2 down to all but the last active row.
For the last active row only (say row 6), set the stake as the remainder to ensure stakes sum exactly to S:
 
- F6: =$H$2 - SUM(F2:F5)

- G2 (return if wins): =F2*D2 (copy down). This should be approximately equal to K in H5 for all rows; minor differences arise from rounding.

Quality checks:
 
- Total staked: =SUM(F2:F6) should equal S (H2).
- Variance check: MAX(G2:G6) − MIN(G2:G6) should be small; reduce rounding increment if variance is too high.
- Overround/arb: Profit (H6) negative implies an expected loss due to overround; positive implies an arbitrage across your prices.

Optional features:
 
- Fractional-to-decimal helper: If fractional odds a/b are entered in helper cells, decimal = 1 + a/b.
- Dynamic range: Convert the grid to an Excel Table so Σ and copying automatically adjust to added rows.
- Per-operator constraints: Add MIN/MAX checks and flag when a calculated stake breaches a limit.

With this template, you retain full visibility over every calculation: effective multipliers E = O − c×(O−1), the harmonic sum of reciprocals, the equalised return K, and any deviation introduced by rounding or stake limits. Save separate versions for football (3 outcomes) and horse racing (variable runners) to streamline routine use.

## The other side of the coin: When is Dutching not worth it?

Dutching can be mathematically precise but becomes poor value or operationally risky when the market overround is large, when stake limits prevent you from placing the calculated amounts, or when prices move while you are executing. In these cases, the equalised return falls below your total stake, or your hedge is broken and you are exposed to a specific outcome. Always weigh frictional costs (commission, rounding, withdrawal fees), execution risk (slippage, partial fills), and operator rules before committing funds.

### What risks come from overrounds and stake restrictions?

Overround is the built-in margin in a market: if you sum implied probabilities from all outcomes (using decimal odds), values above 100% indicate a negative expectation for backers. Even if you split stakes optimally, a positive overround guarantees an expected loss after commission. Stake restrictions and payout caps can also prevent you from placing the stake size required to maintain equal returns, leaving you under-hedged on some outcomes.

Overround impact:
 
- Overround (H) = Σ(1/Oi) using decimal odds Oi. H = 1 is fair, H &gt; 1 implies an expected loss; commission further worsens H via lower effective multipliers.
- Equalised return K = S/H, so with H &gt; 1 and stake S = £100, you necessarily have K &lt; £100 (a loss before any other costs).

Stake restrictions:
 
- If a bookmaker caps your stake below the calculator’s requirement (e.g., you need £32.03 but are limited to £25.00), the affected outcome’s return falls below K, creating a hole in the hedge.
- Max-payout limits can also bind when long prices are involved; always check per-bet and per-day caps before placing.

Commission and fees:
 
- Exchanges typically charge a percentage on net winnings (commonly around 2%–5%), which reduces the effective payout multiplier E = O − c×(O−1).
- Payment and withdrawal fees, if any, reduce net ROI and should be factored into your plan.

### How do odds moves, slippage and partial fills hurt ROI?

Any change in an input price between calculation and placement alters the harmonic sum H and therefore the equalised return K. Slippage (worse price than quoted) and partial fills (especially on exchanges) can convert a near-breakeven plan into a clearly negative one, or break the hedge entirely on one outcome. Recalculate immediately after any change and before submitting remaining bets.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I check if my Dutching prices create an arbitrage?

You have an arbitrage if the harmonic sum H = Σ(1/Ei) is below 1, where Ei are effective decimal odds after commission. Example: two-outcome market with O1 = 2.10 and O2 = 1.95 at 0% commission gives H = 1/2.10 + 1/1.95 = 0.4762 + 0.5128 = 0.9890; staking £100 yields K = £100/H ≈ £101.11 and a risk-free profit P ≈ £1.11.

### Can I mix bookmaker and exchange bets in one Dutch?

Yes, treat each selection’s effective odds separately by applying its own commission and then equalise returns. For instance, Obook = 3.40 (0% c) stays E = 3.40, while Oexch = 3.10 at 2% becomes E = 3.10 − 0.02×(3.10−1) = 3.058; this change alone raises H by about 1/3.058 − 1/3.10 ≈ 0.0015, trimming a £100 equalised return by roughly £0.15.

### How do I include a free bet (stake not returned) in a Dutching plan?

Model a free bet with effective odds E = O − 1 because the stake is not returned. Example: a £25 free bet at O = 5.0 has E = 4.0, so its return contribution is 25×4 = £100; cash legs use E = O (or O − c×(O−1) on exchanges), and you then split stakes so each outcome returns the same K.

### What happens to a Dutch if one leg is voided or a match is postponed?

If a leg is voided, its stake is returned and your equalisation breaks; you must remove that selection, recompute H with the remaining Ei and rebalance stakes. Example: with two legs staked £60 at 2.10 and £40 at 2.90, voiding the £40 leg leaves an unhedged £60 exposure; without recalculation your win and loss scenarios diverge by the entire £40.

### How do dead-heats and Rule 4 deductions affect a dutched horse-racing plan?

A dead-heat for k horses divides your win stake by k, making the effective multiplier approximately Edeadheat = O/k, so a 2-way tie at O = 6.0 returns s×(6.0/2) = 3.0s instead of 6.0s. A Rule 4 deduction of, say, 20p in the pound scales the profit part by 0.80, giving adjusted E ≈ 1 + (O−1)×0.80; at O = 6.0 this becomes 1 + 5×0.80 = 5.0.

### What rounding increment keeps equal returns within a tight tolerance?

Rounding each stake to step Δ changes its return by at most about (Emax×Δ)/2 for the selection with the highest effective odds Emax. To keep return variance under £0.50 when Emax = 6.0, choose Δ ≤ 0.50×2/6 ≈ £0.17; using £0.10 caps per-leg deviation near £0.30, and assigning the final leg as the remainder forces the stakes to sum exactly to S.

### How many selections can I Dutch practically and what minimum stake do I need?

You need total stake S at least equal to n×min_stake to clear operator minimums across n legs; with a £0.10 minimum, S must be ≥ £1.00 for 10 legs and ≥ £5.00 for 50 legs. Execution time and slippage risk scale roughly with n, and each extra leg adds 1/Ei to H, which can turn a marginal edge into a loss if prices are not strong.

### Is Dutching viable in in-play markets with fast price moves?

It is fragile unless you can submit all legs within a few seconds; even a one-tick move can erase your edge. Example: dropping from 3.00 to 2.96 increases that leg’s 1/E by about 0.3333 − 0.3378 = −0.0045, which for a three-way book typically cuts a £100 equalised return by ~£0.45; two similar moves can flip a 0.5% edge negative.

### How do I get the combined price for “any of my selections to win”?

The combined break-even decimal price is 1/H where H = Σ(1/Ei); your equalised return ratio is K/S = 1/H. Example: if H = 0.989, the group price is 1/0.989 ≈ 1.011, implying a 1.1% edge; if H = 1.021, the group price is ≈ 0.979, signalling an expected 2.1% loss before other costs.

### Is Dutching legal in the UK and are there taxes on winnings?

Using a calculator to plan stakes is legal; you must be 18+ and bet with UK-licensed operators. As of 2026, individuals do not pay income or capital gains tax on gambling winnings in the UK, while operators pay gambling duties.

### What’s the practical difference between Dutching, hedging, and laying the field?

Back-Dutching splits stakes to equalise returns across outcomes, hedging adjusts positions to control downside, and laying the field risks a fixed liability against multiple outcomes. A back-Dutch on a set is algebraically equivalent to one lay on the complement with lay odds L = K/P and lay stake P, so liability (L−1)×P equals your total staked S.

### How do exchange commission and partial fills change the equalised return?

Commission reduces effective odds by c×(O−1), so O = 4.0 at 2% becomes E = 3.94, cutting return by £0.06 per £1 stake. If half your intended stake fills at 3.90 instead, E ≈ 3.842 for that portion, adding about 0.5×(1/3.842−1/3.94) ≈ 0.00325 to H and trimming a £100 equalised return by roughly £0.32.

### What quick checks confirm my Dutching plan is internally consistent?

Verify that sum of stakes equals S exactly, that each leg’s rounded return lies within about (Emax×Δ)/2 of K, and that P computed two ways matches: P = K − S and P = S×(1/H − 1). On a correct setup these numbers agree within rounding tolerance, typically a few pence for £0.01 steps.

### Can I replace multiple back bets with one lay bet to simplify execution?

Yes, if a single exchange market cleanly represents the complement event, you can lay it at L = K/P with lay stake P and liability S for the same economics. Example: with H = 0.98 targeting P = £5, K = £250 and S = £245, so a single lay at 50.0 for £5 has £245 liability and pays £5 if the laid event does not occur.

### How should I treat a selection priced in American odds when mixing with UK odds?

Convert first, then Dutch: for +m use O = 1 + m/100 and for −m use O = 1 + 100/m, then apply commission to get E = O − c×(O−1). Example: −150 converts to O = 1 + 100/150 = 1.6667; at 2% exchange commission the effective odds are E ≈ 1.6667 − 0.02×0.6667 = 1.6533.

### What’s the minimum total stake to respect operator stake floors across many legs?

Your total must be at least n times the highest minimum among your operators. If several legs must be placed at firms with a £0.50 minimum while others allow £0.10, and you have 12 legs with 5 at £0.50, the hard lower bound is 5×£0.50 + 7×£0.10 = £3.50; anything below this cannot be executed as calculated.
