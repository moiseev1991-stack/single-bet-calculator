## How does a Dead Heat Calculator work for UK betting?

A dead heat occurs when two or more selections tie for the same finishing position and cannot be split. In UK sportsbook settlement, a dead heat for the win divides your win stake by the number of tied runners and pays each fraction at full odds. For each-way place parts, if a tie spans the final paying place, the place stake is proportionally reduced using the formula: available places divided by the number of tied runners at that boundary. On exchanges (e.g., back bets on Betfair), the same mathematics applies to the backer’s side; layers pay a proportion of full liability. The calculator automates these rules consistently across horse racing, golf, and football place/tie markets.

### How do you compute dead heat payouts manually step by step?

Use the following universal procedures. They cover sportsbook win and each-way markets and align with how Betfair settles the back side of a dead heat.

Win market (sportsbook or exchange back):
 
- Identify the number of tied winners N.
- Divide the win stake by N: effective_win_stake = stake / N.
Settle at full odds:
 
- Fractional: return = effective_win_stake × fractional_odds + effective_win_stake.
- Decimal: return = effective_win_stake × decimal_odds.

- Total return is as above; profit = return − original stake.

Each-way place part (tie at the boundary):
 
- Work out places remaining when the tie occurs (P_avail) and how many tie (T_tied).
- Compute reduction factor R = P_avail / T_tied.
- Effective place stake = original_place_stake × R.
Convert win odds to place odds:
 
- Fractional place odds = win_fraction × place_fraction (e.g., 10/1 at 1/5 → 2/1).
- Decimal place odds = 1 + (decimal_odds − 1) × place_fraction.

Settle the place part at full place odds using the reduced stake:
 
- Fractional: return_place = effective_place_stake × place_fractional + effective_place_stake.
- Decimal: return_place = effective_place_stake × place_decimal.

Each-way scenarios without a boundary tie:
 
- If your selection finishes wholly within the paying places without a tie crossing the last place, no reduction applies to the place stake.
- Settle the win part separately (win/lose or win-dead-heat if applicable); settle the place part at full place odds with full place stake.

Exchange specific (Betfair):
 
- Back bets: identical mathematics to sportsbook on the backer’s side (stake divided by N, paid at full odds).
- Lay bets: liability is divided by N; the layer receives the untied proportion of the backer’s stake. Net figures then have commission applied.

For place dead heats at the boundary, reduce the place stake by P_avail/T_tied, then settle at place odds derived from the win odds.

- Convert fractional to decimal: decimal = 1 + (numerator/denominator).

- Fractional: place_fractional = win_fractional × place_fraction.
- Decimal: place_decimal = 1 + (decimal − 1) × place_fraction.

- Rounding: UK returns are typically rounded to the nearest penny after all multiplications and reductions.

## Horse racing, golf, and football: sport‑specific dead heat rules

Dead-heat settlement differs subtly by sport because the “official result” is defined by different governing bodies and competition formats. In the UK, bookmakers and exchanges apply standard mathematics (stake or liability division) to reflect shared outcomes, while respecting sport-specific result policies. For rule references and dispute resolution, see the [British Horseracing Authority](https://www.britishhorseracing.com/regulation/rules/), [The R&amp;A (Rules of Golf)](https://www.randa.org/rog/), the [UK Gambling Commission](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/), and [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com/).

### How are horse racing dead heats and each‑way place terms settled?

UK horse racing recognises dead heats when horses cannot be split at the line. Bookmakers and exchanges then share the outcome proportionally. Each-way (EW) terms introduce an additional step on the place part when a tie spans the final paying place.

Win market:
 
- If N horses dead heat for 1st, your win stake is divided by N and settled at full odds. Example: £20 at 4/1 in a two-way dead heat → effective win stake £10 → return £10 × 5.0 = £50.
- On exchanges, back bets mirror the same maths; lay liability is divided by N.

Each-way place part (boundary tie):
 
- Identify places paid and whether the tie crosses the last paying place.
- If a tie occurs for the final paying place, reduce the place stake by R = P_avail / T_tied, where P_avail is the number of places remaining at that point and T_tied is the number of tied runners.
- Settle the reduced stake at the EW place odds (win odds × EW fraction such as 1/5 or 1/4).

Each-way place part (no boundary tie):
 
- If the selection finishes wholly within the paid places and is not part of a boundary tie, the place stake is not reduced.

Other considerations:
 
- Rule 4 deductions (where applicable) are applied before dead-heat calculations.
- Best Odds Guaranteed affects the odds used, not the dead-heat method.
- Official results and any amendments follow [BHA Rules of Racing](https://www.britishhorseracing.com/regulation/rules/).

### How does the calculator handle golf ties and place markets?

Professional stroke-play events typically use a playoff to determine a single winner, so win-market dead heats are uncommon. However, ties for non-winning positions are frequent (e.g., T5, T8). For each-way outrights and finishing-position markets (Top 5/10/20), bookmakers apply the standard boundary-tie reduction on the place part.

Win market:
 
- If a playoff produces one winner, no dead heat applies.
- If an event declares joint winners (rare but possible), win stakes are divided by N co-winners and settled at full odds.

Each-way and finishing-position markets:
 
- When a tie spans the last paying place, reduce the place stake by R = P_avail / T_tied, then settle at EW place odds (e.g., 1/5, 1/4).
- If the selection finishes safely within the paying band with no boundary tie, no reduction is applied.
- Enhanced places (e.g., Top 8/10 in majors) use the same reduction logic when ties occur at the boundary.

Governance and data:
 
- Official finishing positions come from the tournament organiser; rules of play are governed by [The R&amp;A](https://www.randa.org/rog/) and the USGA.

### Does football have dead heats, and how are markets affected?

In football, traditional match markets (1X2) list the draw as its own outcome, so “dead heat” does not apply. Dead heats can arise in certain outright or player-prop markets, but many competitions have official tie-breakers that remove the need for a dead-heat split.

Markets where dead heat is unlikely:
 
- League position/Top 4/Top 6/Relegation: settled by official standings using competition tie-breakers (e.g., goal difference, head-to-head). No dead heat if a single official rank is assigned.
- Group Winner in tournaments: settled per official tie-breakers; dead heat is rare.

Markets where dead heat can apply:
 
- Top Goalscorer (league/tournament): if the official award is shared, or if the bookmaker’s terms do not adopt award tie-breakers (such as assists), your stake is divided by N joint winners and settled at full odds.
- Special props (e.g., “Most Cards” across a tournament) may use dead-heat rules if providers declare joint outcomes; check settlement provider and T&amp;Cs.

Regulatory note:
 
- Settlement follows bookmaker rules under the oversight of the [UK Gambling Commission](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/). Disputes can be escalated to [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com/).

## Betfair Exchange vs bookmakers: will results match?

Often, yes—because both sides apply the same dead‑heat mathematics: when selections share a position, your stake (or lay liability) is proportionally divided, and the winning portion is paid at full odds. However, results can still diverge due to exchange commission, different place terms, separate win/place markets on the Exchange, Rule 4 versus Exchange reduction factors, and rounding or settlement policy differences. For primary references, see [Betfair](https://betting.betfair.com/), [UK Gambling Commission](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/), and [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com/).

### What are Betfair dead heat rules and partial voiding?

On the Exchange, dead‑heat settlement is applied by apportioning your matched bet into winning and losing fractions. For back bets, your effective winning stake equals your original stake divided by the number of tied selections; for lay bets, your liability is divided by the same factor. Betfair then applies commission on your net winnings across the market.

Back bets:
 
- Win dead heat (N ties): effective winning stake = stake ÷ N; return = effective stake × decimal odds.
- Place dead heat at boundary: if P_avail places remain and T_tied runners share them, effective stake = stake × (P_avail ÷ T_tied), then settle at the matched place price.

Lay bets:
 
- Win dead heat (N ties): effective liability = full liability ÷ N (you pay out only that fraction).
- Place boundary tie: liability is multiplied by (P_avail ÷ T_tied) for runners tied at the last paying place.

Commission:
 
- Betfair charges commission (e.g., 2% default, varies by account) on net winnings, not on stakes. Payout = stake + profit − commission.

About “partial voiding”:
 
- In dead‑heats, Betfair does not normally void part of your bet; it proportionally settles it as part‑win/part‑lose using the factors above. The term “partial void” is more accurately “proportional settlement.”
- Voids on the Exchange typically relate to market cancellations or non‑runner handling (via reduction factors), not to dead‑heats themselves.

### How do major UK bookmakers settle dead heats?

UK bookmakers apply near‑universal dead‑heat principles aligned with industry norms (e.g., Tattersalls principles), with brand‑specific terms governing odds, place fractions, and deductions. The core arithmetic matches the Exchange on the backer side.

Win markets (fixed odds or SP):
 
- If N share 1st, divide your win stake by N and settle that fraction at full odds. The remaining fraction is treated as a loser.
- Example: £20 at 4/1, two‑way tie → effective win stake £10 → return £10 × 5.0 = £50.

Each‑way (EW) markets:
 
- Place part: if a tie spans the final paying place, reduce the place stake by R = P_avail ÷ T_tied, then settle at EW place odds (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4 of win odds).
- If the selection finishes wholly within the paying places without a boundary tie, the place stake is not reduced.

Common bookmaker policies:
 
- Rule 4 deductions (horse racing) apply before dead‑heat settlement.
- Best Odds Guaranteed, Extra/Enhanced Places, and specific market rules can alter odds or the number of paid places, but not the dead‑heat arithmetic.
- Rounding is typically to the nearest penny.

### Why might exchange and sportsbook payouts differ?

Even with matching dead‑heat arithmetic, several structural differences can lead to different returns.

Commission vs none:
 
- Betfair Exchange deducts commission from net winnings; sportsbooks do not charge commission on settled bets.

Market architecture:
 
- Sportsbooks offer each‑way (one bet split into win/place with a fixed place fraction). The Exchange offers separate win and to‑be‑placed markets with independently traded prices.

Place terms:
 
- Sportsbooks may offer Extra/Enhanced Places (e.g., Top 6 instead of Top 4) that do not exist on the Exchange, creating different winners.

Price source and timing:
 
- Fixed odds vs starting price (SP) vs Betfair Starting Price (BSP) can lead to different odds on the same selection at settlement.

Deductions model:
 
- Rule 4 (sportsbooks) vs Exchange reduction factors (Betfair) can yield slightly different adjusted odds after non‑runners.

Rounding and settlement minutiae:
 
- Rounding rules and how part‑win/part‑lose components are combined can create small penny‑level differences.

## Why do dead heat rules exist, and how did methods evolve?

Dead-heat rules exist to ensure fair and consistent settlement when competitors finish inseparably tied. In British horse racing, the concept predates modern technology and was originally resolved by “run-offs” (a deciding re‑run) or by mutual agreement to divide the prize. With the arrival of photo-finish cameras in the mid‑20th century and the standardisation of bookmaker terms, the industry converged on proportional settlement: divide the relevant stake (or liability) and pay at full odds. Oversight and dispute mechanisms in the UK involve the sport’s governing bodies and independent adjudicators under regulatory supervision.

### How did settlement evolve from coin tosses to shared prizes?

Across sports and contests, early tie-breaks sometimes used random devices (drawing lots or coin tosses) when no practical method existed to separate competitors. British horse racing followed a different path: run-offs were the formal mechanism for dead heats, with stakeholders often agreeing to share the prize instead of running again. As betting markets matured and photography improved result accuracy, settlement shifted decisively toward shared outcomes and proportional bet settlement to preserve fairness and consistency.

Pre–photo-finish era (19th–early 20th century):
 
- Horse racing: official rules provided for a run-off; owners could agree to divide the stakes and place money.
- Other sports/contests: ties sometimes resolved by drawing lots; coin tosses existed as tie-breaks in various events, but not as a standard for racing settlement.

Photo-finish adoption:
 
- United Kingdom: photo-finish technology was introduced after World War II (first used at Epsom in 1947), reducing—but not eliminating—dead heats.

Modern betting practice:
 
- Bookmakers and exchanges use proportional settlement: divide the stake (or lay liability) by the number of tied competitors and settle at full odds; place-part reductions apply at boundary ties.

### What dead‑end approaches failed fairness tests?

Several intuitive but flawed ideas have been rejected because they introduce bias, overpay relative to the betting book, or underpay versus customer expectations. Fairness is tested against symmetry (all tied selections treated identically), proportionality (payout scales with tie size), and arbitrage‑resistance (no “free money” from rule quirks).

Pay full odds to all tied winners without adjustment:
 
- Fails budget balance and arbitrage tests; total payout can exceed the pool/book’s implied probabilities.

Void tied outcomes entirely:
 
- Penalises customers who correctly predicted a joint outcome; violates symmetry and proportionality.

Halve the odds instead of dividing the stake:
 
- Can distort returns when combined with each‑way place fractions or complex multiples; stake division is consistent across odds formats and market structures.

Apply reductions after rounding each component separately:
 
- Early rounding can create penny-level biases; best practice is to calculate proportionally first, then round the final figure.

Ignore place boundary logic in each‑way markets:
 
- Paying full place stakes across boundary ties overpays and breaks parity between tied selections; proportional place‑stake reduction restores fairness.

### Who sets settlement standards in the UK (Tattersalls, IBAS, UKGC)?

Settlement is the product of sport‑specific rules, operator terms, and statutory oversight. Historically, the Tattersalls Committee articulated principles for betting disputes in British racing, including dead‑heat handling (run-offs or division). In the modern era, operators set detailed settlement terms consistent with the sport’s official results, and independent bodies adjudicate disputes under regulatory supervision.

Governing bodies for results:
 
- British horse racing: official results and queries are governed by the sport’s rules (e.g., steward enquiries) under the [British Horseracing Authority](https://www.britishhorseracing.com/regulation/rules/).

Operator terms and industry custom:
 
- Bookmakers and exchanges publish settlement rules in their T&amp;Cs; proportional dead‑heat settlement mirrors long‑standing custom historically associated with Tattersalls principles.

Independent adjudication:
 
- [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com/) (Independent Betting Adjudication Service) reviews customer disputes and issues rulings based on evidence and operator rules.

Regulatory oversight:
 
- The [UK Gambling Commission](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/) licenses operators and requires fair, transparent terms and effective customer redress mechanisms.

In practice, a fair settlement aligns four elements: the official result from the sport’s authority, the operator’s published rules, proportional dead‑heat mathematics, and the oversight/adjudication framework provided by [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com/) and the [UKGC](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/). When these are consistent, exchange and sportsbook outcomes remain predictable and auditable.

## The other side of the coin: Should you just trust settlement and skip calculators?

Operator settlement is authoritative because it follows published market rules and official results. A dead heat calculator is an audit and planning aid—it cannot override terms and conditions. The most credible argument against using one is that manual inputs and hidden assumptions (places paid, place fractions, commission, Rule 4 or reduction factors, rounding) can produce figures that differ from official settlement. Used carelessly, a calculator may create false disputes or poor hedging decisions. Used correctly, it helps you predict, verify, and budget for outcomes before and after settlement.

### What is the strongest argument against using a dead heat calculator?

The strongest argument is authority and context: only the operator’s market definition and settlement rules apply. A calculator—even a precise one—cannot “know” your bookmaker’s specific terms, exchange commission, enhanced places, or non‑runner handling unless you enter them perfectly. Any mismatch (e.g., entering 1/5 place terms when 1/4 applied, or forgetting commission on the Exchange) will yield a different number from the official payout, making the tool look “wrong” when your inputs were incomplete. For many straightforward markets (win-only with clear ties, or 1X2 football where a draw is its own outcome), trusting the published settlement is usually faster and safer.

- Settlement authority: bookmaking T&amp;Cs and exchange rules govern payouts; calculators are advisory.
- Input risk: missed enhanced places, wrong place fraction, omitted Rule 4 or Exchange reduction factors, or ignoring commission/rounding causes discrepancies.
- Non-transferability: some rules are market-specific (e.g., separate Exchange win vs place markets); generic calculators may not mirror every variant.
- Time cost: for simple, unambiguous results, checking the operator’s settled bet slip is quicker.

### When can calculators mislead: which edge cases should you avoid?

Calculators can mislead if you apply the right maths to the wrong market definition. Edge cases generally fall into five buckets: market definition, odds/terms, timing/deductions, multiples/cash‑out, and rounding/commission. If any of these are uncertain, verify the operator’s rules before relying on a calculator output.

Market definition pitfalls:
 
- Enhanced/Extra Places each‑way: using standard places when the bookmaker paid extra (or vice versa) changes boundary‑tie reductions.
- Exchange vs sportsbook: Exchange has separate place markets with their own prices; there is no “EW fraction.”
- Competitions with tie‑breakers (league tables, group winners): no dead heat if the official ranking breaks the tie.

Odds and terms:
 
- Wrong place fraction (1/5 vs 1/4) or mis‑converting fractional to decimal place odds.
- Ante‑post vs day‑of‑race: terms (places/Rule 4 applicability) can differ.

Timing and deductions:
 
- Rule 4 (sportsbooks) vs Exchange reduction factors: applied at different times; forgetting these skews results.
- Photo‑finish amendments: calculators may not reflect result changes if you calculate too early.

Multiples and cash‑out:
 
- Each‑way multiples: per‑leg dead‑heat reductions feed through complex returns; oversimplified calculators can misstate acca outcomes.
- Partial cash‑out or “Edit Bet”: settlement splits may no longer align with the original stake.

Rounding, fees, and liability:
 
- Exchange commission on net winnings vs no commission on sportsbooks.
- Penny rounding after all calculations; rounding too early introduces bias.
- Lay liability: treating lay bets like back bets leads to errors (liability, not stake, is apportioned).

### When does this tool actually save you time and money?

A robust dead heat calculator pays off when the market has genuine complexity or when you need pre‑bet planning. It helps you price edge cases, validate settlement within seconds, and size hedges accurately on the Exchange.

Pre‑bet planning:
 
- Estimate worst‑case place returns under boundary ties (e.g., 3‑way tie for 8th in Top‑8 golf) to decide stake size or whether to take Enhanced Places.
- Model Exchange hedges (back vs lay) under potential dead‑heat scenarios including commission.

Post‑result verification:
 
- Audit each‑way settlements where ties occur at the last paying place; confirm that the reduction factor was applied correctly.
- Check that Rule 4 or Exchange reduction factors and rounding were applied in the correct order.

Operational efficiency:
 
- Speed—produce an auditable breakdown you can compare with your bet slip.

- Predict lay liability under dead heat to avoid under‑covering positions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I work out P_avail in boundary ties like “joint second” for Top 3?

Count how many paid places are still open at the point of the tie, then divide by how many are tied. In a Top 3 where three runners are joint second, two places remain (2nd and 3rd), so P_avail = 2 and T_tied = 3, giving a reduction R = 2/3. Example: £15 place at derived place odds 3.0 with R = 2/3 settles as effective stake £10.00 and return £10.00 × 3.0 = £30.00.

### How are accumulators affected when a leg finishes in a dead heat for the win?

For that leg, the accumulator’s multiplier becomes decimal_odds ÷ N, because only 1/N of the stake is winning. Example: £10 treble at 3.0, 2.0, 1.8 with a two‑way dead heat on leg 1 settles as £10 × (3.0 ÷ 2) × 2.0 × 1.8 = £54.00, versus £108.00 without a dead heat on leg 1.

### How are each‑way doubles or accas settled when a place leg has a boundary dead heat?

Reduce that leg’s place stake by R = P_avail ÷ T_tied, settle at the leg’s place odds, then roll the resulting place return into the next place leg. Example: £5 each‑way double; leg 1 place odds 3.0 with R = 1/3 returns (£5 × 1/3) × 3.0 = £5.00, this £5.00 then goes at leg 2 place odds 2.5 to return £12.50 on the place side.

### How do I calculate a Betfair lay bet when the selection dead‑heats for first?

Divide your lay liability by N and remember you still win the losing fraction of the backer’s stake. Example: lay £10 at 5.0, liability £40; two‑way dead heat means you win £5 stake and lose £20 liability, net −£15 before commission (commission applies only to net wins, so £0 here).

### Do UK bookmakers round dead‑heat settlements the same way?

Most round to the nearest penny after all reductions and multiplications, not at intermediate steps. If £10 place stake faces R = 1/3 and place odds 3.0, the exact calculation uses £10 ÷ 3 = £3.333… then × 3.0 = £10.00, so holding precision until the end avoids penny drift that would show as £9.99 if rounded too early.

### What’s the correct order when Rule 4, each‑way, and a dead heat all apply?

Apply Rule 4 to the win odds first, convert to place odds, then apply dead‑heat reductions, and finally round. Example: £10 each‑way at 10/1 with a 20% Rule 4 becomes 9.0 decimal; place odds at 1/5 become 2.6; if three tie for the last place (R = 1/3), effective place stake is £3.333… and place return is £3.333… × 2.6 = £8.67 after rounding.

### Why is “halving the odds” wrong for dead‑heats with fractional prices?

Stake division is correct; halving fractional odds overpays because fractional odds exclude the returned stake. Example: £10 at 5/1 in a two‑way dead heat should return (£10 ÷ 2) × 6.0 = £30.00, but using 2.5/1 on the full £10 incorrectly pays £35.00; that extra £5.00 is an error.

### Can a dead heat ever pay the same as a clean place result?

Yes, when the tie is entirely within the paid band so R = 1. In a Top 4, if three runners tie for second, there are three paid places left at that point (2nd–4th), so P_avail = 3 and T_tied = 3, R = 1, and the place part pays in full with no reduction.

### How should I enter “Extra Places” each‑way offers to avoid dead‑heat mistakes?

Use the exact places paid on your bet and count P_avail from that number, not from the race’s standard terms. If you took Top 6 and five tie for 6th, P_avail = 1, T_tied = 5, so R = 1/5; the same selection would lose entirely if the market were Top 4 on an exchange.

### Can the calculator account for commission, currency, or taxes like Betfair Premium Charge?

It can apply exchange commission at a user‑set rate and round to your currency, but account‑level fees such as Premium Charge are not per‑bet and cannot be computed from a single market. For example, at 2% commission, £20 profit nets £19.60; Premium Charge, if applicable, is assessed later on cumulative weekly profit.

### Are dead‑heat rules the same in the US and Australia?

No. US pari‑mutuel pools split the pool among tied outcomes first, then derive dividends per $2, so a two‑way dead heat typically halves the dividend versus a sole winner. Many Australian bookmakers use UK‑style stake division, but tote pools follow pool‑split mathematics; this tool targets UK fixed‑odds and Betfair‑style settlement.

### How do I handle Top Goalscorer dead heats with each‑way terms and award tie‑breakers?

If the operator settles on joint winners, the win stake is divided by N and paid at full odds, and the place part follows boundary‑tie rules. Example: £20 each‑way at 10/1, 1/4 terms, three joint winners means win effective stake £6.67 at 11.0 returns £73.37, place odds 3.5 with no boundary tie return £20 × 3.5 = £70.00, total £143.37 before rounding.

### How do Rule 4 deductions differ from Betfair reduction factors numerically?

Both scale winnings, but Rule 4 is a percent deduction on the win portion used by sportsbooks, while Betfair’s reduction factor scales the net odds on matched bets. Example: a 20% Rule 4 on 11.0 reduces to 9.0; a 20% exchange reduction on 6.0 reduces to 1 + (6.0 − 1) × 0.8 = 5.0; in both cases apply dead‑heat maths thereafter.

### What’s the fastest way to audit a bookmaker dead‑heat settlement against your slip?

Confirm the official finishing positions, compute N or P_avail ÷ T_tied, apply any Rule 4 or exchange reductions before the dead‑heat step, then multiply through and round once. A two‑way win dead heat at 6.0 on £10 should always show £10 × (6.0 ÷ 2) = £30.00 on sportsbooks, while an exchange at 2% nets £29.60 after commission.

### How are free bets, bonuses, or stake‑not‑returned promos treated in a dead heat?

Divide the effective stake by N but remember stake‑not‑returned means only winnings are paid. Example: a £10 free bet at 5.0 in a two‑way dead heat has effective free stake £5 and pays £5 × (5.0 − 1) = £20.00, with no stake added back.

### How do I compute a lay bet on a place market when there is a boundary tie?

Multiply your lay liability by R = P_avail ÷ T_tied and note you still win the untied fraction of the backer’s stake. Example: lay £50 at 3.5 on Top 3, liability £125; if three tie for 3rd, R = 1/3 so you pay £41.67 liability, receive £33.33 of backer stake, net −£8.34 before any commission on wins.

### How precise should I be with repeating decimals such as R = 1/3?

Carry full precision through all steps and round only the final return to the nearest penny; this prevents 1p errors on ties. For £10 with R = 1/3 at place odds 3.0, using exact 10/3 yields £10.00, whereas early rounding to £3.33 would display £9.99.
