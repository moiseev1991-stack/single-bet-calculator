## How do I calculate Yankee returns and stakes?

A Yankee is a 4-selection multiple containing 11 bets: 6 doubles, 4 trebles, and 1 fourfold accumulator. There are no singles. To calculate returns, the calculator multiplies the decimal odds of each winning leg across every winning combination, then sums the returns from all winning doubles, trebles, and the fourfold. Total stake equals unit stake multiplied by the number of bets (11 for win-only; 22 for each way). Each way (EW) Yankees split the unit stake into two equal parts (win and place) and settle each part separately using the relevant odds/terms.

### Rule 4, dead-heat, void legs: how are they handled?

The calculator applies industry-standard settlement logic used by UK bookmakers so your Yankee reflects the same adjustments you would see on your bet slip.

Rule 4 (racing withdrawals):
 
- Applied as a pence-in-the-pound deduction to the winnings part of affected selections. The stake is not reduced.
- Implementation: Adjust the winnings portion by the deduction rate, then add back the original stake. Example: £1 at 2/1 (decimal 3.00) returns £3.00 without deductions. With a 10p Rule 4, the winnings (£2.00) are reduced by 10% to £1.80, so return becomes £1.00 + £1.80 = £2.80.
- Multiple withdrawals can lead to multiple deductions; calculators apply them sequentially to the winnings component.
- Rule 4 can affect both win and place parts in EW bets if the withdrawn runner changes the market terms.

Dead-heats:
 
- Win market: Divide the win stake by the number of tied competitors, settle one part as a full winner at the original odds, and treat the remainder as lost. Example: £1 at 3/1 in a two-way dead-heat returns £0.50 × (1 + 3) = £2.00.
- Each Way place market: If the dead-heat extends the number of placed horses beyond places paid, the place stake is split by the number of tied runners occupying the final paid place(s). Example: £1 EW, 3/1, 1/5 terms; two-way dead-heat for the last paid place settles the place stake as £0.50 at place decimal 1.60, returning £0.80.
- Dead-heat factors propagate through all affected combinations inside the Yankee (doubles, trebles, fourfold) that include the tied selection.

Void legs / Non-runners:
 
- Avoided selection is settled at odds of 1.00 (stake returned) within any combination; the combination’s fold reduces by one. Example: a double with one void leg settles as a single on the remaining selection.
In a Yankee, combinations containing the void selection are downgraded:
 
- Affected doubles become singles on the other selection.
- Affected trebles become doubles on the remaining two selections.
- The fourfold becomes a treble on the other three selections.

- If all legs in a component bet are void, that component returns the stake for that component.

Notes: Each Way is typically offered on racing and selected markets only; football Yankees are usually win-only. Rule 4 does not apply to fixed-odds lotteries (e.g., Irish Lottery) but dead-heat and void logic still follow operator rules. Always check the bookmaker’s published settlement terms for the specific market.

### Football Yankee: practical setup and tips

A football Yankee is typically win-only (no EW). Standard football settlement is 90 minutes plus injury time (extra time and penalties do not count unless explicitly stated by the bookmaker). To build a sound four-selection Yankee across football matches, ensure each selection is from a different game or a non-related market to avoid related-contingency restrictions.

Practical setup:
 
- Select four independent markets (ideally from four different matches), e.g., Match Result (1X2), Draw No Bet (DNB), Both Teams To Score (BTTS), Over/Under Goals.
- Confirm settlement scope: 90 mins only (most markets) unless the market title explicitly references Extra Time or Penalties.
- Enter odds for each selection (fractional or decimal) and set outcomes when known (Win/Lose/Void).
- If a DNB leg ends in a draw, it is typically settled “void” by bookmakers; in a Yankee, combinations including that leg are downgraded (e.g., doubles become singles).

Compliance and correlation:
 
- Avoid same-game related picks (e.g., Home Win and Home -1 Handicap in the same fixture) unless your bookmaker explicitly allows a same-game multi; these are usually not permitted inside standard Yankees.
- Player props (goals/cards/shots) are often restricted from cross-match multiples at many firms or must be built via a dedicated bet-builder; check operator rules.

Risk control:
 
- Spread selections across leagues and kick-off times to reduce postponement/abandonment correlation.
- Track “void” rules for postponements (operator-specific) and remember that any void football leg becomes a 1.00 multiplier inside each affected combination in the Yankee.

### Irish Lottery Yankee: numbers, odds, and limits

Irish Lottery “numbers betting” with UK bookmakers is fixed-odds betting on outcomes of the Irish Lotto draws. The Irish Lotto main draw selects 6 numbers from 1–47 (plus a Bonus Ball drawn from the same pool); bookmakers price specific number outcomes (e.g., “one number to be drawn”), and you can place multiples across different draws when allowed. Yankees on Irish Lottery bets are win-only (no EW) and do not use Rule 4 deductions.

Numbers and markets:
 
- Number range: 1–47. Bookmakers typically offer “1 number to be drawn,” “2 numbers to be drawn,” etc., as fixed-odds selections.
- Multiple rules: Most bookmakers require multiples to be placed across different draws to avoid related-contingency issues; same-draw selections are often restricted from being combined.

Odds handling:
 
- Odds are fixed by each bookmaker and shown at selection time; enter the quoted price into the calculator. Odds can differ between operators and may change between draws.
- No EW and no Rule 4. Dead-heat concepts do not apply in standard number betting because the draw outcomes are discrete and fully defined.

Limits and settlement:
 
- Stake limits and maximum payout caps are operator-specific; always check your bookmaker’s published limits before staking.
- If a draw is canceled or declared void per operator rules, affected legs in the Yankee are settled as void and combinations are downgraded accordingly.

Practical tip:
 
- Pick four independent selections across different draw times/days (e.g., lunchtime/teatime or different dates) to ensure your Yankee is accepted and to avoid correlation blocks.

## Yankee vs alternatives: what should you pick?

All three multiples—Yankee, Lucky 15, and Super Yankee (Canadian)—combine your selections into many smaller bets to spread risk and amplify returns when several legs win. The key differences are whether singles are included, how many combinations are created, how much your total stake grows per unit stake, and how many winners are needed for any payout. Choosing between them is mainly about variance tolerance and bankroll planning.

### Yankee or Lucky 15: which suits your risk profile?

A Yankee (4 selections, 11 bets) excludes singles, so you typically need at least two winners for any return. A Lucky 15 (4 selections, 15 bets) adds four singles, which cushions variance because one winner can still generate a payout, but it also increases total stake by 36% versus a Yankee at the same unit stake. If you prefer a steadier return profile and are willing to pay more stake for that stability, Lucky 15 fits better; if you want a leaner stake with higher concentration on multiples, choose a Yankee.

### Super Yankee/Canadian: how do 26 bets change returns?

A Super Yankee (also called a Canadian) uses 5 selections to create 26 bets: 10 doubles, 10 trebles, 5 fourfolds, and 1 fivefold. Compared with a Yankee, the Canadian increases both coverage and stake. With more combinations, partial success (e.g., two or three winners) still returns something via the many doubles and trebles, but your stake outlay rises proportionally (26 × unit stake for win-only; 52 × for Each Way).

Combination coverage by number of winners among 5 selections:
 
- Exactly 2 winners: C(2,2) = 1 winning double.
- Exactly 3 winners: C(3,2) = 3 winning doubles; C(3,3) = 1 winning treble.
- Exactly 4 winners: C(4,2) = 6 winning doubles; C(4,3) = 4 winning trebles; C(4,4) = 1 winning fourfold.
- All 5 winners: 10 winning doubles; 10 winning trebles; 5 winning fourfolds; 1 winning fivefold.

Stake planning:
 
- At £1 unit stake, a Canadian costs £26 (win-only) or £52 (EW). Adjust unit stake down if you need to match a fixed total budget.
- Voids reduce fold size within affected combinations (e.g., a treble with one void becomes a double).

Use cases:
 
- Five correlated events are generally not allowed; choose independent markets across different matches/races to comply with bookmaker rules.
- Useful when you expect several selections to win but want more ways to cash even if one or two lose.

### Each way Yankees: when does EW make sense?

Each Way (EW) Yankees are primarily for horse racing (and sometimes greyhounds) where place terms apply. An EW Yankee doubles the number of parts (win and place), so the total outlay is 22 × unit stake. EW can reduce variance when selections are at bigger prices and you believe they can finish in the places even if they may not win. Always use the exact place terms shown on the race (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4 the odds, with the stated number of places), which are set by the bookmaker for that market.

When EW may be appropriate:
 
- Competitive fields where your selections have strong place chances at double-digit fractional odds.
- Handicap races with more places paid (per the race card terms) versus small fields that are win-only.
- When you prefer smoothing returns across the 22 parts rather than concentrating solely on win lines.

Operational tips:
 
- Confirm that EW is offered on the race; small fields are often win-only.
- Enter the declared place fraction (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4) and the number of places in the calculator so the place part prices correctly.
- Remember that Rule 4 deductions (racing withdrawals) can apply to both win and place parts if the market terms are affected.

Responsible betting: set deposit and loss limits with your operator, and verify licence status on the UKGC public register at [https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/public-register](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/public-register). - Use operator tools such as deposit limits, time-outs, and reality checks; independent support is available via [https://www.gamcare.org.uk](https://www.gamcare.org.uk) and [https://www.begambleaware.org](https://www.begambleaware.org).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the break‑even odds for a Yankee with exactly two, three, or four winners?

Two winners must average about 3.317 decimal each to cover the 11-unit outlay; three winners break even around 1.555 decimal; four winners profit at any odds above 1.000. This comes from solving s·d^2 = 11s for two winners (d ≈ √11 = 3.317), s·(3d^2 + d^3) = 11s for three winners (d ≈ 1.555), and s·(6d^2 + 4d^3 + d^4) = 11s for four winners (d = 1.000), where d is the common decimal price and s is unit stake.

### How do I convert fractional odds and EW place terms to decimal in a Yankee?

Use decimal = 1 + (numerator ÷ denominator), and for places use place‑decimal = 1 + (numerator ÷ denominator × place‑fraction). For example, 10/3 becomes 4.3333; under 1/5 EW terms the place price is 1 + (10/3 × 1/5) = 1.6667; under 1/4 terms it is 1 + (10/3 × 1/4) = 1.8333.

### Will rounding per component change my total Yankee return?

Yes—penny rounding per double/treble/fourfold can move the total by a few pence on small stakes. Example: six identical £0.10 doubles at 1.83 × 1.83 return £0.33489 each; rounding per component pays 6 × £0.33 = £1.98, while aggregating first gives £2.00934 → £2.01, a 3p difference.

### How is a Rule 4 deduction applied inside a multiple?

Adjust the affected leg’s decimal to d′ = 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − r), where r is the Rule 4 rate (e.g., 0.10 for 10p). For instance, a 4.00 leg with a 20p Rule 4 becomes 1 + 3 × 0.8 = 3.40; in a £1 double with an unchanged 3.00 partner the return is £1 × 3.40 × 3.00 = £10.20 instead of £12.00.

### What happens to a Yankee if one leg is void or a DNB push?

Each affected component reduces by one fold and the voided leg is settled at 1.00, so its stake is simply carried through. A £1 double at 2.10 with the other leg void becomes a single returning £2.10; trebles with one void become doubles, and the fourfold becomes a treble.

### Do I pay tax on Yankee winnings in the UK in 2026?

No—customer winnings are paid tax‑free in the UK; the rate charged to bettors is 0%. Since 2001, general betting duties are levied on operators, not on recreational customers.

### Can I enter American odds or exchange prices in a UK Yankee calculator?

Convert American to decimal as 1 + (A/100) for positives and 1 + (100/|A|) for negatives; for example, +250 → 3.50 and −150 → 1.6667. Betting exchanges do not offer Yankees; if you model exchange commission on singles, apply it to winnings only (e.g., 5% of £10 winnings = £0.50 deducted).

### Why was my football Yankee rejected by the bookmaker?

Most rejections are for related contingencies such as multiple selections from the same match that cannot be combined in standard full‑cover bets. Other causes include market‑scope conflicts (e.g., mixing 90‑minute and extra‑time outcomes) or operator‑specific multiples rules; Draw No Bet legs that end drawn are usually settled void rather than causing rejection.

### How do dead‑heats affect EW Yankees in practice?

The relevant stake is split by the number of tied runners and settled at place odds for the paid places. Example: £1 EW component at 10/1 with 1/5 terms in a two‑way dead‑heat for the final place settles the place side as £0.25 at decimal 3.00, returning £0.75, while the win side loses.

### How does a “Total stake” input back‑solve the unit stake for Yankees and EW Yankees?

Unit stake is Total ÷ 11 for win‑only or ÷ 22 for EW, then rounded to the nearest penny without exceeding the budget. For £10 total, a win‑only Yankee uses £10 ÷ 11 = £0.909… which is typically set to £0.90 (total £9.90); for EW it is £10 ÷ 22 = £0.4545… set to £0.45 (total £9.90).

### Do bookmaker payout caps change Yankee returns compared with the calculator?

Yes—operators apply maximum‑payout limits that can override the raw calculation. If a calculator shows £300,000 but your bookmaker cap is £250,000 for that sport/market, the payout is limited to £250,000 even though the arithmetic return is higher.

### How can I estimate the expected value (EV) of a Yankee before placing it?

Sum s × P(all legs win) × product(decimals) over all 11 components and subtract the total stake 11s. As a check, four independent evens selections (each 2.00 decimal with 50% win probability) yield expected return 6 × 0.25 × 4 + 4 × 0.125 × 8 + 1 × 0.0625 × 16 = 6 + 4 + 1 = 11 per 11s outlay, so EV ≈ 0.

### Can I place an Irish Lottery Yankee on numbers from the same draw?

Usually no—most bookmakers bar same‑draw combinations to avoid related‑contingency risk. Build the Yankee across different draws; for example, spread four selections over separate lunchtime and teatime draws on different days so all legs are independent.

### Are free‑bet tokens usable on Yankees, and how are SNR returns calculated?

Most UK operators do not allow tokens on full‑cover bets like Yankees; where a token is allowed on a single accumulator, stake‑not‑returned pays s × (product(decimals) − 1). For example, a £10 SNR token on a 2.50 × 3.00 acca returns £10 × (7.50 − 1) = £65.00 instead of £75.00 with a cash stake.
