What is an Each Way Double Calculator and how does it work?

An each-way double is a multiple bet that combines two selections in two separate events, settled as two linked parts: a win double and a place double. Your total stake is split equally between the win part and the place part. The Each Way Double Calculator automates the settlement by applying each selection’s win odds and its each-way place terms (for example, 1/5 or 1/4 the win odds for a stated number of places). It rolls the return from the first leg into the second leg for each part separately, producing instant totals for win-only, place-only, and combined outcomes, with options to account for dead heats, Rule 4 deductions, or void legs where applicable.

What counts as an each-way double?

An each-way double consists of two selections, each settled on both the win and the place markets under the bookmaker’s each-way terms. Settlement is split into two independent pathways:

  • Win double: both selections must win for a return.
  • Place double: both selections must at least place (a win counts as a place) for a return at reduced place odds.

Outcome logic for a standard each-way double:

  • Both win: win double returns + place double returns are both paid and combined.
  • One wins and the other only places: win double loses; place double returns.
  • Both only place: win double loses; place double returns.
  • Any leg finishes outside the places: both parts that depend on that leg lose.
  • Void/non-runner leg: the multiple reduces (a double becomes a single) on the remaining leg for the affected part(s), settled at the original odds of that remaining leg.

Notes:

  • Each selection’s place terms (place fraction and number of places paid) are set by the bookmaker and can differ by event (e.g., horse racing vs golf outrights).
  • Dead heats and Rule 4 deductions (where applicable under Tattersalls rules) can affect the effective odds and therefore the final returns.

Odds format per selection:

How are win and place returns calculated and combined?

Settlement proceeds separately for the win double and for the place double, compounding the return from leg 1 into leg 2 within each pathway. Core formulas (fractional odds a/b):

  • Win return for a leg at fractional a/b with stake s: s × (1 + a/b).
  • Place odds for each-way fraction f (e.g., 1/5): place fractional odds = (a/b) × f; place return for stake s: s × [1 + (a/b) × f].
  • Decimal equivalents: win decimal D = 1 + a/b; place decimal Dp = 1 + (a/b) × f.

Should you add Rule 4, dead heat, or void legs?

Only add adjustments that actually applied to your markets. The calculator modifies effective odds or stake according to standard UK settlement conventions so your multiple reflects real-world settlement.

Rule 4 deduction (horse racing and some other racing):

  • When a withdrawal shortens the market before the off, a deduction in pence per £1 is applied to winnings (not the returned stake) based on the withdrawn runner’s price at time of withdrawal.
  • How to use: enter the deduction shown by your bookmaker (e.g., “10p in the £” = 10%). The calculator reduces the winnings portion for the affected leg before compounding into the next leg.

Dead heat (tie for a win or a place):

  • Win market: divide the stake by the number of tied runners; one portion settles at full odds, the remainder is lost. Return = (stake / N) × decimal odds.
  • Place market: if more horses share a place than places remaining, the place stake is divided by N and the appropriate portion(s) settle at place odds; any excess portions are treated as losers.
  • How to use: select the dead-heat share (e.g., 1/2 for a two-way tie) for the affected leg and market (win or place).

Void/non-runner:

  • If a leg is void or your selection is a confirmed non-runner, that leg is removed from the multiple for the affected pathway(s).
  • How it settles: an each-way double becomes a single on the remaining leg for win and/or place as applicable, at the original odds of that remaining leg.
  • How to use: mark the leg as void/non-runner; the calculator will reduce the multiple automatically.

Stake options: per-bet vs total stake explained

An each-way double consists of two lines: one win double and one place double. Your total outlay equals the sum of the stakes on these two lines. Calculators usually offer two input modes—choose the one that matches how you think about staking.

Total each-way stake (common default):

  • You enter the total amount across both parts (win + place).
  • Example: total £20 E/W → £10 on the win double and £10 on the place double.

Stake per part (unit stake):

  • You specify the stake that applies to each part separately.
  • Example: £12.50 per part → total outlay £25 (two parts: win and place).

What changes with the choice?

  • Returns scale linearly with the stake per pathway; the math is identical once the calculator splits the total stake.
  • For bankroll control, per-part staking lets you set an exact unit; total stake is convenient when matching a fixed overall spend.

Tip: When checking settlement against a bookmaker slip, confirm whether their “stake” figure refers to the total each-way amount or the unit stake per part. This avoids confusion when comparing calculated and official returns.

Is an each-way double better than other bet types?

Whether an each-way double is “better” depends on your goals: smoothing volatility, protecting stake in big fields, or maximising upside. Each-way doubles split your outlay between a win double and a place double, increasing the chance of some return when strong picks fail to win but still place. Pure win multiples concentrate the stake on outcomes that must all win, creating higher peaks but deeper drawdowns.

Each-way double vs win double: which fits your strategy?

Choose based on your edge, field sizes, and appetite for variance. Key differences:

Risk and variance:

  • Each-way double: two pathways (win + place) improve hit rate; lower variance.
  • Win double: all-or-nothing; higher variance and higher potential peak return for the same total stake.

When each-way shines:

  • Large competitive fields (e.g., big-field handicaps, golf outrights) with generous place terms (e.g., 1/5 odds, 5–10 places in specials).
  • Selections priced at each-way-friendly ranges (typically mid to bigger prices where the place part has non-trivial value).

When win-only is stronger:

  • Short-priced favourites where the place fraction yields minimal uplift.
  • Small fields paying 2 places at 1/5 where the place component is weak.

Settlement behaviour:

  • Each-way double: both must at least place to land the place-double; both must win to land the win-double.
  • Win double: both must win; a place result returns nothing.

Practical tip: If your model expects frequent places but fewer wins at bigger odds, the each-way double can stabilise bankroll swings without abandoning upside if both selections win (you receive both win- and place-double returns).

Double vs treble and accumulators: what changes in settlement?

As you add legs, compounding increases potential return but also increases the probability that one leg voids or loses and collapses the multiple. Settlement rules scale consistently:

  • Win double: 1 bet, both legs must win. A void reduces it to a single at original odds on the remaining leg.
  • Win treble: 1 bet, all 3 must win. A void reduces to a double; two voids reduce to a single.
  • Accumulators (4+ legs): 1 bet, all legs must win. Each void reduces the fold by one.
  • Each-way versions: create two parallel bets (win-path multiple and place-path multiple) using the relevant each-way place fractions for each leg.

Risk scaling:

  • Probability of full collection falls as legs increase (assuming independent legs).
  • Expected volatility rises with more legs; bankroll swings are larger for trebles/accas than doubles.
  • Void handling is protective but cannot rescue a losing leg; it only reduces fold size.

Patent, Yankee, and “2 singles and a double”: when to pick them?

Full-cover and partial-cover perms spread risk across multiple lines, trading a higher total outlay for more ways to get paid. Choose based on how many selections you have and how much protection you want from near-misses.

Patent (3 selections, 7 bets):

  • Composition: 3 singles, 3 doubles, 1 treble.
  • Returns with 1 winner (via a single); stronger payout as more win.
  • Each-way Patent = 14 bets (each line split into win and place).

Yankee (4 selections, 11 bets):

  • Composition: 6 doubles, 4 trebles, 1 fourfold (no singles).
  • Requires at least 2 winners for any return.
  • Each-way Yankee = 22 bets.

“2 singles and a double” (2 selections, 3 bets):

  • Composition: Single on each selection + a double on both.
  • Returns with 1 winner (via its single); if both win, the double adds upside.
  • Each-way version (both singles E/W + E/W double) = 6 bets.

Selection of perm type by objective:

  • Bankroll protection with 3 picks: Patent covers the downside with singles while keeping upside through the treble.
  • Higher-upside stance with 4 strong fancies: Yankee concentrates on multiples; no singles means more exposure to variance but better peak payouts for the same unit stake.
  • Two confident picks with insurance: “2 singles and a double” protects against a split while rewarding the 2/2 outcome.

Remember: voids reduce affected lines to the appropriate lower fold (e.g., a void in a Patent can turn a double into a single). Each-way variants duplicate the bet count and apply place terms to each relevant line, improving hit rate but increasing total stake.

Which sports and scenarios suit an each-way double?

Each-way doubles work best where place terms meaningfully reward near-misses and field sizes create real place value. In UK and Irish horse racing and in golf outrights, standard place frameworks and large fields can make the place path of the double worthwhile. In football outrights and player futures, each-way availability and place terms vary by bookmaker and competition, so value depends on how many places are paid and the prices you’re taking.

Horse racing and golf: how do place terms and field sizes affect returns?

Place terms determine the “place” odds as a fraction of the win price and how many finishing positions are paid. Bigger fields with more paid places improve the chance of cashing the place path in an each-way double, while a larger place fraction (e.g., 1/4 vs 1/5) increases the place payout multiplier. Is an each-way double viable in football outrights and player markets?

It can be, but value is situational. Availability and terms differ by bookmaker and competition, so confirm market rules before staking. General observations:

League winner each-way:

  • Bookmakers often pay places on top finish positions (commonly top 2, sometimes top 3 or 4 for specials). The place fraction is typically 1/4 or 1/5 of the win odds, but this is not uniform across firms.
  • Because leagues have fewer realistic contenders than big racing/golf fields, the “place” may carry less edge unless you’re backing bigger-priced teams.

Top goalscorer (league or tournament):

  • Each-way commonly available with places such as top 3–4 at 1/4 or 1/5 (varies by event and bookmaker).
  • Suitable for bigger-priced players where finishing in the places is materially likely even if the win is tough.

Building doubles:

  • Combine independent markets (e.g., a league outright with a separate league’s top scorer). Some combinations may be restricted; check your bookmaker’s multiple rules.
  • Short-priced legs reduce the value of the place component; each-way doubles are generally more attractive when at least one leg carries a bigger price with solid place prospects.

Bottom line: each-way doubles in football can smooth outcomes in long-season markets, but ensure the each-way terms (places paid and fraction) justify splitting your stake versus a straight win multiple or singles.

Doubles with 3 or 4 selections: are you actually after full-cover bets?

If you find yourself trying to back “all the doubles” across 3 or 4 picks, full-cover (or partial-cover) perms are purpose-built and usually simpler than placing many manual doubles. Consider the following:

Three selections:

  • Trixie (win-only): 4 bets — 3 doubles + 1 treble. Each-way Trixie: 8 bets.
  • Patent: 7 bets — 3 singles + 3 doubles + 1 treble. Each-way Patent: 14 bets.
  • Pick Trixie if you do not want singles; choose Patent if you want a payout with one winner.

Four selections:

  • Yankee (win-only): 11 bets — 6 doubles + 4 trebles + 1 fourfold. Each-way Yankee: 22 bets.
  • Lucky 15: 15 bets — adds 4 singles to the Yankee structure. Each-way Lucky 15: 30 bets.
  • Use Yankee/Lucky 15 if your aim is to cover every double combination without manual entry.

Strategic considerations:

  • Perms trade higher total stake for more ways to collect (especially with singles included).
  • Each-way versions double the number of bets and apply place terms to each relevant line, improving hit rate but increasing outlay.
  • Voids reduce affected lines to the appropriate lower fold automatically (e.g., a voided treble within a Yankee becomes a double).

Rule of thumb: if your priority is to land returns from partial success across several fancies, a full-cover or Lucky-style bet is usually a cleaner approach than trying to replicate the structure by placing ad hoc doubles across 3–4 selections.

Expert insight: history, common pitfalls, and the strongest counterargument

Each-way doubles have been settled under long-standing UK rules frameworks (including Rule 4 deductions and dead-heat conventions) that predate modern tools. Calculators emerged to reduce human error and to model “what-if” scenarios before placing a bet. Understanding where calculators fit—and where they can diverge from bookmaker-specific rules—helps you use them effectively while managing variance and your bankroll responsibly.

A brief evolution: from manual settling to calculators

Settlement moved from pen-and-paper and desk calculators to spreadsheets, then to purpose-built online tools and bookmaker betslips. Each step improved speed and reduced arithmetic mistakes but also introduced differences in rounding, rule interpretation, and handling of promotions.

Manual era:

  • Shop staff used printed tables and pocket calculators, applying Rule 4 and dead-heat splits by hand under UK conventions.
  • Accuracy depended on training; complex multiples were time-consuming and error-prone.

Spreadsheets:

  • Punters built templates to settle fractional and decimal odds and to cascade returns across legs.
  • Flexible but easy to misconfigure (e.g., incorrect place fractions or rounding).

Online calculators:

  • Added EW fractions, places paid, Rule 4, dead-heat, void legs, and odds format conversion.
  • Great for pre-bet planning and cross-checking settlement expectations.

Bookmaker betslips:

  • Show estimated returns using the operator’s own rules, including offer- and market-specific terms.
  • Final settlement still depends on official results, deductions, and house rules at “the off.”

What’s the strongest argument against using a calculator?

The strongest argument is that an independent calculator may not perfectly mirror a specific bookmaker’s house rules, promotions, or market-specific terms—leading to a mismatch between your estimate and official settlement. Key divergence points include:

Each-way terms and timing:

  • Places paid and fractions can depend on field size at the off; late non-runners can alter terms.
  • “Extra places” and specials vary by bookmaker and event; generic tools may not replicate them.

Rule 4 handling:

  • Different firms may publish different deduction tables or apply multiple withdrawals sequentially.
  • Some promotions cap or waive deductions; calculators usually apply standard tables.

Dead-heat conventions:

  • Stake division and place sharing rules are consistent in principle but edge cases (e.g., ties spanning remaining places) can be nuanced.

Rounding and stake treatment:

  • Rounding precision (e.g., to 2dp vs 4dp) and free-bet settlement (stake not returned) can shift totals.
  • Best Odds Guaranteed (where offered) and early/starting price rules are operator-specific.

Balanced practice:

  • Match inputs to your slip exactly: odds format, EW fraction, places paid, Rule 4, voids, and dead-heat shares.
  • Use calculators for planning and cross-checking; treat the bookmaker’s rules and official return as final.
  • If in doubt, consult your bookmaker’s rules or the UK regulator’s guidance at [gamblingcommission.gov.uk](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk).

How do you use results responsibly: bankroll, limits, and variance?

Calculators show potential returns; they do not change probabilities. Use outputs to size bets within a risk framework that protects your bankroll against normal variance, especially with multiples.

Bankroll structure:

  • Define a dedicated betting bankroll separate from living expenses.
  • Typical unit sizing for recreational bettors: 0.5%–2% of bankroll per “line.” An each-way double has two lines (win + place).

Staking methods:

  • Flat staking: same unit per bet; simple and variance-friendly.
  • Proportional staking: fixed % of current bankroll; scales risk as bankroll changes.
  • Kelly fraction (advanced): stake ∝ edge/odds; consider fractional Kelly to reduce drawdowns.

Variance awareness:

  • Doubles have lower hit rates than singles; each-way reduces variance by adding a place-path but does not eliminate losing runs.
  • Correlated selections reduce diversification; avoid combining highly correlated outcomes unless rules allow and you intend that risk.

Operational discipline:

  • Predefine session loss limits and stop-loss points.
  • Track results vs expected value; review if actual place rates deviate materially from assumptions.
  • Avoid chasing losses or increasing stakes to “win back” quickly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do Rule 4 deductions apply to each leg of an each-way double and how do they change the final return?

A Rule 4 reduces only the winnings portion of the affected leg, and it is applied separately per leg and per pathway (win and place). The adjusted decimal for a leg is D' = 1 + (D − 1) × (1 − r), where r is the deduction as a decimal (e.g., 10p in the £ = 0.10). For example, £10 each-way double (total £20), leg 1 at 8/1 (D=9.0) with 10p Rule 4 and leg 2 at 4/1 (D=5.0) with 20p Rule 4. Win pathway: leg 1 D' = 1 + 8 × 0.90 = 8.2 so £10 → £82; leg 2 D' = 1 + 4 × 0.80 = 4.2 so £82 → £344.40. Place pathway with 1/5 terms on both legs: leg 1 place Dp' = 1 + (8 × 0.2) × 0.90 = 2.44 so £10 → £24.40; leg 2 place Dp' = 1 + (4 × 0.2) × 0.80 = 1.64 so £24.40 → £40.02. Combined both-win outcome returns £384.42 instead of £496.80 without deductions, a 22.6% reduction driven by the compounded effect.

How is a dead heat for a place settled in an each-way double?

In a place dead heat where N runners tie for M remaining places, the effective settled stake becomes stake × (M ÷ N) at full place odds and the rest is lost. The reduced return from leg 1 then rolls into leg 2 as the new stake. Example: £10 each-way double (total £20), leg 1 at 20/1 with 1/5 terms (place Dp = 1 + 4 = 5.0) finishes in a three-way tie for two remaining places, so factor = 2/3 and leg 1 place return = £10 × (2/3) × 5.0 = £33.33. If leg 2 place Dp is 3.0, the place pathway returns £33.33 × 3.0 = £100.00. The win pathway is unaffected unless there is also a win dead heat.

What happens to an each-way double if one leg is a non-runner or void?

A void or non-runner reduces the multiple by one fold on the affected pathway, so a double becomes a single on the remaining leg at the original odds. If both legs are void, the full stake is returned. Example: £10 each-way double (total £20); leg 1 void, leg 2 wins at 4/1 with 1/5 terms. Win single returns £10 × 5.0 = £50.00, place single returns £10 × [1 + (4 × 0.2)] = £18.00, total £68.00 returned from £20 staked.

Why can both selections placing still return less than the total stake in an each-way double?

Short-priced legs with 1/5 place terms produce small place multipliers, so two place results can compound to less than your total outlay. Example: £10 each-way double (total £20), legs at 1/2 and 4/9 with 1/5 terms yield place decimals of 1.10 and 1.0889; the place pathway becomes 1.10 × 1.0889 = 1.1978, so £10 → £11.98, net loss £8.02 despite both placing.

How do I enter American odds like −150 and get correct place odds in an each-way double?

Convert American to decimal using D = 1 + 100 ÷ Y for negative prices and then apply the each-way fraction to the profit part only. For −150, D = 1 + 100/150 = 1.6667; with 1/5 terms the place decimal is Dp = 1 + (0.6667 × 0.2) = 1.1333. Two identical legs at −150 with 1/5 terms give a place-path multiplier of 1.1333 × 1.1333 = 1.2844, so a £10 place stake returns about £12.84.

How do rounding rules cause 1–2p differences versus a bookmaker’s settlement?

Most operators round to two decimal places at final settlement and may round intermediate steps differently, whereas calculators often keep 4–6 decimal places internally and round once at the end. If leg decimals include repeating fractions, the compounding of intermediate rounding can shift the outcome by £0.01–£0.02 on a typical £10 unit; for instance, compounding 1.9091 and 1.8889 rounded early versus late can diverge by a penny after two steps.

How do I model a free-bet each-way double where the stake is not returned?

Treat the first leg’s return as winnings only, i.e., use D − 1 for the first step on each pathway, then roll that amount into leg 2 at standard odds. Example: £10 each-way free bet on 8/1 and 4/1 with 1/5 terms. Win path: leg 1 returns £10 × 8 = £80, then leg 2 returns £80 × 5 = £400. Place path: leg 1 returns £10 × 1.6 = £16, then leg 2 returns £16 × 1.8 = £28.80. Total £428.80 versus £496.80 with cash stake returned.

Can I mix different each-way terms in one double, such as a horse at 1/4 odds and a golf outright at 1/5?

Yes, each leg uses its own each-way fraction and places paid, and the calculator applies them per leg before compounding. Example: leg 1 horse at 10/1 with 1/4 terms has place decimal Dp = 1 + (10 × 0.25) = 3.5, leg 2 golf outright at 16/1 with 1/5 terms has Dp = 1 + (16 × 0.2) = 4.2; the place pathway multiplier is 3.5 × 4.2 = 14.7 so a £10 place stake would return £147.00 if both place.

Do UK bettors pay tax on each-way double returns in 2026, and are there any caps or fees to consider?

UK bettors do not pay tax on betting winnings; returns are paid gross to the customer while operators pay duties to HMRC. For deductions, standard Tattersalls Rule 4 caps cumulative withdrawals at 90p in the £ on the affected market, and exchange bets may incur commission per the exchange’s schedule.

How are multiple Rule 4 withdrawals handled on the same leg?

Separate deductions are summed and applied to the winnings portion, subject to the standard cap of 90p in the £. For a leg priced at 5/1 (D=6.0) with withdrawals at 10p, 15p, and 30p, the total r is 0.55, so the adjusted decimal is D' = 1 + 5 × (1 − 0.55) = 1 + 2.25 = 3.25; a £10 win stake returns £32.50 instead of £60.00 on that leg before compounding.

How do extra places and field-size changes affect the place part of a double?

Only the terms in force at the off settle the bet, so losing a place due to a field-size drop eliminates the place return even if a selection finishes in the previously covered position. Example: a 16+ runner handicap paying 4 places at 1/4 reduced to 15 runners pays 3 places; a 4th-place finish then returns £0.00 on the place pathway rather than, for instance, £25.00 from a £10 stake at 10/1 with 1/4 terms (Dp 3.5) if the extra place had stood.

Can I combine related contingencies in an each-way double?

Bookmakers typically prohibit or restrict related contingencies because outcomes are not independent, and such bets may be voided or re-priced. A calculator will show theoretical returns, but you should check house rules; for example, a league winner doubled with the same team’s top-4 finish is commonly treated as related and refused or settled as a single.

When are doubles officially settled across sports like horse racing and golf, and what status should I use in the calculator?

Racing multiples are settled after ‘weighed in’ confirms the official result, while golf outrights settle on the final official leaderboard after any playoff or countback. Use the official off-time terms and the official finishing positions when entering results; for football outrights, settlement occurs on the governing body’s confirmation of final standings or awards.

How is a dead heat for the win on leg 1 compounded into leg 2 in an each-way double?

For a win dead heat among N runners, the win stake is divided by N and one part settles at full odds, with the remainder lost, and the returned amount rolls into leg 2. Example: £10 win stake on leg 1 at 8/1 in a two-way win dead heat gives £5 × 9.0 = £45 carried to leg 2; if leg 2 wins at 4/1, the win pathway returns £45 × 5.0 = £225. The place pathway for leg 1 may also be affected if the dead heat spans the place market; each part is treated independently.
