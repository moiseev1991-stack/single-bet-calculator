Each-way Super Heinz: how does cost and payout change?

Each-way (E/W) duplicates the entire Super Heinz into two parts: a win part and a place part, effectively doubling line count and cost. The win part settles at full win odds; the place part settles at the place fraction (commonly 1/5 or 1/4 of the win odds) for the positions paid (for example, 1–3 or 1–4), as defined by the market. In UK settlement, an Each-way multiple requires that the legs offering Each-way terms actually have place terms; otherwise, bookmakers typically won’t accept the E/W multiple on those legs.

  • Non-E/W cost: lines × unit stake. Example: 120 lines × £0.50 = £60.00.
  • E/W cost: 240 lines × unit stake (win + place). Example: 240 × £0.50 = £120.00.
  • Total-stake mode: the unit stake is computed by dividing your total stake by the number of lines (by 120 or by 240 if E/W).
  • Place odds in multiples: each placed leg’s place part uses the E/W fraction of its win odds; all place parts compound across the accumulator lines.
  • Example (single leg E/W terms): 10/1 with 1/5 place fraction pays place odds of 2/1 for that leg’s place portion within the multiple.
  • Confirm that all intended E/W legs publish place terms for the event.
  • Set the E/W fraction (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4) and places paid as per the card or market.

Choose stake mode:

What should I know about Bet365 and UK settlement quirks?

Bet365 follows standard UK settlement conventions for multiples with a few practical points you should be aware of. These affect how returns are calculated and why a calculator needs toggles for voids, Rule 4, dead heats, and market timing rules.

  • Rule 4 deductions: If a horse is withdrawn after final declarations, affected legs incur a Rule 4 deduction (expressed as p/£). The deduction is applied to the affected leg’s return before it compounds into your multiples.
  • Dead heats: For a dead heat, the win (or place) portion is split by the number of tied competitors (e.g., 2‑way tie halves the effective win outcome for that leg). The adjusted return then rolls into the accumulator.
  • Voids and pushes: A void leg reduces any line containing it by one selection (e.g., a four-fold becomes a treble). On some football lines (e.g., Asian 2.0), an exact outcome can void the leg rather than lose.
  • Timing rules: Standard football markets settle on 90 minutes plus stoppage (extra time/penalties excluded unless explicitly stated). Ensure your selections share the same timing scope.
  • Odds format and rounding: Returns are equivalent across fractional, decimal, and American odds. Final settlement is rounded to the nearest penny in GBP; calculators typically show pennies to match bookmaker payout.
  • Related contingencies: Standard accumulators cannot combine correlated outcomes from the same event unless placed using a builder product priced for correlation. The calculator assumes independent legs.
  • Each-way eligibility: E/W multiples are generally accepted only when each leg has published place terms; check the race card or market rules.
  • Cash Out vs settlement: Cash Out values are offers, not guaranteed settlement figures; they can differ from calculated returns and can change or be withdrawn.

For accurate planning: decide your stake mode (unit vs total), confirm market rules (E/W terms, Rule 4 applicability, dead-heat policy), and ensure legs are independent and allowed in a standard multiple by your bookmaker before placing a Super Heinz.

How do we handle Rule 4, dead heats and voids instantly?

We apply settlement adjustments at the leg level before compounding into each affected accumulator line. This mirrors standard UK practice: Rule 4 reduces the win profit of an affected leg, dead heats proportionally split the winning stake, and voids reduce line size (or act as a factor of 1.0 in accumulators). The calculator propagates these effects across all 120 (or 240 E/W) lines automatically.

  • Rule 4 deduction: Adjust the profit component only. Adjusted decimal = 1 + (original_decimal − 1) × (1 − deduction). Example: 4/1 (decimal 5.00) with a 20p Rule 4 becomes 1 + 4 × 0.8 = 4.20.

Which full-cover variants proved impractical in real betting?

“Impractical” usually means the line count forces unit stakes so small that meaningful returns are hard to achieve, or that legs aren’t eligible (e.g., Each‑Way on non‑E/W markets). The main culprits are the very large structures and their Each‑Way versions.

  • Mixed‑sport E/W multiples: standard football match markets rarely offer Each‑Way; enabling E/W across such coupons isn’t accepted by most bookmakers.
  • Related contingency traps: combining correlated selections from the same event (e.g., match result + player to score) is typically disallowed in standard multiples, so intended “full‑cover” cannot be placed as planned.
  • Check E/W eligibility per leg; E/W doubles total line count and cost.
  • Model realistic unit stakes: for 120 (or 240) lines, ensure your per‑line stake still produces worthwhile returns on the most common win scenarios.

For many bettors, the Super Heinz strikes a balance: broad coverage and manageable cost versus the Goliath’s sheer scale.

What does “official returns” mean for a Super Heinz in 2026?

Official returns are the final, bookmaker-settled payout after applying Rule 4 deductions, dead‑heat splits, voids/pushes and rounding to the nearest penny across all 120 lines (or 240 if each‑way). Cash Out offers are excluded; the total is the sum of all winning line settlements using the accepted odds and outcomes. Are Super Heinz winnings taxed in the UK in 2026?

For UK residents, personal tax on betting winnings is 0%; bookmakers, not punters, pay betting duties. Your Super Heinz returns are therefore paid in full as settled, with no income tax deducted from the payout.

Do UK bookmakers cap maximum payouts on a Super Heinz and how can I model it?

Yes, operators set maximum payout caps by sport and bet type; if your calculated return exceeds the cap, the official return is limited to that cap. To model, compare your calculated total to the operator’s limit, then apply a hard ceiling; for example, if the calculator shows £1,200,000 but the cap is £1,000,000, the effective haircut is £200,000 (16.7%).

How are dead heats settled in the place part of an each‑way Super Heinz?

In a place dead heat where more runners tie than places available, the place stake is reduced by places_available ÷ runners_tied, then settled at place odds. Example: each‑way 1/5 1–3, four runners tie for third, so a £1 place part becomes £0.75; at win odds 10/1 with 1/5 place terms (place odds 2/1, decimal 3.00), the place return is £0.75 × 3.00 = £2.25 on any line containing that leg.

How do I account for Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) in a Super Heinz?

If BOG applies and the Starting Price (SP) is bigger than the taken price, settle that leg at SP; every line containing it scales by SP ÷ taken_price. Example: taken 4/1 (decimal 5.00) but SP is 6/1 (decimal 7.00); a treble line containing that leg increases by 7.00 ÷ 5.00 = 1.40, so a £10.00 line return of £100.00 becomes £140.00. Terms vary by bookmaker and market, so only apply where eligible.

Can I model free bets (stake not returned) inside a Super Heinz?

Yes; for a free‑bet Super Heinz, each winning line pays stake × (product_decimal − 1) instead of stake × product_decimal. Example: a £1 line wins at combined decimal 6.00; cash stake returns £6.00, but a free‑bet (SNR) returns £5.00. Sum this across all winning lines; losing lines cost £0.00 because the stake was free.

How are Asian handicap pushes and quarter‑lines handled in a Super Heinz?

A push (e.g., AH 0 or Over 2.0 exactly) makes the leg a factor of 1.00 and reduces each affected accumulator by one selection. Quarter‑lines split into two half‑stakes on adjacent half/whole lines; to model Over 2.25, treat it as 0.5 × Over 2.0 and 0.5 × Over 2.5 at their respective prices, so exact 2 goals refunds half the stake on lines containing that leg while the other half settles on Over 2.5.

What happens to a Super Heinz if multiple selections are void?

The bet collapses to a full‑cover on the remaining active selections, with singles still excluded. One void leaves 57 lines, two voids leave 26, three leave 11, four leave 4, five leave 1 (the single surviving double), and six voids leave 0; each surviving line then settles on the remaining results.

Can I use different each‑way fractions or places for different legs in one Super Heinz?

Bookmakers settle each leg by its own published E/W terms, so place odds are leg‑specific. If your calculator does not allow per‑leg E/W fractions and places, run separate scenarios or use the strictest terms as a conservative proxy; for accuracy, a line containing legs at 1/5 and 1/4 must apply each leg’s own place odds inside the same product.

Why can my calculator total differ by a few pence from the bookmaker’s settlement?

Most UK operators round the final payout to the nearest penny, but some round per component or per line; rounding method differences can accumulate. The theoretical worst‑case drift from per‑line rounding is about half a penny per line, so up to roughly £0.60 on 120 lines or £1.20 on 240 each‑way lines.

How many winners do I typically need to break even on a Super Heinz?

Break‑even depends on prices; with even‑money legs (decimal 2.00) and £0.50 units, four winners return about £36.00 against £60.00 staked, while five winners return about £116.00, tipping into profit. At 2/1 legs (decimal 3.00), three winners return about £27.00, while four winners return about £121.50, so profit usually begins at four winners for these example prices.

What’s the difference between NRNB and standard ante‑post for multiples like a Super Heinz?

With Non‑Runner No Bet, a non‑runner is void (factor 1.00) and any line containing it reduces in size; with standard ante‑post, a non‑runner is settled as a loser (factor 0), which zeros every line containing that leg. For example, a treble with one NRNB non‑runner becomes a double, but the same treble ante‑post loses entirely even if the other two selections win.
