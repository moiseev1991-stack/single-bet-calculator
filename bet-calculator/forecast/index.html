How do I use the Forecast Bet Calculator?

Choose your bet type (Straight Forecast or Reverse Forecast), select your preferred odds format (fractional or decimal), enter the odds for your selections, and specify your stake as either a per‑line (unit) stake or a total stake. The calculator determines the number of lines automatically (1 for a Straight Forecast; 2 for a Reverse Forecast; for combinations it follows n×(n−1) lines, where n is the number of selections) and computes estimated returns. For UK horse racing, most Straight Forecasts with fixed‑odds bookmakers are settled to the official Computer Straight Forecast (CSF) dividend; returns shown here are estimates based on the odds and stake you enter, and actual settlement may vary by bookmaker rules.

  • Select bet type: Straight Forecast (finish 1st–2nd in exact order) or Reverse Forecast (covers both possible orders with two lines).
  • Pick odds format: fractional (e.g., 5/2) or decimal (e.g., 3.50). You can mix formats across runners if needed.
  • Enter odds for your runners. For combinations, add all runners you want covered; the calculator builds every ordered pair automatically.
  • Set stake: either enter a unit (per‑line) stake or a total stake. The calculator derives the other value based on the number of lines.
  • Review estimated returns. For Reverse Forecasts, remember only one of the two lines can win.

Race Forecast calculator: UK horse racing coverage

This calculator is designed for UK and Irish horse racing across Flat, Jumps (National Hunt), and All‑Weather meetings. It supports Straight Forecasts (SF) and Reverse Forecasts (RF) in races where forecasts are offered—typically events with at least three declared runners. For fixed‑odds bookmakers in Great Britain and Ireland, SF bets are commonly settled using the official Computer Straight Forecast (CSF) dividend published after the race; an RF is simply two SFs in opposite orders, so only the line matching the actual finishing order can win. Because settlement can vary by bookmaker (e.g., deductions for non‑runners or Rule 4, and dead‑heat handling), the calculator provides estimates based on your inputs.

  • Coverage: Great Britain and Ireland; Flat, Handicap, Maiden, Novice, Conditions, Stakes, and Jump races.
  • Eligibility: Forecasts generally require 3+ runners; in events with fewer runners, forecasts are usually not offered.
  • Non‑runners and adjustments: Affected lines are voided or adjusted per bookmaker rules. Use the calculator’s options to reflect non‑runners and typical deductions where applicable.
  • Result dependency: Final SF returns usually track the official CSF; RF outcomes reflect the CSF for the actual 1–2 order at your stake per line.

Straight vs Reverse Forecast: which should I choose?

Straight Forecast (SF) and Reverse Forecast (RF) are two‑runner bets for predicting the first two finishers in a horse race. An SF requires the exact 1–2 order; an RF covers both possible orders by placing two SF lines. In Great Britain and Ireland with fixed‑odds bookmakers, Straight Forecast bets are typically settled to the official Computer Straight Forecast (CSF) dividend published after the race; an RF simply places two SFs, so only the line matching the actual finishing order can return. Your choice hinges on conviction about the exact order versus the desire for cover, balanced against total stake and expected value.

  • Choose a Straight Forecast if you have a strong view on the exact 1–2 order and want to stake a single line.
  • Choose a Reverse Forecast if you expect two runners to dominate but are unsure of the order; note the total stake doubles because there are two lines.
  • Remember: only one line can win in an RF; the other line loses its stake.
  • Availability: forecasts are generally offered in races with at least three declared runners in the UK and Ireland.

What is a Straight Forecast bet?

A Straight Forecast is a bet picking two named horses to finish first and second in the exact order (e.g., “A to beat B”). It consists of one line, and the stake you set applies to that single line. With fixed‑odds bookmakers in Great Britain and Ireland, settlement is commonly to the official CSF dividend that is declared for the winning 1–2 combination; the return equals the CSF dividend to £1 multiplied by your unit stake. If the order is incorrect, the bet loses.

  • Lines: 1 line covering a single order (Horse A 1st, Horse B 2nd).
  • Settlement: usually to the official CSF dividend published after the race in GB/IE fixed‑odds markets.
  • Stake application: your entered stake is per line (one line only).
  • Non‑runner: generally voids the bet because the specified two‑horse combination can no longer occur (check your bookmaker’s rules).
  • Dead‑heats: forecasts are settled per the official procedure; returns reflect the published CSF for results affected by dead‑heats.
  • Not the Tote: on the Tote, the equivalent bet is an Exacta; dividends and settlement mechanics differ from fixed‑odds CSF.

What is a Reverse Forecast bet?

A Reverse Forecast is two Straight Forecasts on the same pair of horses, covering both possible finishing orders (A>B and B>A). It creates two lines; your per‑line stake is applied to each line, so the total stake is doubled. Only the line matching the actual finishing order can win. Settlement for each line is as per a Straight Forecast, typically using the official CSF dividend for the actual 1–2 order.

  • Lines: 2 lines (A>B and B>A).
  • Stake application: total stake = 2 × unit stake (one unit per line).
  • Outcome: only one line can win; the other line’s stake is lost.
  • Non‑runner: if one selection is a non‑runner, both lines are generally void (check bookmaker rules).
  • Dead‑heats: returns follow the published CSF for the finishing result that occurred.

Advanced forecast perms and combos explained

Permutations let you cover multiple forecast outcomes across one or more races. In the UK and Ireland, forecast bets with fixed‑odds bookmakers are typically settled to the official Computer Straight Forecast (CSF) dividend per race. When you combine forecasts across races (doubles, trebles, accumulators), returns are compounded line‑by‑line: the winning line in leg 1 rolls into leg 2 at the declared CSF, and so on. The calculator automates line building, stake allocation per line, and sequential CSF multiplication, so you can see total stakes, potential returns, and risk concentration before placing a bet.

Forecast Doubles and Accumulator calculator guide

A Forecast Double is a multiple across two races, each leg being a Straight Forecast (SF) or a Reverse Forecast (RF). An Accumulator extends this to three or more legs. Settlement is line‑by‑line: for a winning multiple, payout = stake per line × CSF(leg 1) × CSF(leg 2) × ... × CSF(leg n). The number of overall lines equals the product of lines in each leg (SF = 1 line; RF = 2 lines; combination legs have more). Only the exact finishing orders that match a given line in every leg will return.

  • Choose legs: each leg must be a race where forecasts are offered (typically 3+ runners).

How are dead-heats, non-runners, and Rule 4 handled?

In Great Britain and Ireland, fixed‑odds bookmakers usually settle Straight Forecasts to the official CSF dividend. That dividend already reflects the declared runners, starting prices, and any dead‑heat outcomes. As a result, most adjustments are handled at the CSF level rather than by applying manual deductions to forecast returns. The calculator mirrors this by treating CSF as authoritative for settlement estimates and by voiding or filtering lines when a selection cannot run.

  • Dead‑heats: If there is a dead‑heat for first or second, forecasts are settled according to the official CSF publication for that result. Where two outcomes are possible (e.g., dead‑heat for second behind a clear winner), the CSF typically publishes separate dividends for each valid 1–2 combination; only lines matching those combinations can return.
  • Non‑runners: Any line containing a non‑runner is normally void. If a race reduces to fewer than three runners, forecast betting for that race is generally void in full. The calculator removes affected lines from the stake and return calculations.
  • Rule 4 (deductions): Rule 4 deductions apply to early‑price fixed‑odds singles and multiples when a horse is withdrawn, but CSF‑settled forecasts are not subject to a separate Rule 4 deduction. Instead, the official CSF for the finished race reflects the final field and starting prices. If your bookmaker explicitly offers non‑CSF forecast prices (unusual), their published rules on deductions would apply.
  • Abandoned/void race: If a leg is void or abandoned, all forecast lines for that leg are void. In multiples, standard practice is that the multiple is settled on the remaining legs with the void leg treated per bookmaker rules (often as 1.00 for that leg); always check house rules.

Important: settlement policies can vary by bookmaker, but in GB/IE fixed‑odds markets the default is CSF settlement for forecasts. Always check the specific rule summary for the operator you use before placing complex perms or cross‑race multiples.

For pre‑race estimates, input your available odds and stake per line. To mirror post‑race settlement, use the official CSF dividend “to £1” for the correct order and multiply by your stake per winning line.

  • Identify your pair: choose the two runners you expect to finish 1st and 2nd.

Enter odds:

  • Fractional: type as “numerator/denominator” (e.g., 9/4). The calculator converts to decimal internally.
  • Decimal: type as a number including stake (e.g., 3.25).

Select bet type:

  • Straight Forecast (SF): one line for the exact order (A>B).

  • Look up the official CSF dividend for the actual 1–2 order.

  • Non‑runners: a line containing a non‑runner is normally void; if the race has fewer than three runners, forecast betting is typically void.
  • Dead‑heats: returns track the official published CSF for the recorded result.

What's the strongest argument against using calculators?

The strongest argument is that calculators can create an illusion of precision before the race. CSF dividends are only known after the result, and different bookmakers may have specific rules on voids and multiples. If you treat a pre‑race estimate as guaranteed, you could over‑stake or misinterpret risk. Calculators are decision aids, not settlement authorities.

  • Unknown dividends: CSF is declared after the race; pre‑race figures are illustrative only.
  • Operator rules: handling of void legs in multiples, abandoned races, and non‑runners can differ; always check your bookmaker’s rules.
  • Data entry risk: mixing total stake and per‑line stake or mis‑ordering selections can distort outcomes.
  • Over‑coverage: combination forecasts scale as n×(n−1); costs can exceed bankroll quickly.
  • Not pool betting: Tote Exacta/Trifecta use pool dividends, not CSF; a CSF‑based calculator is not suitable for pools.

Mitigations: confirm stake per line vs total stake, sanity‑check line counts, and verify settlement rules with your operator. Where available, cross‑check results and responsible gambling tools provided by your bookmaker and the regulator.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you place an each-way reverse forecast?

No. Forecasts (straight or reverse) are win-only bets on the exact 1–2 order and do not have an each-way variant. Each-way terms apply to single-runner bets with separate win/place parts, which does not exist for a two-runner forecast market.

What happens to a forecast bet if the field cuts to two runners after withdrawals?

It is usually void because forecasts require at least three runners to be a valid market; the stake is returned. In multiples the void leg is typically settled as 1.00, so a £1 SF×SF double where leg 1 returns a CSF of £12.50 and leg 2 becomes void would pay £12.50 (1 × 12.50 × 1.00).

How do I cap spend on a combination forecast to a fixed budget?

Divide your budget by the line count; per-line stake = total budget ÷ lines. Example: 5 selections create 20 lines; a £10 budget gives £0.50 per line (10 ÷ 20). With 7 selections (42 lines), a £21 budget keeps £0.50 per line (21 ÷ 42).

How do I compare the expected value (EV) of a Straight Forecast versus a Reverse Forecast?

Use EV = sum(probability × payout) − total stake. Example: stake per line £1, SF on A over B with CSF D1 = 18.40 and probability p = 0.25 gives EV_SF = 0.25×18.40 − 1 = £3.60. An RF has two £1 lines; if P(A over B) = 0.25 and P(B over A) = 0.20 with D1 = 18.40 and D2 = 10.50, EV_RF = (0.25×18.40 + 0.20×10.50) − 2 = (4.60 + 2.10) − 2 = £4.70.

Do Rule 4 deductions apply to CSF-settled forecasts?

No. CSF-settled forecasts are not subject to a separate Rule 4 deduction; the official CSF already reflects the final field and SPs. For instance, a 30p/£ Rule 4 that would hit early-price singles has a 0p effect on a CSF-settled forecast; settlement follows the published CSF only.

How are dead-heats for second settled on forecasts, in practice?

The official CSF typically publishes separate dividends for each valid 1–2 pairing created by the dead-heat, and only matching lines are paid. Example: result A wins, B and C dead-heat for second; CSF prints A–B = £7.80 and A–C = £6.90 to £1. A £2 reverse forecast on A/B returns £15.60 on the A–B line (2×7.80) and loses £2 on the B–A line, net £13.60.

How are non-runners handled in a one-race combination forecast?

Lines containing a non-runner are void and removed from settlement. Example: 5 selections create 20 lines; if one is a non-runner, lines drop to 12 (for 4 selections). At £0.50 per line your total outlay falls from £10.00 to £6.00; if you keep total stake fixed at £10.00, the derived per-line stake becomes £10.00 ÷ 12 = £0.83.

Can I use the calculator for greyhound racing forecasts?

Yes, the mathematics is identical: a straight forecast is 1 line, a reverse forecast is 2 lines, and combinations follow n×(n−1). Settlement depends on the greyhound operator’s published forecast dividend rather than horse-racing CSF, so always check the applicable rules for that code.

Is the Computer Straight Forecast (CSF) the same as the Tote Exacta dividend?

No. CSF is a fixed-odds dividend quoted to £1 based on the official algorithm and SPs, while a Tote Exacta is a pool dividend determined by the net pool and winning tickets. On the same race the figures can differ markedly, for example a CSF of £18.40 versus a Tote Exacta of £23.90; you cannot infer one from the other.

How should I handle rounding when splitting a total stake across many forecast lines?

Work to the nearest penny per line and expect a small residual when the split is not exact. For example, £10.00 across 12 lines yields £0.83 per line (£9.96 total) with £0.04 unallocated; either accept the £9.96 total, manually add £0.04 to one line, or adjust the per-line stake to £0.84 and reduce the number of lines you place.

Are forecast bet winnings taxable for bettors in the UK and Ireland?

No. For individuals in the UK and Ireland, gambling winnings are not subject to income tax; the tax burden is on operators, not punters. This is a 0% tax rate on personal betting returns under current regimes, but keep records if you need to evidence source of funds.

How do I target a specific payout using an expected CSF dividend?

Stake per winning line = target payout ÷ expected CSF. Example: to aim for about £50 from a line when you expect a CSF near 12.50, stake ≈ £50 ÷ 12.50 = £4.00; remember the actual CSF may differ, so the realised payout can be higher or lower.

What happens to a forecast multiple if one leg is void or abandoned?

The void leg is usually settled as 1.00 and the multiple continues on remaining legs. Example: a £1 SF×SF double with leg 1 CSF = 9.00 and leg 2 void pays £9.00 (1 × 9.00 × 1.00); in an RF×RF double with four lines, the same 1.00 factor applies to each surviving line.
