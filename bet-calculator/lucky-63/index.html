What is a Lucky 63 bet and how are returns worked out?

A Lucky 63 is a full-cover bet on six selections comprising 63 separate lines: all singles, all doubles, all trebles, all four-folds, all five-folds, and the six-fold accumulator. Returns are calculated line-by-line and then summed. For each winning multiple, the unit stake on that line is multiplied by the product of the odds (converted to decimal) of the selections in that line. Voids/non-runners reduce the fold size within affected lines (treated as odds of 1.00), and only surviving lines with all legs settled as “win” (or “place” for the each-way place part) return.

Each-way Lucky 63: how does place and win return work?

An each-way (E/W) Lucky 63 doubles the number of lines to 126 by placing equal stakes on two parts of every line: the win part (at full odds) and the place part (at the stated place terms, typically a fraction such as 1/5 or 1/4 of the win odds). Settlement is independent for each part: the win part requires a win; the place part pays if the selection finishes in the designated places.

  • Stake split: Your entered per-line stake is divided equally between win and place parts (e.g., £1 E/W per line = £0.50 win + £0.50 place).

Place odds conversion:

  • Decimal: place_decimal = 1 + (win_decimal − 1) × place_fraction.
  • Fractional: place_fractional = (numerator/denominator × place_fraction) expressed as a new fraction, plus the returned stake in settlement.

Multiples settlement:

  • Win part of a multiple pays only if all legs win.
  • Place part of a multiple pays only if all legs place (not necessarily win).
  • Mixed outcomes (some win, some place) contribute via the place part only; the win part loses for any non-winning leg.
  • Select E/W and set the place fraction and place count per market (per standard terms).
  • The calculator prices 63 win lines and 63 place lines separately, then sums both to a total return and profit.
  • Voids/non-runners apply as 1.00 multipliers in both win and place parts, reducing fold size accordingly.

Historical path: from Yankee and Heinz to the Lucky 63 full cover

Full-cover combination bets evolved to give bettors exposure across many result combinations with a single entry. The core idea is to combine all permutations of selections so that even partial success can return something. Milestones include the Yankee (4 selections), the Canadian/Super Yankee (5 selections), the Heinz (6 selections without singles), and finally the Lucky 63 (6 selections with singles added).

  • Yankee (4 selections, 11 bets): 6 doubles, 4 trebles, 1 four-fold. No singles.
  • Canadian/Super Yankee (5 selections, 26 bets): 10 doubles, 10 trebles, 5 four-folds, 1 five-fold. No singles.
  • Heinz (6 selections, 57 bets): 15 doubles, 20 trebles, 15 four-folds, 6 five-folds, 1 six-fold. No singles. The name references the “57 varieties.”
  • Lucky 63 (6 selections, 63 bets): All Heinz components plus 6 singles, enhancing cover when only one pick wins.

The addition of singles in the Lucky 63 materially changes risk/return: it increases total stake versus a Heinz at the same per-line stake but improves the chance of any return, especially when one or two selections oblige. This trade-off is why many UK bettors prefer the Lucky 63 for mixed-confidence slips or fields with variable prices.

Advanced settings for UK bettors: each-way, Rule 4, dead heats

Advanced settlement factors can materially change the outcome of a Lucky 63. Rule 4 deductions reduce the winnings portion of affected legs when a rival is withdrawn after final declarations. Dead heats split entitlement between tied runners, altering the effective multiplier applied to a leg. These adjustments are applied per leg across all impacted lines (singles through to the six-fold) and in both the win and, where applicable, the place parts of each-way bets.

How do Rule 4 deductions affect Lucky 63 returns?

Rule 4 (Tattersalls) deductions are applied when a selection runs but a rival is withdrawn after final declarations and the market is not fully reformed. The deduction is expressed as pence-in-the-pound (e.g., 10p/£1 = 10%) and is applied to the winnings portion only (the profit), not to the returned stake. In a Lucky 63, any line containing a leg subject to Rule 4 uses an adjusted price for that leg.

  • Adjusted decimal odds formula (win part): adjusted_decimal = 1 + (original_decimal − 1) × (1 − deduction_rate).
  • Place part uses the same logic but with the calculated place decimal (see Each-way section below).
  • If a selection itself is withdrawn, that leg is void (treated as 1.00) and no Rule 4 is applied to that selection.
  • If there are multiple qualifying withdrawals, bookmakers add multiple deductions according to their schedules.

Dead heat calculator: how are places and ties settled?

Dead heats split entitlement among tied runners. For win markets, the usual approach is to divide the stake by the number of tied runners and settle at full odds (the “other part” of the stake loses). In decimal terms, this is equivalent to multiplying the leg’s decimal odds by 1 ÷ n where n is the number of tied runners. For place markets, when more runners tie than remaining places, only a fraction of the stake is entitled: factor = remaining_places ÷ number_tied_at_last_place. That entitled fraction is settled at the place odds; the remainder loses.

  • Win dead heat (n-way): effective multiplier = original_decimal ÷ n.

Place dead heat at final paying position:

  • Entitled-stake factor f = remaining_places ÷ tied_runners_at_last_place.
  • Effective place return on stake S: S × f × place_decimal.
  • In multiples, the dead-heat factor applies only to that leg within the line; other legs multiply as normal.

Bookmaker settings: Betfred Lucky 63 terms to watch

Bookmaker house rules determine exactly how settlements and promotions apply. For Betfred and other UK firms, Lucky 63s are full-cover bets (63 lines; singles included). The following checks help ensure your calculator settings reflect typical UK practice; always verify current terms on the bookmaker’s site or betslip.

Each-way availability:

  • Common for horse racing and golf; uncommon for football markets. If placing a football Lucky 63, you will usually price win-only lines.

Place terms:

  • Industry-standard horse racing place terms often apply (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4) depending on race type and field size; enhanced places may be offered on selected races and affect the place odds used in the calculator.

Rule 4 and non-runners:

  • Rule 4 deductions apply to the winnings portion of affected legs for bets struck before the withdrawal; if your selection is the non-runner, that leg is void in all lines.

Dead-heat policy:

  • Win dead heats divide stake equally between tied runners; place dead heats apportion the entitled stake by remaining_places ÷ tied_runners_at_last_place.

Bonuses and concessions:

  • Some bookmakers advertise Lucky 15/31/63 concessions (e.g., “one winner” or “all winners” bonuses). Availability, qualifiers (sports/markets), and percentages vary and may be excluded from multiples; confirm on the day.

Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG):

  • Where offered on UK/Irish racing, BOG policies frequently exclude or limit multiples. Check whether BOG applies to Lucky 63 lines before relying on an SP uplift.

Minimum stakes and rounding:

  • Per-line minimums and rounding to the nearest penny can affect total outlay and exact returns; using “stake per line” in the calculator helps mirror bookmaker rounding.

Lucky 63 vs Heinz: which is better for football?

Both Lucky 63 and Heinz are six-selection full-cover multiples popular with UK bettors. The key practical difference is that a Lucky 63 includes singles (6 extra lines), while a Heinz excludes them. For football, where each-way is rarely offered, these bets are almost always win-only. Choosing between them is mainly about coverage and volatility: Lucky 63 costs more at the same per-line stake but can return something with just one winner; Heinz is cheaper per-line and concentrates stake in combinations that require at least two winners.

When is a Heinz better value than a Lucky 63?

Heinz can be a better fit when you do not want singles exposure or when your perceived edge only materialises if several selections land. Because Heinz excludes singles, it avoids committing stake to outcomes that return on just one winner.

  • Low-confidence or short-priced singles: If many selections are short odds with little margin, the singles can dilute expected value; excluding them (Heinz) concentrates stake on higher-paying combinations.
  • Promotions focus on accumulators: If a bookmaker’s bonuses, price boosts, or insurance are targeted at accas or trebles+, Heinz may align better with those terms.
  • Risk appetite: If you accept higher variance in pursuit of larger combined payouts, a Heinz avoids the “drag” of singles.
  • Equal total budget scenario: For a fixed bankroll, a Heinz allows a bigger per-line stake on the 57 lines than a Lucky 63 would on 63 lines, potentially improving returns when multiple legs win.
  • Define goals: recovery potential (favour Lucky 63) versus concentration on combos (favour Heinz).
  • Compare expected value per structure using your own probabilities; remember that excluding singles increases variance.
  • Check house rules and promos: if singles concessions do not apply but acca boosts do, Heinz may be preferable.

How do football markets (BTTS, handicaps) fit a Lucky 63?

Football Lucky 63s are almost always win-only. You can combine standard football markets across different matches, but related contingencies from the same match (e.g., Home Win and Home -1 Handicap in the same game) are usually not permitted in traditional multiples unless placed as a same-game builder, which is treated as a single composite selection.

  • BTTS (Both Teams To Score): Settles as win or lose; no push state. Suitable for inclusion as standard legs.
  • Match odds (1X2), Double Chance, Over/Under: Settle as win/lose; abandoned/postponed events may void per house rules.
  • Draw No Bet (DNB): A draw results in a push/void; in a Lucky 63 line that leg is treated as 1.00 (reduces fold size).
  • Asian Handicap 0 (Level ball): Draw = push/void (multiplier 1.00) in all affected lines.
  • Asian quarter lines (e.g., -0.25, +0.25, -0.75, +0.75): Stake is split across adjacent half-lines (e.g., -0.25 = half on 0 and half on -0.5). Settlement can be half-win/half-void or half-lose/half-void; in multiples this behaves like two parallel legs each carrying half the stake.
  • European Handicap (3-way): No push; outcomes are Home/Draw/Away relative to the handicap line. Treated as a standard win/lose market.
  • Same-match combinations: Typically restricted in traditional accas; use a builder product if you need same-event combinations, then include the builder outcome as one leg within the Lucky 63.
  • Use distinct matches to avoid related-contingency rejections.
  • Set push-capable markets (DNB, AH 0) to “Void” when they draw; the line then prices the remaining legs.
  • For quarter-line Asians, model as split stakes across two selections if your calculator does not natively support half-win/half-lose settlement.

Are Lucky 63 bets worth it? Devil’s advocate and smarter staking

A Lucky 63 spreads your stake across 63 lines from six selections. It offers broad coverage (including singles), which can cushion poor runs, but it also commits more stake than a Heinz at the same per-line amount. Whether it is “worth it” depends on your edge (if any), target variance, and how often you expect multiple legs to land. The maths below shows the thresholds and trade-offs clearly.

What’s the strongest argument against a Lucky 63?

The main case against a Lucky 63 is that it commits stake to many lines that still carry the bookmaker’s margin, diluting value if you do not have a genuine edge on each selection.

  • Margin applied 63 times: Without an edge, the expected return is below 100% across all lines; spreading stake widely does not overcome the house margin.
  • Singles can dilute upside: If your strategy relies on multiple legs winning, adding singles uses bankroll on low-paying lines that do not leverage your edge as strongly as higher folds.
  • Promotions often exclude perms: Acca boosts/insurance typically target straight accumulators and may not apply to full-cover bets or to the singles within a Lucky 63.
  • Higher total outlay: At equal per-line stakes, a Lucky 63 costs more than a Heinz (63 vs 57 lines), increasing exposure and potential absolute loss during downswings.
  • Variance management: While singles reduce variance, they can also reduce long-run ROI if they are placed at negative expected value prices.

When is a Lucky 63 worth it? Expected value and hit-rate math

Assume six independent selections with the same average decimal odds D and true win probability p. Define x = p × D (your per-leg expected return multiplier). For a win-only Lucky 63 with £1 per line, the expected return per £1 staked across the bet is E(Lucky63) = [ (1 + x)6 − 1 ] ÷ 63. Heinz (which removes singles) has E(Heinz) = [ (1 + x)6 − 1 − 6x ] ÷ 57. Break-even for both structures occurs at x = 1 (fair odds). Below are worked examples:

  • Rule of thumb: If your average true probability p is higher than 1/D for most legs but you’re unsure several will land together, choose Lucky 63; if you expect clustered wins, Heinz or targeted accas may be more efficient.
  • Voids/pushes (e.g., Draw No Bet or Asian Handicap 0 draws) are treated as 1.00 multipliers, reducing fold size and slightly improving survival of lines.

What safer alternatives beat a Lucky 63 for value?

“Safer” generally means lower variance and better capital efficiency when edge is modest or uncertain. These structures and approaches often outperform a Lucky 63 for bankroll preservation and, in some cases, expected value.

Targeted singles and doubles only:

  • Back only selections with clear positive EV; pair a few into doubles if both are edge-positive. This trims low-value lines and reduces exposure.

Trixie/Patent instead of full-cover sixfolds:

  • Trixie (3 selections: 3 doubles, 1 treble) or Patent (adds 3 singles) offer coverage with fewer lines and lower outlay.

Yankee/Canadian for mid-size perms:

  • Yankee (4 selections, 11 lines) or Canadian/Super Yankee (5 selections, 26 lines) reduce stake count versus a Lucky 63 while preserving multiple routes to a return.

Heinz with weighted staking:

  • If your thesis requires several winners, use a Heinz and reallocate the budget saved from not staking singles to the higher folds.

Use push-friendly markets to manage downside:

  • Draw No Bet or Asian Handicap 0 can void rather than lose on draws, softening variance across multiples.

Leverage genuine, transparent promotions:

  • Where permitted, acca boosts/insurance on trebles+ can lift EV more reliably than staking into 63 unboosted lines. Always confirm current terms and exclusions.

Bottom line: Lucky 63s are best when you want broad cover and expect a mix of outcomes. If your edge is concentrated in fewer, stronger opinions, trimming lines (or switching structure) typically improves long-run value and variance control.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I combine multiple Rule 4 deductions on one selection in a Lucky 63?

Multiply the winnings portion by each remaining percentage, not by adding deductions. Use adjusted_decimal = 1 + (original_decimal − 1) × (1 − d1) × (1 − d2) × …; for example, 3.50 with 15p and 5p deductions becomes 1 + 2.50 × 0.85 × 0.95 = 3.01875 (rounded to 3.02). This compounded rate then applies to every line in the Lucky 63 that contains that leg.

Can I price a Lucky 63 using a free bet stake (stake not returned)?

Yes—treat each winning line as returning profit only, with the stake part removed. For a single line at decimal 3.00 using a free bet, £1 returns £2.00, not £3.00; across a Lucky 63 struck entirely with a free bet token, every winning line’s return is line_stake × (decimal − 1), so a six-fold at 3.00, 2.20, 4.00, 2.50, 3.50, 2.10 would return stake × ((3−1)×(2.2−1)×(4−1)×(2.5−1)×(3.5−1)×(2.1−1)) instead of stake × (3×2.2×4×2.5×3.5×2.1).

How are fractional pennies rounded on Lucky 63 settlements and does it change totals?

UK fixed‑odds bookmakers typically round each settled bet to the nearest penny, often by truncation down, at the end of the line. A return of £0.2375 is usually paid as £0.23, not £0.24; across 63 lines this can trim a few pence in aggregate, which is why using stake per line values that produce whole‑penny returns helps match bookmaker payouts.

Does Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) apply to Lucky 63 multiples?

Usually no; most major UK firms exclude multiples from BOG or limit BOG to singles only. If BOG is exceptionally applied to a multiple, it affects the settled odds of the qualifying leg(s) only, so a 10% SP uplift on one leg lifts each live line containing that leg by 10% on the winnings portion, not on the returned stake.

How do I enter SP or in‑play odds in the calculator?

Enter the decimal, fractional, or American price you actually took; SP or in‑play labels do not change the math. If SP is unknown pre‑off, leave it blank until confirmed or use an estimate, noting that a 5% change in one leg’s decimal increases the winnings portion of every live line containing it by exactly 5%.

What happens to US spreads/totals that push in a Lucky 63?

A push is treated as a void leg with a 1.00 multiplier, reducing the fold size. For example, a -110 spread that pushes in a treble makes that line settle as a double, so a £1 treble at 1.91, 1.91, and push becomes £1 × 1.91 × 1.91 = £3.6481 (rounded by house rules) for that line.

How should I treat extra time or penalties in football when using the calculator?

Price according to the market rules you bet: 1X2 settles on 90 minutes plus injury time only, while “To qualify” includes extra time and penalties. If the team won after extra time but drew in 90 minutes, a 1X2 leg is a loser; only a “To qualify” leg is a winner, so mark outcomes accordingly before computing the Lucky 63.

How are dead heats handled with enhanced places in each‑way Lucky 63s?

When more runners tie than remaining places, only a fraction of stake is entitled: factor f = remaining_places ÷ tied_runners_at_last_place, then settle that fraction at place odds. With 4 places paid, a three‑way tie for 4th gives f = 1⁄3; for a 10/1 shot at 1/5 terms, place decimal is 1 + (11−1)×0.2 = 3.0, so £1 place part returns £1 × 1⁄3 × 3.0 = £1.00.

What if my total stake doesn’t divide evenly by 63 (or 126 each‑way)?

The exact per‑line stake is total_stake ÷ number_of_lines and can be a repeating decimal, so £20 each‑way across 126 lines is £0.158730… per line for both win and place parts. Accurate calculators carry full precision during multiplication and round only at the end of each line, whereas bookmakers using stake‑per‑line entry avoid these micro‑fractions altogether.

Is a Lucky 63 or a straight 6‑fold accumulator better value on average?

At equal total spend, the 6‑fold has higher expected return when your per‑leg edge is strong, but much higher variance. Using x = p×D, a £63 6‑fold has expected return 63×x⁶, while a £63 Lucky 63 has expected return (1+x)⁶ − 1; at x = 1.05 this is roughly £84.4 for the acca versus about £73 for the Lucky 63, but the Lucky 63 preserves more capital when few legs win.

Do bookmaker maximum payout caps affect Lucky 63 results?

Yes—caps can clip high‑odds perms materially. If a bookmaker caps football multiples at £250,000 and your theoretical Lucky 63 return is £400,000, the effective haircut is 37.5%, so the paid total is £250,000 regardless of the computed figure; this cap applies to the entire bet, not per line, per typical house rules.

Are returns from Lucky 63 bets taxable in the UK and does the tool include any fees?

UK punters do not pay tax on gambling winnings, so returns are gross of tax. The calculator does not include exchange commission or fees; to model exchange commission c on decimal D, use D' = 1 + (D − 1) × (1 − c), for example 2% commission turns 3.50 into 1 + 2.50 × 0.98 = 3.45.

Can I set banker selections in a Lucky 63 calculation?

Bankers are not part of a standard Lucky 63, which prices every combination equally. To simulate bankers, compute the full Lucky 63 then exclude any lines that omit your banker, or use a custom perm where only combinations containing the banker are staked; for six picks with one banker, you would be removing all lines that lack that selection.

What if an event is postponed—how should I reflect that in the Lucky 63?

If the bookmaker voids the leg after a postponement window, mark it Void so its multiplier is 1.00 and the fold reduces. Many UK firms void if the event is not completed within 24–48 hours or if it moves to a different venue, but thresholds vary, so applying Void in the calculator mirrors the typical settlement outcome.
