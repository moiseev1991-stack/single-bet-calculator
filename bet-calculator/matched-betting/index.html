Matched Betting Calculator

Use this UK‑ready matched betting calculator to optimise qualifying and free bets with exchange commission factored in. Supports 3‑way and Each Way modes, with clear steps for Smarkets and Betfair users.

Matched Betting Calculator

How do I use the Matched Betting Calculator (UK)?

This calculator balances a bookmaker back bet with an exchange lay bet to lock in a small qualifying loss or extract value from a free bet. In the UK, exchanges charge commission only on net winnings for a market. Typical base rates are 2% at Smarkets and 5% at Betfair Exchange (rates can vary by account and promotion; always confirm on the exchange’s help pages at [smarkets.com/help](https://smarkets.com/help) and [support.betfair.com](https://support.betfair.com)). Enter decimal odds, your stake, the lay odds, and the exchange commission; choose the bet type (Qualifier or SNR free bet), and the calculator returns the lay stake and liability.

What inputs do I enter: back odds, stake, lay odds, commission?

The core inputs reflect the bookmaker price, your stake, the exchange lay price, and the exchange’s commission. The calculator also needs the bet type because the formula differs for a qualifying bet versus a “stake not returned” (SNR) free bet.

  • Qualifier: Lay stake = (Back odds × Back stake) ÷ (Lay odds − c)
  • SNR free bet: Lay stake = [(Back odds − 1) × Free bet size] ÷ (Lay odds − c)

Underlay vs overlay: when should you nudge the lay stake?

“Balanced” aims to make outcomes as equal as possible. Nudging the lay stake changes which outcome is more profitable:

  • Underlay (reduce lay stake): Higher profit if the back bet wins; lower profit (or higher loss) if it loses. Also reduces exchange liability.
  • Overlay (increase lay stake): Higher profit if the back bet loses (exchange win); lower profit if it wins. Increases exchange liability.

Step‑by‑step with Smarkets or Betfair Exchange (UK)

Follow this process to minimise slippage and mismatches between the bookmaker and the exchange.

  • Find a tight match: Identify a market where the bookmaker back odds and exchange lay odds are close with adequate liquidity at your target lay stake.
  • Confirm commission: Check your current exchange commission (e.g., 2% Smarkets, 5% Betfair) via [smarkets.com/help](https://smarkets.com/help) or [support.betfair.com](https://support.betfair.com), and enter it in the calculator.
  • Enter inputs: Type back odds and back stake (or free bet size), the exchange lay odds, and select Qualifier or SNR in the calculator.
  • Review outputs: Note the lay stake and liability. If needed, choose Underlay or Overlay and recheck the projected outcomes.
  • Place the exchange order first if liquidity is thin: Submit the lay at the quoted lay price. If using a limit order, ensure it is fully matched before placing the bookmaker bet. If liquidity is strong, many place the back bet first and lay immediately after.
  • Enter the exact lay stake: On Smarkets and Betfair, the “Stake” box for a lay bet is your lay stake (not liability). Check that the platform shows the expected liability before confirming.
  • Place the bookmaker back bet: Use the same selection and stake used in the calculator. Verify the accepted odds match your input; if the odds move, recalculate before placing.
  • Record and reconcile: Save the event, odds, stakes, commission, and expected profit. After settlement, compare actual P&L with the calculator’s projection.
  • Smarkets specifics: Commission is typically 2% on net market winnings; the bet slip displays “Liability” and “Potential profit” clearly. Partial matches are possible; unmatched portions remain as open lay offers until canceled or matched.
  • Betfair specifics: Standard UK base commission is often 5% on net market winnings (account-dependent). Betfair also supports partial fills; unmatched orders persist at your limit price unless you cancel or edit them.
  • Good practice: If a lay is partially matched at multiple prices, update the calculator with the weighted average lay odds and total matched lay stake to verify expected P&L.

Which mode do you need: 3‑Way, Each Way, Qualifier, or SNR?

Different offer mechanics and settlement rules require different calculator modes. Use 3‑Way when a push/void is possible, Each Way for horse racing bets split into win and place legs, and choose Qualifier vs SNR to reflect whether your stake is returned (cash bet) or not returned (free bet). Correct mode selection ensures lay stakes, liability, and projected P&L are computed under the right assumptions for UK exchanges and bookmakers.

When should I use the 3 way calculator for win/lose/void outcomes?

A 3‑Way calculator produces three projected results: back‑win, back‑lose (i.e., lay‑win), and void/push. Use it whenever the rules reasonably allow an outcome where your original bookmaker bet is canceled and refunded, and the exchange position may also void or be canceled according to market rules.

  • Draw No Bet (football): Home/away wins settle normally; a draw voids the back bet (push). Exchanges typically offer a “Draw No Bet” market that also voids on a draw.
  • Asian Handicap 0 (AH 0): Team wins = back‑win, team loses = back‑lose; a draw = push/void on both sides.
  • Tennis retirements (rule differences): Some bookmakers void if a set is not completed; exchanges (e.g., Betfair) void if the match is not completed under market rules. A 3‑Way setup helps you plan for a potential void branch. Always check rules in advance.
  • Non‑runner or event cancellation: Horse non‑runners or abandoned events commonly void both bookmaker and exchange bets on that selection/market.
  • Confirm settlement rules for both the bookmaker and the exchange on the specific market.
  • Enter back odds, lay odds, stake, and commission as usual.
  • Enable “3‑Way” mode and verify the void branch is aligned to the correct scenario (both sides void). If rules can lead to “book‑only void” or “exchange‑only void,” use a calculator that supports those toggles and review P&L for each path.

Each Way calculator: how are place terms and EW fractions handled?

An Each Way bet is two bets: win and place, each at half the total stake for a 1x EW. The place odds are derived from the win odds using the bookmaker’s EW fraction and stated place terms (e.g., 1/5 odds, 3 places). In matched betting, you typically lay the win selection in the exchange “Win” market and lay the same runner in the exchange “Place” market separately, each with its own lay odds and potentially different liquidity.

  • EW stake split: Total stake £S becomes £S/2 on Win and £S/2 on Place.

Place odds conversion (decimal): Place odds = 1 + (Win odds − 1) × EW fraction.

  • Example: Win odds 10.00 with 1/5 terms → Place odds = 1 + 9 × 0.2 = 2.80.
  • Lay separately: Compute a lay stake for the Win leg using win lay odds and commission, and a lay stake for the Place leg using place lay odds and commission.

Settlement pathways:

  • Runner wins: Win leg settles as a win; Place leg also settles as a place win.
  • Runner places (but not first): Win leg loses; Place leg wins.
  • Runner unplaced: Both legs lose.
  • Edge cases: Dead‑heats in win or place markets apply dead‑heat rules on returns and exchange P&L; recalculate using the official settlement reduction factors.

Qualifying bets return the stake from the bookmaker; SNR (“stake not returned”) free bets do not return the nominal stake—only the winnings. Because of that, the balanced lay stake has a different numerator. Commission (c) is the exchange commission expressed as a decimal (e.g., 0.02 for 2%).

Can I build my own Excel matched betting calculator?

Yes. A simple workbook can reproduce industry‑standard calculations for Qualifier, SNR, 3‑Way, and Each Way. Below is a minimal structure using decimal odds and commission applied to exchange winnings.

Inputs (single‑selection market):

  • b = Back odds (decimal), l = Lay odds (decimal), S = Stake (£), c = Exchange commission as a decimal (e.g., 0.02 for 2%).

Excel setup (example cell mapping):

  • B2 = b, B3 = l, B4 = S, B5 = commission percent (e.g., 2). In any formula, use c = B5/100.

Qualifier (cash stake returned):

  • Lay stake L: = $B$4 * $B$2 / ($B$3 - ($B$5/100))
  • Liability: = ($B$3 - 1) * L
  • Profit if back wins: = $B$4 * ($B$2 - 1) - ($B$3 - 1) * L
  • Profit if back loses: = -$B$4 + L * (1 - $B$5/100)

SNR (stake not returned) free bet:

  • Lay stake Lsnr: = $B$4 * ($B$2 - 1) / ($B$3 - ($B$5/100))
  • Profit if back wins: = $B$4 * ($B$2 - 1) - ($B$3 - 1) * Lsnr
  • Profit if back loses: = Lsnr * (1 - $B$5/100)
  • 3‑Way (Win/Lose/Void): Use the Qualifier or SNR formulas for L and add a “Void” branch. If both sides void, profit = 0. If rules differ (e.g., book voids but exchange stands), add a branch: P&L = − exchange liability or + exchange net depending on which side stands. Always confirm market rules.

Each Way (horse racing):

  • Split stake: Win stake = S/2; Place stake = S/2.
  • Place odds (decimal): p = 1 + (b − 1) × fraction, where fraction is 0.20 for 1/5, 0.25 for 1/4, etc.
  • Compute separate lays using exchange Win lay odds lw and Place lay odds lp (and same commission c):
  • Win lay Lw: = (S/2) * b / (lw − c) | Place lay Lp: = (S/2) * p / (lp − c)
  • Example (SNR): b = 5.00, l = 5.10, S = 20, c = 0.02 → L ≈ 20×(5−1)/(5.10−0.02) = 80/5.08 ≈ 15.75; Back‑win P ≈ 20×4 − 4.10×15.75 = 15.43; Back‑lose P ≈ 15.75×0.98 = 15.44 (minor rounding differences are normal).
  • Resources: Excel desktop (licence required; see [support.microsoft.com/excel](https://support.microsoft.com/excel)) and Google Sheets (free with Google account; offline via [support.google.com/docs/answer/6388102](https://support.google.com/docs/answer/6388102)).

Risks, limits, and the other side of the coin: is using a matched betting calculator always safe?

A calculator can get the maths right and still produce the wrong outcome in practice if execution, rules, or account status change. The strongest risk is operational: odds and liquidity move, settlement rules differ by market, and bookmakers can restrict (“gub”) accounts. Treat calculators as planning tools, not guarantees. Verify rules, allow buffers for slippage, and follow safer gambling and bankroll controls.

What is the strongest argument against matched betting calculators?

The most compelling counter‑argument is that calculators cannot protect you from execution and settlement risk. They balance outcomes under fixed inputs and uniform rules, but real betting is dynamic: prices update, orders can partially match, commissions vary by account, and markets settle under rule sets (voids, dead‑heats, Rule 4 deductions) that may diverge between bookmaker and exchange. Human error (misclicks, wrong market, stake rounding) compounds this. As a result, a “correct” lay stake can still yield unexpected losses if any real‑world assumption breaks.

Bankroll management and gubbing: how do you stay under the radar?

Bankroll controls limit the impact of slippage and outlier events. “Under the radar” behaviour reduces the chance of bookmaker restrictions. Neither removes risk entirely, but both materially reduce tail losses and workflow friction.

Capital allocation:

  • Separate bookmaker float and exchange float. Ensure exchange balance covers maximum projected liability plus a safety buffer (e.g., an extra 10–20% for odds drift).
  • Liability formula: Liability = (Lay odds − 1) × Lay stake. Plan exposure across overlapping events so total liability stays within limits.

Sizing and sequencing:

  • Keep stake sizes proportionate to market liquidity; avoid thin side markets unless you can place the lay first and watch fills.
  • Avoid cascading exposure across many legs without contingency funds (e.g., accas with sequential laying).

Execution hygiene:

  • Double‑check selection, market (Win vs Place), odds format (decimal), and commission before confirming.
  • Recalculate if any price or stake changes on the slip; match exchange odds to at least two decimal places where supported.

Staying off restriction lists (“gubbing”):

  • Avoid only betting on promotions; mix in organic markets and casual timings.
  • Vary stake sizes and markets; occasional multiples/Each Way mirrors typical recreational behaviour.
  • Do not consistently snipe palpable errors or late steam; follow bookmaker T&Cs.

Record‑keeping:

  • Maintain a ledger of date, event, market, odds, stakes, commission, expected and actual P&L.
  • Reconcile after settlement; investigate any mismatch (dead‑heat, R4 deduction, partial matches).

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an acceptable qualifying loss in UK matched betting and how do I measure it?

Target a loss per qualifier expressed as a percentage of back stake; 1–3% is typical depending on spread and commission. You measure it as absolute loss divided by the bookmaker stake, using the calculator’s two outcome profits which should match for a balanced qualifier. For example, with £10 at 3.00 backed and 3.05 laid at 2% commission, the balanced lay stake is £9.90 and the projected loss is about £0.30, i.e., 3.0% of stake. If you can reduce the spread to 3.00/3.02 at 2% commission, the loss falls to £0.20 on £10, i.e., 2.0%.

How much of an SNR free bet can I retain, and what odds maximise retention?

With equal back/lay odds and no commission, the retention fraction is (b−1)/b; higher back odds increase retention. Commission trims this slightly. At b = 4.0 with equal lay odds and 2% commission on the exchange, a £20 SNR free bet yields a balanced lay of about £15.08 and profit near £14.77, i.e., roughly 73.9% retention. At b = 5.0 on the same terms, retention rises toward about 80% without commission and approximately 78% with a 2% exchange fee.

How large should my exchange balance be to cover liability safely?

You must at least cover your maximum calculated liability; many keep a margin above that to absorb price moves or partial matches. Liability is (Lay odds − 1) × Lay stake and is reserved as soon as the lay is matched. Example: SNR free bet £25 at b = 5.0, laid at l = 5.10 with 2% commission gives L ≈ 19.69 and liability ≈ 4.10 × 19.69 = £80.71. Holding £95–£100 instead of exactly £80.71 materially reduces the chance of getting stuck if the market drifts before you can adjust.

How do I handle partial matches at multiple lay prices without breaking my hedge?

Aggregate matched lay stakes and compute a weighted average lay price, then recompute P&L using L = sum of lay stakes and l_eff = sum(Li × oi) ÷ sum(Li). This preserves accuracy across fills. If you needed L ≈ 10 and were matched £6 at 3.04 and £4 at 3.08, then L = 10 and l_eff = (6×3.04 + 4×3.08)/10 = 3.056. For a £10 qualifier at 3.00 back with 2% commission, your back‑win profit becomes about −£0.56 and back‑lose about −£0.20, revealing a small overlay from price slippage.

How do I adjust the calculator for Rule 4 deductions on horse racing?

Reduce the bookmaker’s back odds before calculating by applying the Rule 4 percentage to the win component. Use b_adj = 1 + (b − 1) × (1 − R4), where R4 is the decimal deduction (e.g., 0.20 for 20p in the pound). Example: If b = 5.00 and a 20p Rule 4 applies, b_adj = 1 + 4 × 0.80 = 4.20. Enter 4.20 as your back odds, then compute lay stake and liability as usual.

What happens if the bookmaker voids but the exchange lay still stands?

You are left with an unhedged exchange position; your P&L equals the exchange result alone. If the selection wins, you lose the full lay liability; if it loses, you win the lay stake net of commission. Using a common qualifier setup with L ≈ £9.90 at l = 3.05 and 2% commission, a book‑only void means a win at the exchange costs about £20.30 liability, while a loss at the exchange pays about £9.70 after commission. This asymmetry is why verifying both sides’ settlement rules is critical.

Are Betfair Premium Charges or other extra fees included, and how should I model them?

Standard calculators apply a single commission rate to exchange winnings; they do not model weekly Premium Charges. If you are subject to a higher effective take, approximate by increasing the commission input to your average rate. For instance, if your effective exchange take averages 20%, set commission to 20%. A £20 SNR at b = 5.0, l = 5.10 with c = 0.20 yields L ≈ 16.33 and profit ≈ £13.06 per outcome, i.e., about 65.3% retention instead of roughly 77% at 2%.

How should I round lay odds and stakes to match exchange increments without distorting P&L?

Round odds to the nearest tick shown on the exchange and stakes to the minimum currency increment allowed, then read the liability on the slip and reconcile it to the calculator’s output. Small rounding changes should be verified against displayed liability before confirming. If your balanced lay is £19.69 at 5.10 but you must round to £19.70, liability changes by about 4.10 × £0.01 = £0.041. The difference is small per bet, but consistent reconciliation prevents cumulative drift in your ledger.

How do I convert fractional odds to decimal for calculator inputs quickly?

Add 1 to the fractional ratio expressed as a decimal: Decimal = 1 + (Numerator ÷ Denominator). This aligns your bookmaker price with exchange‑style inputs. For example, 11/4 becomes 1 + 11/4 = 3.75, and 6/5 becomes 1 + 6/5 = 2.20. Enter these decimal odds along with your stake, lay odds, and commission.

Underlay vs overlay: how can I size the nudge quantitatively?

Each £1 reduction in lay stake improves back‑win profit by approximately (l − 1) and reduces back‑lose profit by approximately (1 − c). The reverse holds for an overlay of the same size. At l = 3.05 with 2% commission, reducing L by £0.40 lifts back‑win profit by about 2.05 × 0.40 = £0.82 and lowers back‑lose profit by about 0.98 × 0.40 = £0.39. From a balanced qualifier at roughly −£0.30 both ways, that underlay yields about +£0.52 if the back wins and about −£0.69 if it loses.

Can I use the calculator for exchange‑to‑exchange hedging or back‑back dutching?

Yes. Treat the first exchange back as the bookmaker side and adjust its odds for back‑side commission using b_eff = 1 + (b − 1) × (1 − c_back), then use the usual formulas with the second exchange as the lay side. Example: Back on Exchange A at b = 4.10 with 2% back commission gives b_eff ≈ 4.038. Laying on Exchange B at l = 4.00 with 2% lay commission for a £50 qualifier yields L ≈ 50 × 4.038 ÷ (4.00 − 0.02) = £50.73, producing about a £0.29 balanced loss, i.e., roughly 0.6% of stake.

How do I estimate the expected value (EV) of a free bet before placing it?

For a balanced SNR, the two outcome profits are nearly equal, so EV is approximately that per‑outcome profit. Higher back odds generally increase EV, but exchange commission reduces it. With S = £20, b = 5.00, l = 5.10, and c = 2%, L ≈ 15.75 and the per‑outcome profit is about £15.44, implying a retention of roughly 77.2% of the free bet’s face value. Changing to b = 4.00 on similar terms drops retention toward about 74%.

Do UK exchanges charge commission when the lay bet loses?

No. UK exchanges charge commission on net winnings in a market; if your lay loses, there is no commission on that market. If your lay wins, commission is applied to the lay stake won according to your account rate. For instance, at 2% commission with L = £10, a lay‑win pays about £9.80, while a lay‑loss pays nothing and you forfeit the calculated liability. Always verify your personal rate on the exchange’s help pages.

Smarkets 2% vs Betfair 5%: which produces a smaller qualifying loss at similar prices?

Often the lower commission wins even if the lay odds are slightly worse, but you should compute both options. Compare balanced losses side by side using your actual rates. On a £10 qualifier at b = 3.00, Smarkets at l = 3.05 and 2% yields L ≈ £9.90 and a loss ≈ £0.30. Betfair at l = 3.00 and 5% gives L ≈ £10.17 and a loss ≈ £0.34. Despite the better lay price, the higher commission made Betfair about £0.04 worse on this stake.

How do I model dead‑heats on Each Way bets across book and exchange legs?

Apply a dead‑heat factor k/n to the stake or return for the affected leg, then recompute P&L on both the bookmaker and the exchange place or win markets. The unaffected leg settles normally. If a £10 EW (i.e., £5 place stake) at b = 10.00 with 1/5 terms places in a two‑way dead‑heat for the final place, the place leg return is halved: standard place return would be £5 × 2.80 = £14; with a two‑way dead‑heat it becomes £7. On the exchange place lay, both potential profit and liability for that leg are similarly halved, so recalculate using 50% of the original matched lay for that settlement.
