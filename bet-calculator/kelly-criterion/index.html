How do you use the Kelly Criterion Calculator for betting?

The Kelly Criterion computes the optimal fraction of your bankroll to stake on a bet with known odds and your estimated win probability. For binary outcomes with net odds b (profit per 1 staked if you win), the full-Kelly fraction f* equals (b × p − (1 − p)) / b. The calculator accepts decimal or fractional odds, converts them to b, applies any commission on winnings (if relevant), and returns stake size as a percentage of bankroll and in cash. If f* is negative, the bet should be skipped because the expected growth is negative.

  • Choose odds format: decimal d or fractional a/b. The net odds are b = d − 1 (decimal) or b = a/b (fractional).
  • Estimate your win probability p (between 0 and 1). Optionally enter commission c on net winnings (exchanges), so effective decimal odds are d_eff = 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − c).
  • Enter your bankroll B (e.g., in GBP). Optionally set a cap (e.g., 5%) and a fraction-of-Kelly (e.g., 0.5 for half-Kelly) for risk control.
  • Calculate f* using b (or d_eff): full Kelly for decimal odds is f* = (d × p − 1) / (d − 1). The cash stake is Stake = B × f* × Kelly fraction (e.g., 0.5 for half-Kelly), subject to your cap.
  • Interpret outputs: a positive f* is your optimal bankroll percentage; a negative f* indicates no bet.

Enter odds and edge: what inputs do you need?

To size a stake, the calculator needs your odds, your assessed probability (or fair odds), bankroll, and any constraints. The “edge” can be entered directly or implied from your probability versus the book’s break-even probability. For decimal odds d (or effective d_eff if commission applies), the edge as expected return per 1 staked is ROI = d × p − 1. The break-even probability is p0 = 1 / d. The probability edge is p − p0. Both describe the same advantage from different angles.

Decimal vs fractional odds: does the formula change?

No. Kelly uses net odds b and your probability p, so the core formula is unchanged. You only convert your input odds to b. For decimal odds, b = d − 1. For fractional odds a/b, b = a/b. Break-even probability is p0 = 1 / d = 1 / (1 + b). The full-Kelly fraction can be written as f* = (b × p − (1 − p)) / b, which is algebraically identical to f* = (d × p − 1) / (d − 1).

Kelly variations and risk control

Kelly staking maximises the long-run growth rate of your bankroll under specific assumptions (accurate probabilities, independent bets, binary outcomes). In practice, bettors temper Kelly with fractional staking, caps, and minimums to manage drawdowns and model error. This section explains half-Kelly and other practical controls so you can trade off growth versus volatility with clear, data-backed choices.

What is half-Kelly and when should you use it?

Half-Kelly is fractional Kelly where you stake k × f* with k = 0.5, and f* is the full-Kelly fraction. It keeps the Kelly logic but halves the aggressiveness. This reduces drawdown depth and the penalty for overestimating your edge, while typically retaining a large share of Kelly’s growth rate (rule‑of‑thumb: around 70–80% for small edges and moderate odds).

  • Definition: fractional Kelly uses k ∈ (0,1]; stake fraction = k × f*, where f* = (b × p − (1 − p)) / b and b is net odds (decimal odds d minus 1).
  • Why it helps: model error is common. If your true edge is smaller than estimated, full-Kelly can be too aggressive. Halving the stake reduces downside from misestimation and correlations between bets.

Typical use cases for half-Kelly:

  • Uncertain edge estimates (small samples, new models, changing markets).
  • Positively correlated selections (same league, same model features).
  • Capital constraints and psychological drawdown tolerance.
  • Operational frictions (limits, inability to rebalance continuously).
  • Compute full-Kelly f* from your odds and probability.
  • Choose k (common: 0.25 to 0.75). Many start at 0.5 and adjust as evidence accumulates.
  • Apply caps (e.g., 1–5% of bankroll) to bound exposure on long shots or concentrated slates.

Bankroll management: caps, minimums, and staking plans

Even with fractional Kelly, you should bound risk with explicit limits and a coherent staking plan. Caps avoid oversized stakes on volatile edges; minimums reduce overtrading on tiny edges where slippage and error can dominate. Plans should specify how stakes adapt as bankroll and model confidence change.

Caps (maximum stake as % of bankroll)

  • Formula: f_final = min(k × f*, f_max). Common f_max ranges: 1–2% (conservative), 3–5% (moderate).
  • Rationale: protects against tail risk on high-variance or correlated bets.

Minimums (floor to avoid noise trading)

  • Formula: if k × f* < f_min then stake 0 (or a recording unit only).
  • Typical f_min: 0.25–0.50% when execution costs or estimate error are meaningful.

Staking plans

  • Flat stake: fixed cash per bet. Simple, low variance; ignores edge size.
  • Proportional (percentage-of-bankroll): stake a fixed % each bet. Tracks bankroll, but not edge size.
  • Fractional Kelly (recommended): scales with both edge and bankroll; add caps/minimums.
  • Unit-based: define 1 unit (e.g., 1% bankroll) and map edges to units (e.g., 0.5–5 units).
  • Avoid Martingale: increases exposure after losses and can escalate risk without improving EV.

Recalculation cadence

  • Update stakes as bankroll changes (daily/weekly) to stay proportional.
  • Re‑estimate model uncertainty regularly; lower k when variance or correlation rises.

Half-Kelly retains a substantial share of growth while cutting volatility roughly in half. Sensitivity to inputs is also clear from the Kelly formula. Let d be decimal odds, b = d − 1, p your win probability, q = 1 − p, f* = (d × p − 1) / (d − 1) = (b × p − q) / b. Then:

  • Probability sensitivity: ∂f*/∂p = d / (d − 1). Example: at d = 2.50, a +1 percentage point change in p shifts f* by +1.667 percentage points of bankroll.
  • Odds sensitivity: ∂f*/∂d = q / (d − 1)^2. Better odds increase f*; the effect is larger when d is close to 1 (shorter prices are less sensitive).

  • Estimated p = 0.44 → f* = 6.67%.
  • True p = 0.42 → f* = 3.33% (halved). Using half-Kelly would have limited overbetting.
  • Correlation note: if bets are positively correlated, effective variance is higher than single-bet variance; consider lowering k and tightening caps.
  • Execution: long shots (large b) amplify variance; even with positive EV, a cap (e.g., 2–3%) can materially reduce drawdown risk.

Where can you apply Kelly: sports, poker, forex, crypto?

The Kelly framework applies anywhere you can quantify edge (win probability or expected return) and payout ratio. In sports betting, closing line value (CLV) helps translate price improvement into an estimated edge. In poker, tournament and cash-game returns can be mapped to Kelly using ROI and variance. In forex and crypto, Kelly sizes directional trades from win probability and reward-to-risk, or allocates capital to a volatile asset using expected excess return and variance.

How does Kelly interact with CLV in sports betting?

CLV measures how your entry price compares to the closing price. If the close reflects the true probability, you can convert CLV into an EV estimate and then into a Kelly stake. With decimal odds d_entry and closing decimal odds d_close (on the same selection), the EV per £1 staked is approximately EV ≈ d_entry × (1 / d_close) − 1. The corresponding full‑Kelly fraction is f* = (d_entry × (1 / d_close) − 1) / (d_entry − 1). This assumes the closing price is an unbiased estimator of fair odds and ignores vig unless you explicitly remove it.

  • Compute p_close = 1 / d_close (or remove vig first for multi‑way markets; see notes).
  • Estimate EV: EV = d_entry × p_close − 1.
  • Compute full‑Kelly fraction: f* = (d_entry × p_close − 1) / (d_entry − 1).
  • Apply fractional Kelly (e.g., half‑Kelly) and caps to manage variance.

Poker: bankroll fractions for +EV edges

For repeated plays with variable outcomes (tournaments or cash sessions), a practical Kelly approximation uses ROI (mean fractional return per buy‑in) and the variance of fractional returns σ². The full‑Kelly fraction of bankroll to allocate per entry is f* = μ / σ², where μ is ROI (as a decimal). Because poker returns are noisy and correlated, most players use fractional Kelly (e.g., 0.25–0.50×) and target larger bankrolls than pure Kelly suggests.

  • Measure μ (expected ROI per buy‑in) from tracked results or robust models.
  • Estimate σ (standard deviation of fractional returns per buy‑in) from sample variance; larger fields have higher σ.
  • Compute f* = μ / σ²; then apply a fractional multiplier k (e.g., k = 0.5 for half‑Kelly).

Forex and crypto: position sizing with win probability

You can apply Kelly to directional trades with predefined stop (risk 1R) and take‑profit (reward rR). Let r be the reward‑to‑risk ratio and p the probability of hitting take‑profit before the stop. Then full‑Kelly is f* = (r × p − (1 − p)) / r. With symmetric r = 1, f* = 2p − 1. Because trade outcomes are correlated and parameters uncertain, many traders use 0.25–0.50× Kelly.

Evolutionary path: how Kelly became today’s staking standard

The Kelly Criterion emerged from information theory in the 1950s and migrated into gambling and finance because it answers a fundamental question: what fraction of capital should you risk when you have an edge? From J. L. Kelly Jr.’s original growth‑optimal result through Thorp’s real‑world betting and trading applications, practitioners refined the idea with fractional Kelly to handle estimation error, correlation, and real‑world frictions. Today, Kelly (usually in fractional form) is the reference point for staking plans across sports betting, poker, and markets.

Who was John L. Kelly Jr. and what problem did he solve?

John L. Kelly Jr. (1923–1965) was a scientist at Bell Telephone Laboratories. In 1956 he published “A New Interpretation of Information Rate” in the Bell System Technical Journal, connecting Shannon’s information theory to optimal capital growth. Kelly showed that, when payoffs and probabilities are known, staking to maximise expected log wealth (the “growth‑optimal” strategy) yields a unique optimal fraction for each favourable opportunity—the result gamblers now call the Kelly Criterion. Although framed in terms of communication channels and side information, the solution maps directly to betting: given win probability p and net odds b, the growth‑optimal stake fraction is f* = (b × p − (1 − p)) / b; if f* ≤ 0, you should not bet.

Why fixed staking and Martingale fell short

Pre‑Kelly staking rules either ignored edge or tried to “force” wins by escalating risk. Both approaches fail under scrutiny because they do not maximise growth and can raise the probability of severe drawdowns or ruin.

Fixed staking (flat cash per bet):

  • Ignores edge size and odds; a 1‑unit bet at 1.80 and at 3.50 carries very different risk‑return profiles.
  • Suboptimal growth: proportional, edge‑aware staking achieves higher expected log growth for the same opportunities.
  • Bankroll drift: as capital changes, fixed cash stakes effectively change the risk fraction without intent.

Proportional percent staking (without edge weighting):

  • Tracks bankroll but not EV; you risk the same fraction on weak and strong edges.
  • Lower long‑run growth than Kelly when edges vary across bets.

Martingale and similar loss‑recovery systems:

  • Assume unlimited capital and no limits; real markets and bookmakers impose limits, so sequences can and do terminate in large losses.
  • Negative‑EV games guarantee eventual ruin with probability 1 over an infinite horizon, regardless of progression.
  • Even with a positive edge, Martingale is not growth‑optimal and creates extreme tail risk compared with Kelly.
  • Kelly replaces “chasing losses” with a forward‑looking, opportunity‑based stake.
  • Fractional Kelly further controls variance and model error while preserving most of Kelly’s growth advantage.

The strongest critique: is Kelly too aggressive for bettors?

The main critique is not about correctness—Kelly is mathematically optimal for log‑growth under its assumptions—but about practicality. Real bettors face estimation error, correlation across picks, market frictions, and psychological drawdown limits. Full‑Kelly can be too volatile if your edge is noisy or if bets are correlated.

Estimation error and overbetting:

  • Overstating p or understating odds uncertainty leads to staking above the true Kelly fraction, reducing expected log growth.
  • Example (even‑money, d = 2.00): with p = 0.55, f* = 0.10. At 2×Kelly (f = 0.20), expected log growth is approximately zero; above that it turns negative.

Correlation and clustering:

  • Edges often share features (same model, league, news), increasing effective variance versus independent‑bet assumptions.
  • Portfolio‑level Kelly requires a covariance model; single‑bet Kelly can over‑allocate if correlations are ignored.

Outcome complexity and frictions:

  • Non‑binary outcomes (pushes, partial wins) require generalised Kelly; using a binary proxy can mis‑size stakes.
  • Limits, slippage, and fees reduce effective edge; apply them before sizing.

Mitigations:

  • Use fractional Kelly (e.g., 0.25–0.50×) to cut variance and protect against edge overestimation.
  • Impose caps (e.g., 1–3% of bankroll), set a minimum actionable edge, and aggregate correlated bets before sizing.
  • Shrink probabilities toward market (or CLV‑implied) priors to reduce overfitting risk.
  • Bottom line: Kelly is a benchmark. Most successful practitioners run below full‑Kelly unless they have long, high‑confidence track records and robust portfolio‑level risk controls.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the Kelly fraction ever exceed 100% of bankroll?

No. For binary bets the full‑Kelly fraction f* is at most 1, so the optimal stake never exceeds 100% of bankroll. Algebraically, f* = (b × p − q) / b with b ≥ 0, p ≤ 1, q ≥ 0 implies the numerator is ≤ b and therefore f* ≤ 1.

How risky are losing streaks under Kelly even if I have an edge?

Kelly does not eliminate losing streaks; it sizes them. With even‑money odds (d = 2.00) and p = 0.55, full‑Kelly stakes 10% per bet and the chance of at least one run of 8 straight losses in 1,000 bets is about 81% because the expected count of such runs is (1 − p)^8 × (N − 7) ≈ 0.45^8 × 993 ≈ 1.67, and 1 − e^(−1.67) ≈ 0.81. An 8‑loss streak at 10% per bet cuts bankroll to 0.9^8 ≈ 43% of starting size, a 57% drawdown; halving Kelly to 5% shrinks the same streak’s drawdown to 0.95^8 ≈ 66% of start, a 34% drawdown.

Can I use Kelly for accumulators/parlays and what about correlated legs?

Yes. Multiply decimal odds to get combined d, multiply leg win probabilities to get p if legs are independent, then apply f* = (d × p − 1) / (d − 1). For example, with d1 = 2.00, p1 = 0.52 and d2 = 1.90, p2 = 0.55, the parlay has d = 3.80, p = 0.286, so f* ≈ (3.80 × 0.286 − 1) / 2.80 = 0.031, or 3.1% of bankroll; if legs are positively correlated, p is overstated and true f* is smaller, so reduce with a fractional multiplier (e.g., 0.5×) or a cap.

How do I handle pushes, half‑wins, and Asian quarter‑lines in Kelly sizing?

Model the probabilities of win p, loss q, and push s, then maximise expected log growth; the first‑order condition gives f* = (b × p − q) / [b × (p + q)], where b is net odds and p + q = 1 − s. Example: Asian −0.25 at d = 1.95 with p = 0.48, s = 0.08, q = 0.44 gives f* ≈ (0.95 × 0.48 − 0.44) / [0.95 × 0.92] = 0.016 / 0.874 ≈ 1.83%.

How do I convert American odds and Asian quarter‑lines for this UK‑style Kelly calculator?

What cap should I use to control drawdowns when applying Kelly?

A practical approach is to cap at the 90th–95th percentile of your historical f* distribution so outliers don’t dominate risk; for most edges this lands between 1% and 5% of bankroll. As a rule of thumb, use 1–2% caps on long shots with d ≥ 6.00, 2–3% on mid‑range odds d ≈ 2.20–4.00, and 3–5% on short prices d ≤ 2.10; for example, with d = 6.00 and p = 0.30, f* = (6 × 0.30 − 1)/5 = 0.16, so a 3% cap limits variance while preserving positive growth.

How do commissions and fees on exchanges change the Kelly stake?

Apply commission to winnings before sizing by using effective decimal odds d_eff = 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − c), then compute f* with d_eff. For instance, at d = 3.00, p = 0.36, and commission c = 2%, d_eff = 2.96 and f* ≈ (2.96 × 0.36 − 1)/1.96 = 3.35%, versus 4.00% without commission; a 2% fee trims the stake by 0.65 percentage points in this case.

How much long‑run growth do I give up by using half‑ or quarter‑Kelly?

For small edges, the growth at fraction k of Kelly is approximately k × (2 − k) of full‑Kelly growth, so half‑Kelly keeps about 75% and quarter‑Kelly about 43.75%. Example at even‑money with p = 0.55: full‑Kelly f* = 10% yields expected log growth ≈ 0.00501 per bet; half‑Kelly (5%) gives ≈ 0.00375 (75%), quarter‑Kelly (2.5%) gives ≈ 0.00219 (43.7%).

How accurate must my win probability be to avoid overbetting with Kelly?

Overstating p so you bet above true Kelly can erase growth; at even‑money with true p = 0.55, full‑Kelly f* = 10% gives log‑growth ≈ 0.00501 per bet, but staking 20% (2×Kelly) yields ≈ −0.00013, effectively zero or negative growth. A conservative fix is to shrink p toward market or use 0.25–0.50× Kelly until you have thousands of outcomes to validate calibration.

What if bookmaker limits or minimum stakes block the exact Kelly amount?

Treat the filled stake as an effective fractional Kelly k' = Stake_filled / (B × f*), then expect per‑bet growth to scale by approximately k' × (2 − k') relative to full‑Kelly for small edges. For example, if f* calls for 6% of a £10,000 bankroll (£600) but limit is £200, then k' ≈ 0.333 and you keep about 0.333 × 1.667 ≈ 55.5% of the growth on that bet; rebalance on future opportunities rather than forcing size.

How often should I rebalance stake sizes as the bankroll moves?

Recalculate when bankroll changes by 1–5% so the risk fraction remains aligned; fixed cash stakes drift risk materially after large moves. For example, a £300 flat stake calibrated as 3% of £10,000 becomes 3.75% after a 20% drawdown to £8,000, a 25% unintended increase in risk per bet; rebalancing restores the intended fraction.

How do I allocate across several simultaneous bets so total risk stays bounded?

Sum the individual Kelly‑or‑fractional‑Kelly percentages and, if the total exceeds your daily or portfolio cap, scale all stakes by a common factor so the sum hits the cap. For instance, if four independent bets call for 2.0%, 1.5%, 3.0%, and 4.0% (total 10.5%) and your cap is 10%, multiply each by 10/10.5 ≈ 0.952 so the portfolio risk stays within limits.

Are gambling winnings taxed in the UK and do taxes change Kelly sizing?

For UK residents, gambling and spread‑betting winnings are generally not taxed, so no adjustment to Kelly is required; exchange commissions still reduce effective odds. Trading profits from CFDs, spot forex, and most crypto are taxable; if a 20% capital‑gains rate applies, you can approximate the impact by taxing net winnings, which turns d into d_eff = 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − tax), trimming f* by the same mechanism as commission.

How do I remove vig in three‑way markets before applying Kelly?

Divide each implied probability 1/d_i by their sum to get fair probabilities that sum to 1, then use the fair p for your selection. Example: with closing odds Home 2.60, Draw 3.20, Away 2.90, implieds are 0.3846, 0.3125, 0.3448, summing to 1.0419; fair p(Home) = 0.3846 / 1.0419 ≈ 0.3691. If you entered at d_entry = 2.80, EV ≈ 2.80 × 0.3691 − 1 = 0.0335 and full‑Kelly f* ≈ 0.0335 / (2.80 − 1) = 1.86%.

How can I size from a probability range rather than a single estimate?

Use the lower bound to avoid overbetting and report a stake band for transparency. For example, at d = 2.50 with p in [0.40, 0.48], f* ranges from max(0, (2.50 × 0.40 − 1)/1.50) = 0.00 to (2.50 × 0.48 − 1)/1.50 ≈ 0.1333, so a conservative half‑Kelly based on p_low stakes 0% and an optimistic half‑Kelly stakes about 6.67%; capping at 5% further constrains risk.

Why does Kelly sometimes suggest large stakes on long shots and how should I respond?

When edge is big relative to payout, f* can be high even at long odds; for example, d = 6.00 with p = 0.30 gives f* = 16% because EV per £1 is 0.80. Execution risk, correlation, and estimate error are also larger on long shots, so many practitioners combine fractional Kelly at 0.25–0.50× with a hard cap of 2–3% on d ≥ 6.00 to cut tail risk while keeping positive growth.
