<h2>How are returns and profits calculated?</h2>
<p>The calculator follows standard UK settlement rules. For win-only singles, profit equals stake multiplied by the odds margin, and total return equals stake plus profit. For decimal odds, the stake is already included in the price; for fractional odds, the stake is added after calculating profit. Each-way singles are split into two equal parts (win and place) and settled separately. Where applicable, Rule 4 deductions and dead-heat rules are applied to winnings (not stake), and void selections return the stake.</p>
<h3>Fractional vs decimal: how do we convert and compute?</h3>
<p>Fractional odds A/B express net profit B-stake yields A profit units (profit = stake × A/B; return = stake + profit). Decimal odds D express total return per £1 (return = stake × D; profit = stake × (D − 1)). Conversion is direct: decimal = 1 + A/B; fractional = (D − 1) as a fraction. Returns are identical whichever format you use.</p>
<ul><li>Fractional A/B → Decimal D = 1 + A/B.</li><li>Win-only fractional profit = stake × (A/B); return = stake + profit.</li><li>Win-only decimal return = stake × D; profit = return − stake = stake × (D − 1).</li><li>Rounding: UK operators typically display to two decimals for currency, applying rounding at the end of the calculation.</li></ul>

<h3>How are each-way singles calculated: place terms and reductions?</h3>
<p>An each-way (EW) single splits your stake into two equal bets: a win part and a place part. The place part is settled at reduced “place terms” (a fraction of the win odds) and for a set number of places, which vary by race conditions and bookmaker rules. Your total outlay is double the per-part stake (e.g., £10 EW = £20 total).</p>
<p>Place odds:</p>
<ul><li>Fractional: place fractional = (A/B) × place fraction (e.g., 8/1 at 1/5 → 8/5).</li><li>Decimal: place decimal = 1 + (D − 1) × place fraction.</li></ul>
<p>Common UK place terms (can vary; check market rules):</p>
<ul><li>2–4 runners: win only (no place part offered).</li><li>5–7 runners: 1/4 odds, places 1–2.</li><li>8+ runners (non-handicaps): 1/5 odds, places 1–3.</li><li>Handicaps 12–15 runners: 1/4 odds, places 1–3.</li><li>Handicaps 16+ runners: 1/4 odds, places 1–4.</li></ul>
<p>Settlement outcomes:</p>
<ul><li>Win: both win and place parts pay.</li><li>Placed (not first): win part loses; place part pays.</li><li>Unplaced: both parts lose.</li><li>Void/Non-runner: stake returned on both parts.</li></ul>
<p>Worked example: £10 each-way at 8/1 with 1/5 place terms. Total stake £20. If the selection wins: win return = £10 × (8/1) + £10 = £90. Place return = £10 × (8/5) + £10 = £26. Total return £116; profit £96. If the selection is placed (not first): total return £26; profit £6.</p>
<p>Note: Any industry deductions (e.g., Rule 4) are applied to the winnings portion of each part. Bookmakers may also advertise “extra places” promotions; when offered, they change only the place count, not the place fraction, unless stated.</p>
<h3>What about Rule 4, dead heats, and void bets?</h3>
<p>These adjustments follow well-established UK rules. The calculator applies them to the winnings part of your bet according to the parameters you enter.</p>
<p>Rule 4 (non-runner after betting formed):</p>
<ul><li>A per-£1 deduction (e.g., 5p–90p) is applied to winnings based on the withdrawn horse’s price at withdrawal; stake is not reduced.</li><li>Adjusted decimal margin = (original decimal − 1) × (1 − deduction rate). Return = stake × (1 + adjusted margin).</li><li>Example: £10 at 4/1 (margin 4.00). With a 10p Rule 4, adjusted margin = 4.00 × 0.90 = 3.60 → return £46.00; profit £36.00.</li></ul>
<p>Dead heats:</p>
<ul><li>For an N-way tie, divide the stake by N; settle one portion as a winner at full odds, treat the remainder as losers.</li><li>Win example (2-way): £10 at 6/1 → settle £5 at 6/1 (return £35), £5 loses → total return £35; profit £25.</li><li>Place example (last paying place tied 2-way): place stake £10 at place odds 2/1 → settle £5 at 2/1 (return £15), £5 loses → place return £15.</li></ul>
<p>Void bets and non-runners:</p>
<ul><li>Singles that are void or non-runners return the stake in full.</li><li>Football markets may specify push conditions (e.g., Draw No Bet if drawn). The calculator’s “Void” result reflects this by returning the stake.</li></ul>
<p>Always check the market rules published by your operator; settlement conventions can vary by event and promotion. For dispute resolution in Great Britain, the independent ADR provider will be named in the bookmaker’s terms, and regulatory oversight is by the UK Gambling Commission.</p>

<h3>How are markets like BTTS, Over/Under, Draw No Bet settled?</h3>
<p>Settlement follows clear, market-specific rules. The calculator mirrors these by letting you choose the market logic and enter the final score at 90 minutes (unless your market explicitly includes ET/pens).</p>
<p>Both Teams To Score (BTTS):</p>
<ul><li>BTTS: Yes wins if both teams score at least once in 90 minutes (own goals count).</li><li>BTTS: No wins if at least one team fails to score in 90 minutes.</li><li>If a match is abandoned, settlement depends on bookmaker rules; commonly void if not completed on the day or within a specified window.</li></ul>
<p>Over/Under Goals:</p>
<ul><li>Settled on total goals at FT. For lines ending in .5 (e.g., 2.5), no push is possible.</li><li>For integer lines (e.g., 2.0), “push” may apply: exactly 2 goals → stake returned.</li><li>Example: Over 2.5 wins at FT scores of 2-1, 3-0, etc.; loses at 1-1, 2-0.</li></ul>
<p>Draw No Bet (DNB):</p>
<ul><li>Settled on the 90-minute result: if your team wins, bet wins; if draw, stake is void/refunded; if your team loses, bet loses.</li><li>Example at FT: Team A 1-1 Team B → DNB on either side is void.</li></ul>
<p>Corner/Bookings markets (if supported by your bookmaker):</p>
<ul><li>Typically 90-minute settlement. Player bookings and corners accrued in ET do not count unless stated.</li></ul>
<h3>How does cash out affect your potential return?</h3>
<p>Cash Out converts a live bet into an immediate, fixed payout offered by the bookmaker. Once accepted, your original potential return no longer applies. Partial Cash Out splits your stake: one part is settled instantly at the offer price, and the remainder stays active at the original odds.</p>
<p>Full Cash Out:</p>
<ul><li>Payout equals the Cash Out offer; finalised immediately regardless of the eventual match result.</li><li>Example: £10 at 2.50 (potential £25). If offered £14.50 to Cash Out and you accept, your return is £14.50; your bet is closed.</li></ul>
<p>Partial Cash Out (proportional split):</p>
<ul><li>If you cash out fraction f of your stake, immediate payout ≈ f × (full Cash Out offer). Remaining live stake ≈ (1 − f) × original stake at original odds.</li><li>Example: Original £10 at 2.50; offer £14.50. Partial Cash Out 50%: receive ~£7.25 now; £5 continues at 2.50 (potential remaining return £12.50).</li></ul>
<p>Availability and rules:</p>
<ul><li>Cash Out is not guaranteed; markets may be suspended during key events or priced off.</li><li>Free bet stakes: if your bet was placed with a free bet, the “stake not returned” rule usually applies; Cash Out calculations reflect this (check operator terms).</li><li>Settlement disputes follow the operator’s published rules and applicable regulations in Great Britain.</li></ul>
<h2>Single vs Double vs Patent: which calculator do I need?</h2>
<p>Choose the calculator that matches your bet structure. A Single is one selection settled once. A Double is two selections multiplied together; both must win for a full payout (void legs reduce to a Single). A Patent covers three selections as seven bets (3 Singles, 3 Doubles, 1 Treble), returning something from just one winner. Using the correct calculator ensures accurate returns, especially when each-way terms, Rule 4 deductions, or voids apply.</p>
<h3>Single vs double: how does the payout differ?</h3>
<p>A Single settles on one selection only. A Double multiplies two selections; both must win for the combined return. If one leg of a Double is void (e.g., non-runner), the bet settles as a Single on the remaining leg at its odds. If either leg loses, the Double loses.</p>
<p>Single (win-only):</p>
<ul><li>Decimal: Return = Stake × D; Profit = Stake × (D − 1).</li><li>Fractional A/B: Profit = Stake × (A/B); Return = Stake + Profit.</li></ul>
<p>Double (win-only):</p>
<ul><li>Decimal: Return = Stake × D1 × D2; Profit = Return − Stake.</li><li>If one leg is void: Return = Stake × Dw (the winning leg’s decimal); Profit = Return − Stake.</li></ul>
<p>Each-way:</p>
<ul><li>Single EW: win part + place part (at reduced place odds) settle separately.</li><li>Double EW: four parts (win-win, win-place, place-win, place-place). Use an Acca/EW-capable calculator for accuracy.</li></ul>

<h2>Trust, history, and risks</h2>
<p>Accurate bet calculators are built on published settlement rules used by licensed UK bookmakers. They convert odds, apply each-way terms, and factor in adjustments such as Rule 4 and dead heats. Understanding where these tools came from—and their limitations—helps you use them confidently and avoid common mistakes, especially in football and horse racing markets.</p>
<h3>How did bet calculators evolve from slips to apps?</h3>
<p>Before digital tools, punters estimated returns manually or asked shop staff to price up multiples. As online betting grew, web-based calculators standardised the maths, added each-way logic, and automated Rule 4 and dead-heat adjustments. Modern apps now mirror operator settlement conventions and let you toggle football market contexts (90 minutes vs To Qualify), free-bet stakes, and advanced racing rules.</p>
<p>Paper era:</p>
<ul><li>Punters used fractional odds tables and mental arithmetic; shop tills produced final figures only after a bet was struck.</li></ul>
<p>Early web calculators:</p>
<ul><li>Introduced consistent conversion between fractional, decimal, and American odds; automated Singles and basic multiples.</li></ul>
<p>Modern calculators:</p>
<ul><li>Support each-way structures, Rule 4 scales, dead-heat splits, void/push handling, and football market-specific settlement.</li><li>Quality-of-life features: stake-not-returned (free bets), rounding controls, and partial/early cash-out scenarios.</li></ul>
<h3>The other side of the coin: When can a calculator mislead?</h3>
<p>Even the best calculator can yield the wrong answer if inputs don’t match the market’s settlement rules. The maths is precise; mismatched assumptions are the usual culprit. Use the checklist below to avoid pitfalls.</p>
<p>Market context mismatch:</p>
<ul><li>Football 90 minutes vs To Qualify/Lift the Trophy. AET/penalties rarely count for 1X2 markets; entering the “winner after pens” as a 90-minute win will overstate returns.</li></ul>
<p>Each-way terms entered incorrectly:</p>
<ul><li>Wrong place fraction (1/5 vs 1/4) or wrong number of places (e.g., extra places promo not reflected) changes place returns materially.</li></ul>
<p>Adjustments not applied:</p>
<ul><li>Rule 4 after a withdrawal or a dead heat at the last paying place can cut winnings; forgetting to apply them inflates results.</li></ul>
<p>Free bets and stake return:</p>
<ul><li>Many free bets pay “stake not returned.” If you forget to toggle SNR, your total return will be overstated by the stake amount.</li></ul>
<p>Rounding and displayed prices:</p>
<ul><li>Operators round currency to two decimals at settlement. Minor pence differences can occur if you round mid-calculation.</li></ul>
<p>Voids/pushes in multiples:</p>
<ul><li>Void legs reduce an accumulator component (e.g., Double to Single). Treating a void as a winner or loser will distort totals.</li></ul>
<p>Cash Out vs potential:</p>
<ul><li>Once you Cash Out, your potential return is replaced by the offer value. Calculating “what might have been” using original odds is no longer relevant to your settled payout.</li></ul>

<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>What are “official returns” in UK racing and when is a single bet settled?</h3>
<p>A single is settled when the race result is declared official (“weighed in”), and the official starting price (SP) or your taken price is used to compute the return. If you took SP, your payout is stake × SP (decimal) or stake + stake × SP margin (fractional); settlement typically occurs within minutes of the weigh-in and uses industry-standard Rule 4 and dead-heat conventions where relevant. The SP is the industry benchmark for settlement; for example, a £10 SP single at 5/1 returns £60.00 (profit £50.00) once the result is official.</p>
<h3>How do I calculate a free bet (stake not returned) single versus a cash bet?</h3>
<p>For stake-not-returned (SNR) bets, only winnings are paid; the original stake is excluded from the return. In decimal odds D, a cash bet returns stake × D, while an SNR free bet returns stake × (D − 1). For example, £10 at 2.50 pays £25.00 with a cash stake but £15.00 with SNR; at 5.00 it pays £50.00 cash versus £40.00 SNR. In fractional A/B, profit is stake × A/B for both, but only a cash bet adds the stake back to make the total return.</p>
<h3>Are gambling winnings taxable for UK punters in 2026?</h3>
<p>Individual punters in Great Britain pay 0% tax on gambling winnings; returns are paid gross and are not reportable as income. Operators, not customers, pay duties such as Remote Gaming Duty (historically 21% for remote casino games) or General Betting Duty (historically 15% on fixed-odds bets), but these taxes are not deducted from your bet settlement. A £10 single at 3/1 still settles at £50.00 return (profit £40.00) to the customer.</p>
<h3>Which Rule 4 band applies and how do I adjust my payout?</h3>
<p>Rule 4 deducts a fixed pence-in-the-pound from winnings based on the withdrawn runner’s price; you multiply your winnings by (1 − deduction). Common bands are 90p for 1/9 or shorter, 85p for 2/11–2/17, 80p for 1/4–1/5, 75p for 3/10–2/7, 70p for 2/5–1/3, 65p for 8/15–4/9, 60p for 8/13–4/7, 55p for 4/5–4/6, 50p for 20/21–5/6, 45p for Evens–6/5, 40p for 5/4–6/4, 35p for 13/8–7/4, 30p for 15/8–9/4, 25p for 5/2–3/1, 20p for 10/3–4/1, 15p for 9/2–11/2, 10p for 6/1–9/1, 5p for 10/1–14/1, and no deduction at 16/1 or bigger. For example, £10 at 4/1 has £40.00 winnings; a 20p Rule 4 pays £40.00 × 0.80 = £32.00 winnings, so total return is £42.00.</p>
<h3>How do dead heats affect a single each-way bet payout?</h3>
<p>In a dead heat, divide the relevant stake by the number of tied runners, settle one portion as a winner at full odds, and treat the remainder as losers. If £10 each-way at 10/1 (1/5, 3 places) ties three-way for second, the place stake £10 is split into £3.33 winners and £6.67 losers; place odds are 10/1 × 1/5 = 2/1, so place return is £3.33 × 2 + £3.33 ≈ £10.00, while the win part loses unless your selection is first. The total each-way return in this scenario is approximately £10.00 on a £20.00 outlay (loss ≈ £10.00).</p>
<h3>What’s the difference between Ante-Post and NRNB for single bets if my horse is a non-runner?</h3>
<p>Ante-post singles generally forfeit the stake if your selection does not run, while Non-Runner No Bet (NRNB) singles refund the stake when the selection is a non-runner. On a £20 ante-post single at 8/1, a non-runner returns £0.00; the same £20 at 8/1 under NRNB returns £20.00. For winners and placers, both markets settle at the taken odds and applicable terms; only the non-runner treatment differs.</p>
<h3>Can I model Asian handicap or quarter-goal lines (-0.25, +0.75) on a single?</h3>
<p>Yes, split the stake across the two adjacent half lines and apply push or win/lose rules to each portion. For -0.25, treat it as half on 0 (push if draw) and half on -0.5 (loses if draw); for example, £20 at 1.90 on -0.25 returning a draw yields £10 pushed and £10 lost, so total return £10.00. For +0.75, split into +0.5 and +1.0; a one-goal defeat returns half the stake on +1.0 and loses the +0.5 half, while a draw wins both halves at the quoted price.</p>
<h3>How do UK bookmakers round payouts on single bets?</h3>
<p>Most GB operators round currency at settlement to the nearest penny, with 0.5p rounding up, after completing all calculations and deductions. This end-rounding can create 1p differences versus mid-calculation rounding; for example, a computed return of £19.995 rounds to £20.00, while rounding earlier steps might show £19.99. Consistent end-rounding avoids cumulative rounding drift across adjustments such as Rule 4 or dead heats.</p>
<h3>What happens to single bets if a football match is postponed or abandoned?</h3>
<p>If a match is not completed, many GB operators void singles unless the event is finished within a defined window; common policies require completion the same day or within 24–72 hours, but exact cut-offs vary by bookmaker. Practically, a £25 Over 2.5 Goals single is usually voided and £25 returned if the match stops at 1-0 after 30 minutes and is not resumed within the operator’s time window; if later completed within policy, settlement uses the final 90-minute result.</p>
<h3>How does Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) change a single’s return and how should I input it?</h3>
<p>BOG replaces your taken early price with the higher of early price or SP, so returns increase only if SP is bigger. Enter the final settled odds that apply under BOG; for example, £10 at 8/1 with SP 10/1 pays £110.00 under BOG instead of £90.00, adding £20.00 profit. If SP shortens to 6/1, you still receive the taken 8/1 and would input 8/1.</p>
<h3>What is the break-even win rate for my single at given odds?</h3>
<p>Break-even equals the implied probability of your odds: for decimal D it is 1/D, for fractional A/B it is B/(A + B), and for American +X it is 100/(X + 100) while for −Y it is Y/(Y + 100). Concretely, 2.00 needs 50.00%, 1.91 needs about 52.36%, 2.50 needs 40.00%, and 4.00 needs 25.00% to break even over time; winning more frequently than this threshold yields positive expected value.</p>
