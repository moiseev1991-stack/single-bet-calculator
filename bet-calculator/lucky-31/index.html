What is a Lucky 31 and how does the calculator work?

A Lucky 31 is a full-cover bet with singles on five selections. It creates 31 separate lines: every possible single, double, treble, fourfold and the fivefold accumulator. A calculator processes each line independently based on your stake per line, odds format (fractional or decimal), and the result of each selection (win, lose, or void/non-runner). It then sums all winning line returns and shows total return, total stake, and profit or loss. Voids reduce affected multiples (treating the voided leg at odds of 1.00), while losers make that specific line return zero. If placed each-way, the calculator duplicates all lines into win and place parts and applies the relevant place terms.

Bet breakdown: 31 lines from singles to fivefold

A Lucky 31 uses five selections to create every combination from singles to the fivefold. The line count is fixed and does not change with stake size.

  • Singles: 5 lines (one on each selection)
  • Doubles: 10 lines (all two-selection combos)
  • Trebles: 10 lines (all three-selection combos)
  • Fourfolds: 5 lines (all four-selection combos)
  • Fivefold: 1 line (all five selections together)

How are returns calculated for winners, losers, and voids?

Each line is settled independently and then summed. For a win-only Lucky 31 with unit stake s per line:

  • Convert odds to decimal if needed. For fractional a/b, decimal = 1 + a/b.
  • Winning line return = s × (product of decimal odds of all winning legs in that line).
  • If any leg in a line loses, that line returns £0.00.
  • If a leg is void/non-runner, treat it as decimal 1.00 in that line (the multiple reduces). A void single returns the stake.
  • Total return = sum of all line returns. Profit/Loss = Total return − (31 × s).

How does each-way change win and place returns?

Each-way (EW) doubles a Lucky 31 to 62 lines: 31 win lines and 31 place lines. Your total stake doubles because you are placing two bets on each line. The win part uses full win odds; the place part uses reduced place odds based on the market’s place terms (for example, 1/5 or 1/4 of the win odds, with the number of places determined by the event rules). A winner also counts as “placed” for the place part.

  • Stake: An EW Lucky 31 at s per line stakes 62 × s in total.

Place odds conversion:

  • Fractional: (a/b) at place fraction f becomes (a × f) / b.
  • Decimal: place_decimal = 1 + (win_decimal − 1) × f.

Settlement:

  • Win part: every leg in that line must win to return.
  • Place part: every leg in that line must finish in a place (winners count as placed) to return.
  • Voids: treated as odds 1.00 on both win and place parts (the multiple reduces).

Enter odds, results, and each-way or Rule 4 deductions

Follow these steps to set up and settle a Lucky 31 correctly. The same inputs are applied consistently to every affected line of the bet.

  • Select odds format (fractional, decimal, or American) and stake per line. Remember, a win-only Lucky 31 has 31 lines; each-way doubles to 62 lines.

  • Win: the selection won (for EW, this also counts as placed).
  • Lose: the selection lost (that line returns £0 on the relevant part).

Apply Rule 4 deductions (only if your race had a late withdrawal). Enter the deduction as pence in the pound (e.g., 20p = 0.20). The Rule 4 deduction reduces the winnings portion only:

  • Adjusted decimal for settlement = 1 + (original_decimal − 1) × (1 − deduction).
  • Adjusted fractional profit = original_fraction × (1 − deduction).
  • Example: 4/1 with a 20p Rule 4 becomes profit odds 4 × 0.8 = 3.2 (decimal 4.20).

Review totals:

  • Total stake = lines × stake per line (31×s for win-only; 62×s for EW).
  • Total return = sum of all winning line returns (after deductions and place terms).
  • Profit/Loss = Total return − Total stake.

Does each-way double the cost for a Lucky 31?

Yes. Each-way turns 31 win-only lines into 62 lines by adding an equal-stake place part alongside the win part for every line. Because you are staking on two parts (win and place) for each of the same 31 combinations, the total stake doubles. This doubling happens regardless of the odds or place terms; place terms affect returns, not the cost.

  • Example at £0.50 per line: win-only costs £15.50; each-way costs £31.00.
  • Example at £0.20 per line: win-only costs £6.20; each-way costs £12.40.
  • Rule 4 deductions, voids, and dead-heats can change returns but never change the total stake already placed.

What is my total liability and how is it shown?

For fixed-odds bookmaker bets like a Lucky 31, your total liability is simply your total stake (the amount you risk and can lose). The calculator shows this clearly as “Total stake,” alongside “Total return” and “Profit/Loss.” Liability here is not the same as exchange lay liability—it does not depend on odds; it equals the money you have staked.

  • Win-only liability (exposure) = 31 × s
  • Each-way liability (exposure) = 62 × s
  • Display conventions: calculators typically show Stake per line, Lines, Total stake, Estimated return based on current inputs, and Profit/Loss = Return − Total stake.

Examples:

  • £0.20 per line: liability is £6.20 (win-only) or £12.40 (each-way).
  • £0.50 per line: liability is £15.50 (win-only) or £31.00 (each-way).

If you use a free bet token (stake not returned), your out-of-pocket liability may be reduced, but the calculator will reflect this only if it offers a “free bet” or “stake not returned” toggle; otherwise liability equals the cash stake you enter.

Is a Lucky 31 right for me compared with other perms?

Full-cover bets let you spread five or more selections across many combinations to balance the chance of a return against total cost and volatility. The Lucky 31 is a five-selection full cover that includes singles (31 lines). Its closest no-singles analogue is the Canadian (also known as a Super Yankee) with 26 lines. For four selections you would typically choose a Lucky 15 (includes singles) or a Yankee (no singles). For three selections a Patent is the full-cover with singles, while six selections are usually covered by a Heinz (no singles). Choosing the right perm depends on how many picks you have, whether you want singles, your typical prices, and your bankroll.

Lucky 31 vs Lucky 15 vs Yankee: when to choose each?

Match the perm to your number of selections and appetite for singles, cost, and volatility. These are common use-cases:

  • Lucky 31 (5 selections, 31 lines incl. singles): Choose when you have five picks and want the safety of singles. You accept a higher outlay than a Canadian (26 lines) in exchange for more frequent small returns.
  • Lucky 15 (4 selections, 15 lines incl. singles): Choose when you have four picks and value having a return even if only one wins. Total cost is moderate compared with five-selection perms.
  • Yankee (4 selections, 11 lines, no singles): Choose when you expect at least two winners among four picks and want a lower stake than a Lucky 15. Suits bettors comfortable without singles.

When is a Patent or Heinz a better fit?

Pick a Patent or Heinz when your number of selections or volatility target sits at either end of the spectrum:

  • Patent (3 selections, 7 lines incl. singles): Best when you have exactly three picks and want the singles “safety net.” One winner can return something, while two or three winners scale up through doubles and the treble. Useful for shorter-priced selections where singles still return meaningful amounts.
  • Heinz (6 selections, 57 lines, no singles): Best for six picks when you prefer lower outlay per line than adding singles would require, and you expect multiple winners. It is high-line-count and high-volatility; staking should be sized accordingly. If you need singles with six selections, consider a Lucky 63 (adds singles to a Heinz), but be aware the cost rises to 63 lines win-only.

If your typical strategy is to seek two or more winners at decent prices, no-singles perms (Yankee, Canadian, Heinz) concentrate stake on multiples. If you value frequent smaller returns and tracking performance selection-by-selection, full-cover with singles (Patent, Lucky 15, Lucky 31, Lucky 63) is usually more appropriate.

Does a Lucky 31 amplify variance and risk?

It depends on your comparison point and how you size the stake:

  • Versus five singles only: A Lucky 31 amplifies variance because most stake is in multiples; payouts can be much larger when several win, but you can also lose the whole stake if results go against you.
  • Versus a Canadian (26 lines, no singles): A Lucky 31 generally reduces payout variance per unit stake because singles add more chances of a small return; however, total exposure is higher (31 vs 26 lines). At the same unit stake s, the Lucky 31 risks 31×s versus 26×s.
  • Stake-normalised view: If you fix a total budget B, you can reduce the unit stake for a higher-line perm so that B is constant (e.g., s = B/31 for a Lucky 31, B/26 for a Canadian). With equal budgets, including singles usually smooths returns but shifts some stake away from high-multiple lines, trimming extreme upside.
  • Each-way effect: EW doubles outlay and can reduce variance further because place parts can return without winners, but your cash at risk also doubles.

In short: singles decrease volatility of outcomes but increase the number of lines (and therefore potential outlay) if your unit stake per line is unchanged. Tailor stake size to your bankroll and objective—steady tracking of picks (include singles) versus concentrated shots at higher multiples (exclude singles).

Origins of Lucky permutations in UK betting

“Lucky” bets are UK full-cover multiples with singles included, designed to give more frequent small returns while preserving upside when several selections win. They grew out of high-street multiple betting in the mid–20th century and became popular as simple, named packages covering all combinations for a given number of selections.

  • Patent (3 selections, 7 lines): 3 singles, 3 doubles, 1 treble.
  • Lucky 15 (4 selections, 15 lines): 4 singles, 6 doubles, 4 trebles, 1 fourfold.
  • Lucky 31 (5 selections, 31 lines): 5 singles, 10 doubles, 10 trebles, 5 fourfolds, 1 fivefold.
  • Lucky 63 (6 selections, 63 lines): 6 singles, 15 doubles, 20 trebles, 15 fourfolds, 6 fivefolds, 1 sixfold.

Alongside these, no-singles “full-cover” variants also became staples (e.g., Yankee for four selections, Canadian/Super Yankee for five, Heinz for six). The “Lucky” naming convention helped bettors quickly choose a structure that matched their number of picks and appetite for smaller, more frequent returns (via singles) versus concentrating stake on higher multiples for larger but rarer payouts.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do dead-heats affect a Lucky 31 settlement?

In a dead-heat for a winning position, the affected line’s stake is divided by the number of tied runners and the odds stay the same; for place markets, if H horses tie for the final K payable places, the effective stake is multiplied by K/H. Example (win dead-heat): £2 single at 4/1 in a two-way dead-heat returns (2 ÷ 2) × (4 + 1) = £5. Example (place dead-heat): with 1/5 terms and three tied for the final one payable place, a £2 place stake is treated as £2 × (1 ÷ 3) at the place odds; if the place decimal was 1.80, the return is (2 ÷ 3) × 1.80 = £1.20. In multiples, the dead-heat factor simply scales that leg’s contribution: a two-way win dead-heat multiplies the line’s return by 1/2, while a three-for-two tie on the last paying place multiplies it by 2/3.

How are multiple Rule 4 deductions applied across different legs in a Lucky 31?

Each affected leg is adjusted independently on its profit portion before combining lines. If a leg has decimal d and a deduction r (e.g., 0.20 for 20p), its adjusted decimal is 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − r). Worked example: Leg A 7/2 (decimal 4.50) with 20p Rule 4 becomes 1 + (4.50 − 1) × 0.80 = 3.80; Leg C 3/1 (4.00) with 10p becomes 1 + 3 × 0.90 = 3.70. A double using A and C settles at 3.80 × 3.70 = 14.06 per £1 on that line. Legs without deductions remain unchanged.

In what order should I apply place terms, Rule 4, and dead-heat adjustments?

Apply odds transformations in this sequence on each affected leg: calculate place odds if each-way, then apply Rule 4 to the profit portion, then apply dead-heat reduction to the effective stake/return factor. Micro-case: Win odds 9/2 (decimal 5.50) with 1/5 place terms and 20p Rule 4, dead-heat two-way for first. Place decimal first: 1 + (5.50 − 1) × 0.2 = 1.90. Apply Rule 4 on profit: win decimal becomes 1 + 4.50 × 0.8 = 4.60; place decimal becomes 1 + 0.90 × 0.8 = 1.72. Apply dead-heat factor 1/2 to the relevant part(s): a win dead-heat halves the win-part contribution; a place dead-heat halves the place-part contribution.

What happens to returns if two or more selections are void in a Lucky 31?

Voids reduce the affected legs to decimal 1.00 in every line they appear, so combinations compress to smaller multiples and void singles simply return the unit stake. If two legs are void and three run, all lines effectively settle as if built from the three remaining selections: you’ll receive stake back on the two void singles, reduced multiples wherever a void leg appeared, and full settlement on combinations using only the three runners. If all five are void, each line returns exactly the unit stake and total profit is zero.

Can a Lucky 31 be placed with free bet (stake not returned) funds and how is it settled?

If a line uses a stake-not-returned fund, its first winning leg contributes profit only; the initial stake is excluded from the rollover. For a k-leg winning line with decimals d1…dk and free stake s, return equals s × (d1 − 1) × d2 × … × dk. Example: free £1 on a double 4.00 and 3.00 pays £1 × (4 − 1) × 3 = £9; the equivalent cash stake would have paid £12.

What are the typical UK each-way place terms by race type and field size?

Standard industry terms are commonly: handicaps with 16+ runners pay 1/4 the odds for 1–4, handicaps with 12–15 pay 1/4 for 1–3, races with 8–11 runners pay 1/5 for 1–3, and races with 5–7 runners pay 1/5 for 1–2; below 5 runners, each-way is usually not offered. Some meetings offer enhanced places or special terms; always use the exact fraction and places shown for the market you bet.

Are ‘one winner’ or ‘all correct’ Lucky 31 bonuses included in calculator returns?

Unless a dedicated bonus toggle is provided, headline bonuses are not included because they are brand- and market-specific. As a check, compute base returns first, then apply the published bonus rate to the qualifying scenario, for example adding 10% to total returns on all-correct, or enhancing a single winning single to the specified boosted payout; record the adjustment separately to avoid double counting.

How much can rounding change my final total on a 31-line settlement?

Bookmakers settle to two decimal places and differences arise depending on whether they round per line then sum, or sum raw values then round. The practical impact is small: if 31 lines each sit within ±£0.005 of a penny boundary, the total can differ by up to about £0.31 between the two methods; matching your bookmaker’s per-line rounding policy yields the closest replication.

Can I use a Lucky 31 for football, tennis, or golf markets?

Yes; the structure is sport-agnostic as long as each selection is a fixed-odds market that can settle as win, lose, or void. Each-way and Rule 4 are most common in horse and greyhound racing; in football or tennis you’ll usually use win-only markets, and postponements typically result in void legs per the book’s rules.

How are postponed or abandoned events treated within a Lucky 31?

They settle as void legs if the bookmaker’s time window or rescheduling conditions aren’t met, leaving the affected combinations to reduce accordingly. For example, if a match is abandoned and settled void, every line that includes it treats that leg at decimal 1.00, while singles on that leg return unit stake; consult the sport-specific cut-off (often 24–72 hours) in the event rules before marking the outcome.

What is the maximum and minimum possible return from a win-only Lucky 31?

The minimum is £0 if every running leg loses; the maximum occurs when all five win and equals unit stake multiplied by the sum of all non-empty products of the five decimals. Example with equal decimal 3.00 and £1 unit stake: total return is 5×3 + 10×9 + 10×27 + 5×81 + 1×243 = £1,023, reflecting singles, doubles, trebles, fourfolds, and the fivefold.

How many winners are typically needed to break even on a Lucky 31?

It depends on the average odds. With equal decimal d and exactly m winners, the return is s × [C(m,1)d + C(m,2)d² + … + d^m]; break-even occurs when this meets or exceeds the total outlay. Benchmarks with £1 stakes: at decimal 2.00, three winners return 26 ( 31); at decimal 3.00, two winners return 15 ( 31).

How do I enter non-standard fractions like 100/30 or 11/10 correctly?

Convert the fraction to decimal as 1 + a/b and enter the exact price rather than rounding to a nearby popular fraction. Examples: 100/30 becomes 1 + 100/30 = 4.3333… (decimal 4.3333, often shown as 10/3), and 11/10 becomes 1 + 11/10 = 2.10; using precise decimals preserves accuracy when lines multiply.

Are UK taxes or commissions applied to Lucky 31 returns?

For UK-licensed fixed-odds bookmakers, customer winnings are paid tax-free, so no income or betting tax is deducted from returns. If a platform charges commission (e.g., exchanges), that is separate from a Lucky 31 calculator model; the calculator outputs gross returns based on odds and outcomes.

How are place dead-heats with more ties than remaining places calculated?

Use a proportional reduction: if H horses tie for the final K paying places, the line’s place stake is multiplied by K/H and settled at the place odds. Example: with places 1–4 and five horses tying for fourth, the place factor is 1/5; a £2 place component at decimal 1.80 returns (2 × 1/5) × 1.80 = £0.72 for that leg within any affected line.

Do bookmaker maximum payout limits affect a Lucky 31 result from the calculator?

Yes, if the theoretical return exceeds the bookmaker’s cap for that sport and market, the paid amount will be restricted to the limit even though the calculator shows the full theoretical figure. Because limits vary by brand, event grade, and channel, compare your computed total with the operator’s published maximum for that market before staking.

What if my unit stake is below the bookmaker’s minimum, like 5p or 10p?

The calculator will show mathematical returns for any stake, but operators enforce minimum unit stakes, commonly between £0.05 and £0.10 online. If your input is below the book’s minimum per line, the bet may be rejected or auto-adjusted; verify the platform’s minimums to ensure the stake is executable.

How can I compute expected value (EV) for a Lucky 31 with my own probabilities?

For each line, EV equals unit stake × product of decimals × product of your win probabilities for that line; total EV is the sum across all lines, and EV profit equals EV return minus total outlay. If all five picks share decimal d and win probability p and are independent, EV return simplifies to s × [(1 + d p)^5 − 1]; for d = 2.50 and p = 0.38, EV return ≈ s × [(1 + 0.95)^5 − 1] = s × 27.20, implying an EV loss of about 31s − 27.20s = 3.80s, while at p = 0.42 it becomes s × [(1 + 1.05)^5 − 1] ≈ s × 33.39, an EV profit of 2.39s.

How are negative American odds handled inside a Lucky 31?

Convert to decimal using 1 + 100/|Y| for negative prices and 1 + X/100 for positive prices, then proceed normally. Examples: −120 becomes 1 + 100/120 = 1.8333, +140 becomes 1 + 140/100 = 2.40; a double of −120 and +140 settles at 1.8333 × 2.40 = 4.3999 per £1 on that line.

Does the calculator support partial cash-out of lines mid-event?

No; partial cash-out changes stake and odds dynamically on selected lines, creating a new position that depends on the operator’s cash-out algorithm. To audit such scenarios, record the cashed-out value and treat the remaining position as a new, smaller perm on the unresolved legs.

How do non-runner reductions interact with each-way place markets in the same race?

When a deduction is announced for a race, the same Rule 4 rate typically applies to both the win and the linked place market, reducing only the profit portion on each part. Example: at 1/5 terms with win decimal 6.00 and a 15p deduction, adjusted win decimal is 1 + (6 − 1) × 0.85 = 5.25, adjusted place decimal is 1 + [(6 − 1) × 0.2] × 0.85 = 1 + 1.00 × 0.85 = 1.85.

Is a Lucky 31 the same as a five-pick full-cover parlay in US terms?

Functionally yes; it is the full set of all non-empty combinations from five selections, including singles, settled as separate tickets. The key differences are that UK settlement often includes each-way, Rule 4, and racing-specific dead-heat rules; if you omit each-way and racing adjustments, the math aligns with a five-leg full-cover parlay set.

How can I sanity-check inputs before settling a large Lucky 31?

Check three invariants: void singles must return exactly the unit stake, any line containing a losing leg returns zero for that part, and if all five win at decimal d with unit stake s, the total return must equal s × [5d + 10d² + 10d³ + 5d⁴ + d⁵]. For example, with d = 2.20 and s = £0.50, the all-winners return should be £0.50 × (11 + 48.4 + 106.48 + 118.58 + 51.536) ≈ £167.01.
