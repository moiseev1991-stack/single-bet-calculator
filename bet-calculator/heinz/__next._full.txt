<p>A Heinz is a 57-bet multiple built from 6 selections; a Super Heinz is a 120-bet multiple built from 7 selections. Online calculators automate the combinatorics, convert fractional or decimal odds, and apply rules such as each-way terms, voids, and dead heats. To get accurate UK returns, you set the stake (unit or total), define outcomes for each leg, and the tool sums all winning components across doubles to accumulators.</p>
<h3>Step-by-step: enter odds, stake, each-way terms</h3>
<p>Follow these steps to compute a Heinz or Super Heinz accurately:</p>
<ul><li>Select bet type: Heinz (6 selections, 57 bets) or Super Heinz (7 selections, 120 bets).</li><li>Choose odds format: fractional (e.g., 5/2, 1/1) or decimal (e.g., 3.50, 2.00). The calculator will internally convert as needed.</li></ul>
<p>Enter each selection’s odds and result:</p>
<ul><li>Result options typically include Won, Lost, Void/NR (non-runner), and Dead Heat.</li><li>For football, use markets like Match Result, BTTS, or Over/Under with their quoted odds.</li></ul>
<p>Set stake model:</p>
<ul><li>Unit stake (per bet): the amount staked on each component (e.g., £1 per bet). Total outlay = unit stake × number of bets.</li><li>Total stake: enter the total outlay; the calculator back-solves the implied unit stake.</li></ul>
<p>If Each-Way, toggle each-way and set place terms:</p>
<ul><li>Place fraction (commonly 1/5 or 1/4) and number of places (market dependent).</li><li>Each-way doubles the stake (win part + place part) and settles the place part at the place fraction of odds for placed selections.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Optional settlement adjustments (if provided): Rule 4 deductions (horse racing), dead heat places, or commission (exchanges).</li></ul>

<ul><li>Total stake, total return (including stakes), and profit.</li><li>Breakdown by bet type (doubles, trebles, fourfolds, etc.).</li></ul>

<h2>What is a Heinz bet vs Super Heinz vs Lucky 63?</h2>
<p>These are full-cover multiple bets used across UK horse racing and football. A Heinz combines all doubles up to a sixfold from 6 selections (57 bets). A Super Heinz does the same from 7 selections (120 bets). A Lucky 63 is a Heinz with singles added for 6 selections (63 bets). If placed each-way, the stake is doubled because you are placing both a win and a place version of every component.</p>
<h3>How much does a Super Heinz bet cost?</h3>
<p>The cost is the number of bets multiplied by your unit stake, and doubled if each-way.</p>
<ul><li>Formula (win-only): Cost = 120 × unit stake.</li><li>Formula (each-way): Cost = 120 × unit stake × 2.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Unit stake £0.10: win-only £12.00; each-way £24.00.</li><li>Unit stake £0.25: win-only £30.00; each-way £60.00.</li><li>Unit stake £0.50: win-only £60.00; each-way £120.00.</li><li>Unit stake £1.00: win-only £120.00; each-way £240.00.</li></ul>
<p>Tip: Minimum unit stakes vary by bookmaker and channel (online vs shop). Always check your operator’s staking rules before placing a Super Heinz.</p>
<h3>Heinz or Lucky 63: which is better and when?</h3>
<p>Both use 6 selections. The difference is singles: a Lucky 63 adds 6 singles to the 57 Heinz components, increasing coverage and cost. Choose based on risk tolerance, expected strike-rate, and whether singles provide meaningful protection for your prices.</p>
<p>Choose Lucky 63 when:</p>
<ul><li>You want a return even if only one or two selections win (singles can soften losing sequences).</li><li>Your selections are shorter prices, so singles can meaningfully recoup outlay.</li><li>You value potential bookmaker concessions sometimes associated with “Lucky” bets (e.g., one-winner consolations or all-winners bonuses where offered; terms vary by firm).</li></ul>
<p>Choose Heinz when:</p>
<ul><li>You prefer to concentrate stake on multiples for higher potential upside.</li><li>You’re comfortable with no singles safety net (zero return if fewer than two winners).</li><li>You want to keep cost about 10.5% lower than Lucky 63 at the same unit stake (57 vs 63 bets).</li></ul>
<p>Example intuition: If exactly one of your six selections wins at, say, 2.50 (6/4), a Lucky 63 returns the single (less overall loss), while a Heinz returns nothing. If three or more win—especially at bigger prices—the Heinz’s heavier weighting toward multiples can yield stronger returns for the same unit stake.</p>

<h2>Does the calculator handle each-way, Rule 4, and dead heats?</h2>
<p>Yes. A modern Heinz/Super Heinz calculator applies Rule 4 deductions to the affected legs only, settles dead heats correctly for win and place parts, and prices each-way components using the stated 1/4 or 1/5 place terms. In multiples, adjustments are applied leg-by-leg, and the calculator recomputes every winning component (doubles to accumulators) using the adjusted odds or stakes.</p>
<h3>How are Rule 4 deductions applied in multiples?</h3>
<p>Tattersalls Rule 4 is a deduction to winnings (not to the stake) when a horse is withdrawn after you’ve placed your bet and prices are reformed. In a multiple, the deduction applies only to components that include the affected selection. The standard mechanics are:</p>
<ul><li>Identify the bookmaker-declared deduction (e.g., 10p, 20p, 30p per £1 of winnings).</li><li>Convert each affected selection’s price to decimal (D). For fractional a/b, D = 1 + a/b.</li></ul>
<p>Reduce the winnings part by the deduction r (e.g., r = 0.20 for 20p):</p>
<ul><li>Adjusted decimal D′ = 1 + (D − 1) × (1 − r).</li><li>Example: 3/1 (D = 4.00) with a 20p deduction → D′ = 1 + 3 × 0.8 = 3.40.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Recalculate each component bet that contains the leg using D′. Other legs remain at their original odds.</li><li>If multiple legs incur deductions, apply each leg’s D′ independently; the combined price is the product of all legs’ decimals.</li></ul>
<table><thead><tr><th>Original odds</th><th>Decimal (D)</th><th>Deduction (r)</th><th>Adjusted decimal (D′)</th><th>£1 return after Rule 4</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>1/2</td><td>1.50</td><td>20p</td><td>1 + (0.50 × 0.80) = 1.40</td><td>£1.40</td></tr><tr><td>Evens (1/1)</td><td>2.00</td><td>20p</td><td>1 + (1.00 × 0.80) = 1.80</td><td>£1.80</td></tr><tr><td>5/2</td><td>3.50</td><td>20p</td><td>1 + (2.50 × 0.80) = 3.00</td><td>£3.00</td></tr><tr><td>5/1</td><td>6.00</td><td>20p</td><td>1 + (5.00 × 0.80) = 5.00</td><td>£5.00</td></tr></tbody></table>

<h3>Dead-heat settlement: worked example</h3>
<p>Dead heats split the winning stake across the number of tied runners. In a win market dead heat with N ties, your effective return for that leg is (stake ÷ N) settled at full odds. In decimal terms, you can treat the leg’s effective decimal as D/N for compounding in multiples. For place parts, if the dead heat spans the final available places, settlement is proportionate to the number of places available within the tie.</p>
<table><thead><tr><th>Scenario</th><th>Market</th><th>Inputs</th><th>Settlement rule</th><th>£1 stake return</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>Win DH (2-way)</td><td>Win only</td><td>3/1 (D = 4.00), 2 tied</td><td>Stake halved (1/2) at full odds</td><td>(£1 ÷ 2) × 4.00 = £2.00</td></tr><tr><td>Double with DH first leg</td><td>Win double</td><td>Leg 1: 3/1, 2-way DH → effective D = 4.00/2 = 2.00; Leg 2: 6/4 (D = 2.50)</td><td>Multiply adjusted decimals</td><td>£1 × 2.00 × 2.50 = £5.00</td></tr><tr><td>EW place DH for final place</td><td>Each-way place part</td><td>Terms 1/5, 3 places; 10/1 (Dwin = 11.00); 2 tie for 3rd</td><td>Place odds decimal = 1 + (10 × 1/5) = 3.00; apply 1/2 DH factor</td><td>£1 place stake × 3.00 × 1/2 = £1.50</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In a Heinz/Super Heinz, the calculator applies the dead-heat factor only to components that include the dead-heating selection. Other components are unaffected and are summed into the overall return.</p>
<h3>What do 1/4 and 1/5 each-way terms mean in a Heinz?</h3>
<p>Each-way duplicates your multiple into two parallel bets: a win Heinz and a place Heinz. The place part settles at a fraction of the win odds (commonly 1/4 or 1/5) provided the selection finishes within the stated places. In football, each-way is rare; in horse racing, the fraction and number of places depend on race type and field size published by the bookmaker.</p>
<ul><li>Place fraction: 1/4 or 1/5 of the win odds becomes the place odds.</li><li>Places paid: e.g., 1–2–3 in handicaps with 8–15 runners; extended places may apply in promotions but follow the operator’s stated terms.</li><li>EW stake: total outlay doubles (win + place components for every bet in the Heinz).</li></ul>
<p>Settlement interaction:</p>
<ul><li>Win part requires the selection to win.</li><li>Place part pays if the selection finishes in the paid places; dead heats for place are settled proportionately.</li></ul>
<p>Quick conversion examples (per £1 unit in the place part):</p>
<ul><li>8/1 at 1/5 terms → place decimal = 1 + (8 × 1/5) = 2.60; return £2.60.</li><li>5/1 at 1/4 terms → place decimal = 1 + (5 × 1/4) = 2.25; return £2.25.</li><li>9/4 at 1/5 terms → place decimal = 1 + (2.25 × 1/5) = 1.45; return £1.45.</li></ul>

<h2>The other side of the coin: Is a Heinz bet right for you?</h2>
<p>A Heinz (6 selections, 57 bets) and a Super Heinz (7 selections, 120 bets) spread your stake across many combinations. That diversification does not eliminate risk; it redistributes it. Your bankroll can face sharp swings because most of your total outlay still sits in higher-fold multiples that depend on multiple legs landing. Before choosing a Heinz-style multiple, assess variance, staking, correlation between selections, and whether a different full-cover bet (Yankee, Canadian, Goliath) better matches your risk tolerance and budget.</p>
<h3>The volatility problem: bankroll swings and exposure</h3>
<p>Variance in full-cover bets comes from two forces: the number of high-fold components (which are volatile) and the average price of your selections. Even when you land several winners, returns can disappoint if your winners are short-priced or spread across components that don’t stack together in the same folds.</p>
<p>Exposure math:</p>
<ul><li>Heinz: 57 unit bets; Super Heinz: 120; Goliath: 247. Your total outlay scales linearly with the unit stake.</li><li>At £1 unit stake, a Super Heinz outlay is £120 win-only (or £240 each-way). A downswing of a few losing days can materially dent a small bankroll.</li></ul>
<p>Why swings feel larger than on singles:</p>
<ul><li>Stake weighting skews toward higher folds as selection count increases; these components have higher payoff but lower hit rate.</li><li>Correlation traps: multiple picks tied to the same underlying event (e.g., same match) can all fail together.</li></ul>
<p>Practical controls:</p>
<ul><li>Size the unit stake so total outlay is a small fraction of bankroll (e.g., 0.5–2%).</li><li>Prefer one market per event to keep legs independent.</li><li>Mix prices: include some shorter odds to lift base hit rate, while retaining a few bigger prices for upside.</li><li>Track results by fold size to see where your edge (if any) actually resides.</li></ul>

<h3>Can pushes, voids, or non-runners reduce your payout?</h3>
<p>Yes. In UK fixed-odds multiples, a void or push leg is normally settled at decimal 1.00 for that selection (stake returned for that leg), which reduces the effective fold length. This lowers returns because that leg no longer multiplies your price. Non-runners in horse racing are typically void; late withdrawals can also trigger Rule 4 deductions on other runners. Terms vary slightly by bookmaker; always check house rules.</p>
<p>Accumulator impact:</p>
<ul><li>Treble with one void becomes a double at the remaining two legs’ odds (the void leg contributes ×1.00).</li><li>In a Heinz/Super Heinz, every component including the voided selection prices that leg at 1.00; components without it are unaffected.</li></ul>
<p>Football examples:</p>
<ul><li>Postponed match beyond the bookmaker’s reschedule window → that leg is void; components compress in size.</li><li>Asian handicap push → that leg is a push (void) at 1.00 in multiples; half-wins/half-losses on quarter lines settle proportionately per market rules.</li></ul>
<p>Racing examples:</p>
<ul><li>Declared non-runner → void leg (1.00) in multiples; if another horse is withdrawn, surviving legs may face Rule 4 deductions on their winnings.</li><li>Dead heats are not voids; they are settled by splitting stake or using proportional factors per the market and then compounded in the multiple.</li></ul>
<p>If you believe a settlement is incorrect, you can seek adjudication guidance via [IBAS](https://www.ibas-uk.com). Always review your bookmaker’s published settlement rules for voids, pushes, and non-runners before staking.</p>

<p>Divide the total outlay by the number of bets, then divide by 2 if each-way. Example 1: Heinz win-only with £28.50 total → unit stake = £28.50 ÷ 57 = £0.50. <h3>Why might my calculator total differ by a few pence from the bookmaker settlement?</h3>
<p>UK operators round returns to the nearest penny per component bet, then sum across components; small per-line rounding can create a few pence variance. With 57 Heinz components, the theoretical worst-case rounding drift is under 29p (57 × £0.005), though typical differences are within a few pence; Rule 4 rounding and EW place fractions can add another 1–2p variance per affected line.</p>
<h3>Are Heinz, Super Heinz, and Lucky 63 winnings taxable in the UK in 2026?</h3>
<p>No, UK gambling winnings for individuals are not subject to income tax; operators pay duties instead. Keep personal records for budgeting, but HMRC does not tax recreational betting profits; this applies whether the return comes from a Heinz, Super Heinz, or Lucky 63.</p>
<h3>How do extra places promotions change each-way Heinz settlement?</h3>
<p>Extra places only change whether the place part qualifies; the place fraction (commonly 1/5 or 1/4) remains as stated. If a runner finishes 4th and the bookmaker pays 4 places at 1/5, the place side pays at decimal 1 + (win odds × 1/5); if standard terms were 3 places, the same selection would not pay on the place side. For a 10/1 shot at 1/5, the place decimal is 3.00; one place-side £1 unit returns £3.00 when 4 places are paid but returns £0.00 if only 3 places are paid.</p>
<h3>Do Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) prices change a Heinz return and how can I model it?</h3>
<p>If your bookmaker applies BOG to multiples, any leg where SP exceeds your taken price should be settled at SP, which increases every component containing that leg. Example: early 4/1 (decimal 5.00) drifts to SP 6/1 (7.00); each affected double, treble, etc., multiplies by 7.00 instead of 5.00. A calculator without a BOG toggle will understate these components unless you manually replace that leg’s decimal with the SP.</p>
<h3>What happens to each-way terms and returns if non-runners reduce the field after I’ve placed the bet?</h3>
<p>Settlement usually follows the each-way terms that applied when you placed the bet, while separate Rule 4 deductions may apply to winnings on remaining runners. Some operators alter place terms when fields shrink materially; because policies vary, rely on the bookmaker’s published rules for whether original places are honoured; the calculator can still price the win side with Rule 4 and the place side per the stated places/fraction you input.</p>
<h3>Can I enter American odds (+150, -120) into a Heinz calculator that expects fractional or decimal?</h3>
<p>Yes—convert to decimal first, then enter. Use D = 1 + (American/100) for positive prices and D = 1 + (100/|American|) for negative prices. Examples: +150 converts to 2.50, -120 converts to 1.8333; a -200 converts to 1.50.</p>
<h3>How should I account for exchange commission when estimating a Heinz-style return?</h3>
<p>Apply commission to the winnings part of each leg before compounding. For decimal D, commission c, the adjusted decimal is D′ = 1 + (D − 1) × (1 − c). Example: 2.50 at 5% commission becomes 1 + 1.5 × 0.95 = 2.425; a double with two such legs returns stake × 2.425 × 2.425 = 5.8806. Note that most exchanges do not offer true accas; use this method only for estimation.</p>
<h3>How do you settle a dead-heat on the place part when more horses tie than remaining places?</h3>
<p>Use a proportional factor equal to places available within the tie divided by number tied. If two places remain and three horses tie, each place-side return is multiplied by 2/3. Example: place decimal 3.00 with £1 place unit in a 3‑way tie for the final two places pays £3.00 × 2/3 = £2.00.</p>
<h3>Can I include same‑game selections (for example BTTS and Over 2.5 from one match) in a Heinz?</h3>
<p>Many bookmakers restrict correlated same‑game selections in traditional multiples; they may only be allowed inside a single Bet Builder leg. A Heinz assumes independence across selections; calculators do not police correlation, so using one market per event keeps the model and bookmaker rules aligned.</p>
