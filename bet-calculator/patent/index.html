A Patent is a 7-line multiple across three selections: 3 singles, 3 doubles, and 1 treble. The calculator totals returns and profit for win-only or each-way (EW) settlements, applying built-in rules such as Rule 4 deductions, dead-heats, and void legs. What settlement rules are built in (Rule 4, dead-heat, voids)?

The calculator follows standard UK fixed-odds settlement. You can apply Rule 4 deductions to affected markets, handle dead-heats correctly, and mark void/non-runner legs. These rules flow through all 7 lines of the Patent automatically.

Rule 4 (UK horse racing):

  • Applied when a horse is withdrawn after betting has formed a market.
  • Deduction is a pence-per-£1 factor taken from winnings (profit), not from the returned stake.
  • Multiple withdrawals are applied sequentially to the winnings portion.
  • Place part of EW bets is also subject to Rule 4 when applicable.

Dead-heat:

  • For a win dead-heat between _k_ runners, settle as if stake is divided by _k_ at full odds; the remainder of the stake is lost.
  • For place dead-heats, if _k_ runners share the final paying place across _m_ available places in that tie, the effective stake fraction is _m ÷ k_.

Voids/non-runners:

  • A void single returns the stake.
  • In multiples, a void leg is removed: a double with one void becomes a single on the remaining leg; a treble with one void becomes a double on the other two legs.
  • In a Patent, one void selection redistributes stakes across remaining valid lines; duplicate lines that result (e.g., two AB doubles) are summed.

What is a Patent bet and what does it include?

A Patent is a full-cover multiple across three selections that includes singles. It breaks into seven separate lines: three singles (one on each selection), three doubles (every pair), and one treble (all three). Because it contains singles, a Patent can return money with just one winner, while still giving you the compounding upside of doubles and the treble. Stakes are staked per line (unit stake). For each-way (EW) betting, every line is duplicated into a win part and a place part, doubling the total outlay.

  • Number of selections: 3
  • Number of bets (win-only): 7 (3 singles, 3 doubles, 1 treble)
  • Stake multiplier: 7× unit stake (win-only) or 14× unit stake (each-way)
  • Minimum winners for a return: 1 (because singles are included)

Trixie vs Patent vs Yankee: which suits your strategy?

These are all “full cover” style multiples, but they differ in how much cover they provide and how much stake they require. A Patent adds singles to a three-selection full cover (more protection, higher stake). A Trixie uses only doubles and a treble (no singles, lower stake, higher variance). A Yankee extends to four selections without singles, increasing lines and potential upside at the cost of a larger stake.

Why did the Patent format emerge and replace older permutations?

The Patent gained popularity in UK fixed-odds betting because it adds singles coverage to a three-selection full cover, creating a clearer balance between protection and upside than older no-singles permutations. While Trixies and other perms are still widely available, many recreational bettors prefer Patents because they can get money back with one winner, understand exactly how lines are built, and use straightforward settlement rules across all seven components.

  • Practical protection: Singles reduce the “all or nothing” feel of no-singles perms, smoothing bankroll swings.
  • Straightforward structure: Three selections produce an intuitive 3–3–1 split that is easy to track and audit.
  • Each-way friendly: Duplicates cleanly into win/place parts for racing, keeping settlement consistent.
  • Calculator- and app-ready: Modern tools show line-by-line returns instantly, minimizing disputes.

Trade-offs remain: a Patent requires a higher total stake than a Trixie, and covering singles dilutes peak upside when all legs are big prices. That said, for many bettors the steadier return profile and transparency explain why the Patent became a go-to three-selection format alongside, and often preferred to, older no-singles permutations.

When does a Patent underperform: what’s the strongest counterargument?

The strongest argument against using a Patent is that you “pay for protection” via singles. In scenarios where you expect multiple legs to land (and especially when prices are bigger), a no-singles perm like a Trixie concentrates stake into doubles/treble and can yield higher returns for the same total outlay.

Two-winner scenario (equal total stakes):

  • Setup: A=3.00 (2/1), B=3.50 (5/2), C=2.00 (Evs). A and B win; C loses. Total outlay £14.
  • Patent (£2 unit over 7 lines): returns £34.00 (AB double + A and B singles); profit £20.00.
  • Trixie (£3.50 unit over 4 lines): only AB double wins → £3.50 × (3.00 × 3.50) = £36.75; profit £22.75.

All-three-winners (equal total stakes):

  • Patent (£14 total): returns £106.00.
  • Trixie (£14 total): returns £36.75 + £21.00 + £24.50 + £73.50 = £155.75.

Trade-off summary:

  • Patent smooths variance with singles and can return with one winner.
  • Trixie amplifies upside when 2–3 win but returns nothing on one-winner days.
  • Choose Patent for protection and clearer cash-back potential; choose Trixie when you’re targeting clustered winners and can tolerate dry spells.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate a Patent bet with a free bet (stake not returned)?

Treat each of the seven lines as an SNR bet where the stake is not returned, so a line with decimal odds D returns u × (D − 1) instead of u × D. For a full Patent with all three winners at 3.00, 3.50, and 2.00 and unit stake u = 1 from a free bet, the total is (2 + 2.5 + 1) for the three singles, plus (3×3.5 − 1) + (3×2 − 1) + (3.5×2 − 1) for the three doubles, plus (3×3.5×2 − 1) for the treble, which sums to £46.00. With a cash stake the same slip returns £53.00, so the SNR reduces the overall by exactly £7.00, the sum of the seven line-stakes.

Can I mix a cash stake and a free bet in one Patent?

Yes, split the bet into two Patents with proportional unit stakes and add the results. For example, a £10 total outlay made up of £3 cash and a £7 SNR free bet becomes a cash unit of £3 ÷ 7 ≈ £0.428571 per line and a free-bet unit of £7 ÷ 7 = £1 per line; if all three selections at 3.00, 3.50, and 2.00 win, the cash part returns 0.428571 × £53 ≈ £22.71 and the SNR part returns 1 × £46 = £46.00, for a combined £68.71 before per-line rounding.

How are rounding differences handled on Patent returns?

UK settlement rounds each completed line to the nearest penny, then sums, which can differ from rounding once at the end. The maximum discrepancy versus single total rounding is bounded by the number of active lines, typically up to £0.07 on a win-only Patent or £0.14 on an each-way Patent; this comes from at most one penny of rounding variance per line across 7 or 14 lines.

What odds or hit-rate are needed to break even on a Patent?

With equal decimal odds d and independent per-leg win probability p, the expected return is 3pd + 3p²d² + p³d³ and break-even occurs when this equals 7. At fair odds (p = 1/d), pd = 1 and the expected return is exactly 7, so the expected profit is zero; scenario break-evens are d ≥ 7.00 with exactly one winner and d ≥ about 1.83 with exactly two winners since 2d + d² ≥ 7.

How can I back-solve the unit stake to hit a target return if all three win?

Divide the target by the sum of all seven decimal products. For selections with decimals d1, d2, d3, the unit stake u equals Target ÷ (d1 + d2 + d3 + d1d2 + d1d3 + d2d3 + d1d2d3); for 3.00, 3.50, and 2.00 this denominator is 53, so a £100 target implies u = £100 ÷ 53 ≈ £1.8868 and a total stake of about £13.21.

How do handicap or totals pushes affect a Patent?

A push settles as void, which returns the stake on that line and reduces any multiple containing the leg by one selection. For example, if leg A is a −1.0 handicap push, all A singles return stake only, AB and AC doubles become singles on B and C, and the ABC treble becomes the BC double; you should mark the pushed leg as Void so the calculator cascades the reduction through all seven lines.

Do place-term changes after non-runners affect an each-way Patent, and how should I enter them?

Yes, revised place terms change both the number of places paid and sometimes the place fraction, which can turn a previously paid place into a loser. A common case is 8 runners at 1/5 odds 3 places becoming 7 runners at 1/5 odds 2 places; a selection finishing 3rd would now lose on the place part, so enter the final official place terms and the finishing position to settle each place line correctly.

Can I apply commission or boosts to a Patent, and how do I model them?

Apply boosts by increasing the affected leg’s decimal odds or the final multiple’s decimal product by the stated percentage, while commission reduces winnings only. For three legs each at 3.00 with unit 1, the cash return is £63 and winnings are £56; a 5% commission gives £56 × 0.95 = £53.20 in winnings and a £60.20 total return, whereas a 10% treble-only boost multiplies the treble from 27.00 to 29.70, lifting the total from £63.00 to £65.70.

Does a Patent support banker selections or variable stakes per line?

No, a true Patent uses equal unit stakes across 3 singles, 3 doubles, and 1 treble and has no banker concept. If you want different exposure, place three separate bets instead: one on singles, one on doubles, and one on the treble with the individual stakes you want; for instance, £2 on each single, £1 on each double, and £0.50 on the treble mimics a weighted permutation rather than a Patent.

How do Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) and odds boosts change Patent settlement?

BOG replaces the taken price with the higher SP for winning racing legs on both singles and multiples that include that leg, increasing every affected line by the same multiplier. If a leg taken at 5.00 returns at 6.50 SP, its effective decimal becomes 6.50, so any line containing it rises by 30% for that leg’s factor; for example, a double AB increases from 5.00×B to 6.50×B, and the treble ABC increases by the same 30% on that leg’s contribution.

How do multiple Rule 4 deductions apply across a Patent?

Convert each affected leg’s decimal D to an adjusted D′ = 1 + (D − 1) × Π(1 − r_i), where r_i are the sequential deductions, then use D′ everywhere that leg appears. A 10p and 15p Rule 4 on a 5.00 leg gives D′ = 1 + 4 × 0.90 × 0.85 = 4.06; paired with an unaffected 3.50 leg, the double’s decimal becomes 4.06 × 3.50 = 14.21, so a £1 unit returns £14.21 on that line.

How is a place dead-heat settled in an each-way Patent with numbers?

Divide the place stake by the number of tied runners across the last paying place, then settle at full place odds. With 1/5 place terms and 8 runners paying 3 places, if three horses dead-heat for third, the effective stake fraction is 1 ÷ 3; a £10 each-way at 6/1 has a £10 place part settled at 6/5 decimal 2.20, so the place return is (£10 × 1/3) × 2.20 = £7.33, while the remaining two-thirds of the place stake is lost.

Can a calculator replicate cash-out values on a Patent?

No, cash out uses a bookmaker’s proprietary model with margin, volatility, and hedging inputs, so it cannot be derived from public odds alone. If you cashed out the entire Patent, use the bookmaker’s cash-out figure as the final result; if you partially cashed out a component, treat the cashed line as a fixed settled return and remove its future correlation, because remaining lines no longer follow standard multiple math.

Does currency or odds precision change Patent results?

The math is currency-agnostic; only rounding rules change the pennies. Using two-decimal rounding per line, switching from pounds to euros or setting decimal odds to three or four places alters only the last cent or penny and typically shifts a win-only Patent by no more than ±0.07 in the settlement currency due to per-line rounding accumulation.
