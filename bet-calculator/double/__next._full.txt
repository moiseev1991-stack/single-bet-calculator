1f:T870a,<h2>How do you calculate a Double bet return?</h2>
<p>A Double combines two selections into one bet. The return from Selection 1 (including the original stake) rolls on to Selection 2. If either leg loses, the overall return is £0. Using decimal odds, the total return is Stake × (Decimal 1) × (Decimal 2). Using fractional odds A/B, first convert to decimal as 1 + A/B and then multiply through. Voids/pushes act as odds of 1.00 for that leg, effectively turning the Double into a Single on the other leg.</p>
<h3>Stake, odds and settlement: what inputs does the calculator need?</h3>
<p>To price a Double accurately, the calculator needs the monetary stake, odds for both legs, and the settlement status of each selection. Optional fields account for special settlement cases (e.g., dead-heats). Here is the minimum and recommended input set:</p>
<ul><li>Stake: total amount staked on the Double (e.g., £10).</li><li>Odds format: choose fractional (e.g., 5/4), decimal (e.g., 2.25), or American (e.g., +125).</li><li>Selection 1 odds and status: price plus settlement outcome (Won, Lost, Void/Push, Dead Heat with number of ties).</li><li>Selection 2 odds and status: price plus settlement outcome (Won, Lost, Void/Push, Dead Heat with number of ties).</li></ul>
<p>Settlement conventions: apply standard UK fixed-odds rules:</p>
<ul><li>Won: multiply by full odds (converted to decimal, includes stake).</li><li>Lost: overall return becomes £0.</li><li>Void/Push/Non-runner: treat as decimal 1.00 for that leg.</li><li>Dead Heat (n-way tie on the win market): the effective factor for that leg is decimal odds divided by n.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Convert Selection 1 odds to decimal (if needed).</li></ul>
<p>Apply settlement for Selection 1:</p>
<ul><li>Won: Stage 1 return = Stake × Decimal 1.</li><li>Void/Push: Stage 1 return = Stake × 1.00.</li><li>Dead Heat (n-way): Stage 1 return = Stake × (Decimal 1 ÷ n).</li><li>Lost: Final return = £0 (stop).</li></ul>
<ul><li>Convert Selection 2 odds to decimal (if needed).</li></ul>
<p>Apply settlement for Selection 2 to Stage 1 return:</p>
<ul><li>Won: Final return = Stage 1 return × Decimal 2.</li><li>Void/Push: Final return = Stage 1 return × 1.00.</li><li>Dead Heat (n-way): Final return = Stage 1 return × (Decimal 2 ÷ n).</li><li>Lost: Final return = £0.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Profit = Final return − Stake.</li></ul>

<h3>Fractional, decimal and American: how does format affect returns?</h3>
<p>Returns are identical once odds are converted to decimal. Conversions: Fractional A/B → Decimal = 1 + A/B. American +M → Decimal = 1 + (M/100). American −M → Decimal = 1 + (100/M). The Double return is Stake × Decimal 1 × Decimal 2 regardless of the original formats.</p>

<h2>Which sports and markets can you use a Double on?</h2>
<p>You can place a Double on most fixed-odds sports markets offered by UK bookmakers, including football, horse racing, and basketball (NBA). A Double multiplies the decimal prices of both legs. If either leg loses, the overall return is £0; if a leg is void/push, it settles at 1.00 for that leg. Markets with push potential (e.g., Asian handicaps, spreads, whole-number totals) can reduce variance in Doubles because a push keeps the bet alive on the other leg.</p>
<h3>Football Double: which markets work best?</h3>
<p>For football, aim for markets with clearly defined settlement and reasonable prices so the Double’s combined price remains realistic. Use independent matches to avoid related-contingency restrictions unless using a book’s same-game builder that prices correlation explicitly.</p>
<ul><li>Match Result (1X2): simple win/draw/win market; no push condition. Doubles here can be high variance due to the draw outcome.</li><li>Draw No Bet (DNB): reduces variance; a draw pushes (factor 1.00) and rolls the stake to the other leg only. Price is shorter than 1X2.</li><li>Double Chance (e.g., Home/Draw): highest hit rate but much shorter odds; useful as a stabiliser leg.</li><li>Both Teams To Score (BTTS): binary outcome with no push; combines well with result-based markets from different matches.</li></ul>
<p>Over/Under Goals:</p>
<ul><li>Whole-number lines (e.g., Over 2.0) can push if the exact total lands, acting as 1.00.</li><li>Half-lines (e.g., Over 2.5) avoid push but increase variance.</li><li>Asian goal lines (e.g., Over 2.25) can split stakes and partially push.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Asian Handicap: push possible on whole numbers (e.g., -1.0); quarter lines split stakes and can create partial wins/pushes.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Example (independent matches): £10 Double on 1X2 @ 2.10 and BTTS @ 1.80 → Return = £10 × 2.10 × 1.80 = £37.80; Profit = £27.80.</li><li>Example with push safety: £10 Double on DNB @ 1.75 and Over 2.0 @ 1.95; if Over 2.0 pushes, Return = £10 × 1.75 × 1.00 = £17.50; Profit = £7.50.</li></ul>
<h3>Horse racing Double and Each Way: how are place terms applied?</h3>
<p>An Each Way (E/W) Double is two bets: a Win Double and a Place Double of equal stakes. The win part requires both horses to win. The place part multiplies using “place odds” derived from race place terms (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4 the win odds) and pays if each horse places (a win also counts as a place). A non-runner is a void leg (factor 1.00). Dead-heats reduce the effective odds by the number of tied horses at the relevant market (win or place).</p>
<table><thead><tr><th>Race type</th><th>Runners</th><th>Places paid</th><th>Place fraction</th></tr></thead><tbody><tr><td>All (flat & jumps)</td><td>2–4</td><td>Win Only</td><td>—</td></tr><tr><td>All (flat & jumps)</td><td>5–7</td><td>1st–2nd</td><td>1/4</td></tr><tr><td>Non-handicap</td><td>8+</td><td>1st–3rd</td><td>1/5</td></tr><tr><td>Handicap</td><td>8–11</td><td>1st–3rd</td><td>1/5</td></tr><tr><td>Handicap</td><td>12–15</td><td>1st–3rd</td><td>1/4</td></tr><tr><td>Handicap</td><td>16+</td><td>1st–4th</td><td>1/4</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Place odds are computed from fractional win odds A/B using the fraction f (e.g., 1/5): place fractional odds = (A × f) / B; place decimal = 1 + (A/B × f). Example: 10/1 at 1/5 → place fractional 2/1 (decimal 3.00). In an E/W Double, returns are calculated separately and then added.</p>
<p>Example (Stake £5 E/W; total £10): Horse 1 at 10/1 (f=1/5), Horse 2 at 3/1 (f=1/5).</p>
<ul><li>Both WIN: Win Double = £5 × 11.00 × 4.00 = £220.00; Place Double = £5 × 3.00 × 1.60 = £24.00; Total Return = £244.00; Profit = £234.00.</li><li>H1 PLACES only, H2 WINS: Win Double = £0; Place Double = £5 × 3.00 × 1.60 = £24.00; Profit = £24.00 − £10.00 = £14.00.</li><li>Any LOSER: that leg settles at 0.00 for the relevant part (win or place); a non-runner is 1.00 for that leg.</li></ul>
<h3>NBA Double: moneyline, spread or totals?</h3>
<p>For NBA Doubles, choose markets based on your tolerance for push risk and price. UK books usually settle NBA moneyline “including overtime,” whereas spreads and totals can push on whole numbers. Player props may void if the player does not take the court (bookmaker rules vary), which would act as 1.00 for that leg in a Double.</p>
<ul><li>Moneyline (incl. OT): no push; higher prices on underdogs, shorter on favourites. Good anchor leg when confident in the matchup.</li><li>Spread (handicap): pushes on whole numbers (e.g., -3.0) settle 1.00; half-point lines (e.g., -2.5) avoid pushes but change the price.</li><li>Totals (game or team): pushes on whole numbers; quarter/half markets behave the same way.</li><li>Player props: check house rules; commonly void if the player records no minutes, acting as 1.00 in the Double.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Example (independent games): £20 Double on Moneyline @ 1.62 and Over 224.5 @ 1.91 → Return = £20 × 1.62 × 1.91 = £61.88; Profit = £41.88.</li><li>Spread push scenario: £20 Double on -3.0 @ 1.91 (push) and ML @ 1.80 (win) → Return = £20 × 1.00 × 1.80 = £36.00; Profit = £16.00.</li></ul>
<h2>How many Doubles are in 3 or 4 selections, and how are they settled?</h2>
<p>With n independent selections, the number of distinct Doubles is the combination C(n, 2) = n × (n − 1) ÷ 2. Each Double is a separate bet that pairs two selections; settlement is independent across pairs. Returns from winning pairs are added together. A losing leg settles that Double at £0; a void/push/non‑runner leg settles at factor 1.00 for that Double, effectively turning it into a Single on the other leg at its original odds.</p>
<h3>Doubles with 3 selections: how are the 3 pairings settled?</h3>
<p>With three selections A, B, C, you have C(3, 2) = 3 separate Doubles: AB, AC, BC. You can stake a unit amount per Double (e.g., £2 per Double; total stake £6) or specify a total stake to be split evenly across the three bets. Each Double settles on its own outcomes.</p>
<p>Pairings:</p>
<ul><li>AB (pairs Selection A with Selection B)</li><li>AC (pairs Selection A with Selection C)</li><li>BC (pairs Selection B with Selection C)</li></ul>
<p>Key properties:</p>
<ul><li>If exactly two of the three selections win, you’ll have exactly one winning Double (the pair of winners); the other two include the loser and return £0.</li><li>If all three win, all three Doubles win and returns are summed.</li><li>If a selection is void/push, any Double containing it reduces to a Single on the other leg at its original odds.</li></ul>
<p>Example (unit stake £5 per Double; total £15): A @ 2.10, B @ 1.80, C @ 3.00 (decimal). Outcomes:</p>
<ul><li>A and C win; B loses → Winning Double: AC only → Return = £5 × 2.10 × 3.00 = £31.50; Profit = £31.50 − £15 = £16.50.</li><li>If all three win → AB = £5 × 2.10 × 1.80 = £18.90; AC = £31.50; BC = £5 × 1.80 × 3.00 = £27.00; Total Return = £77.40; Profit = £62.40.</li></ul>
<h3>Doubles with 4 selections: what about voids and non‑runners?</h3>
<p>With four selections A, B, C, D you have C(4, 2) = 6 Doubles: AB, AC, AD, BC, BD, CD. Each selection appears in (n − 1) = 3 Doubles. Voids/pushes/non‑runners (NR) settle at factor 1.00 for any Double they touch, converting that Double to a Single on the remaining leg. If both legs of a Double are void/push/NR, that Double returns the unit stake. In UK horse racing, if a runner is withdrawn and the race goes ahead, a Tattersalls Rule 4 deduction (expressed as pence per £1) may reduce the winnings part on affected legs; the deduction applies per affected leg and does not reduce the stake portion.</p>
<p>Impact of voids/non‑runners:</p>
<ul><li>One NR (e.g., D): AD, BD, CD become Singles on A, B, C respectively; AB, AC, BC remain Doubles.</li><li>Two NRs (e.g., C and D): AC, AD, BC, BD become Singles on A or B; CD is void (stake returned); AB remains a Double.</li><li>Pushes (e.g., spreads/totals landing on the line) behave the same as voids for that leg (factor 1.00).</li></ul>
<ul><li>Rule 4 illustration (winnings-only deduction): If a winning leg has decimal price D and a 15p/£1 deduction applies, the adjusted single-leg return is Stake × [1 + 0.85 × (D − 1)]. In a Double, apply the adjusted factor for that leg before multiplying by the other leg.</li></ul>
<p>Example (unit stake £5 per Double; total £30): A @ 2.20, B @ 1.73, C @ 2.60, D @ 3.20. If D is NR:</p>
<ul><li>AD → Single on A: £5 × 2.20 = £11.00 (if A wins).</li><li>BD → Single on B: £5 × 1.73 = £8.65 (if B wins).</li><li>CD → Single on C: £5 × 2.60 = £13.00 (if C wins).</li><li>AB, AC, BC remain full Doubles and settle as usual.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Rule 4 example within a Double: Suppose AB wins but A’s race has a 10p/£1 deduction and A @ 3.50 (decimal). Adjusted A factor = 1 + 0.90 × (3.50 − 1) = 1 + 0.90 × 2.50 = 3.25. If B @ 2.00, unit £5 → Return = £5 × 3.25 × 2.00 = £32.50.</li></ul>
<h3>Cross doubles vs standard doubles: what changes in payout?</h3>
<p>Standard Doubles perm: choose any two from a single list of selections, giving C(n, 2) bets. Cross Doubles: take at least two separate events/groups and back multiple selections in each, then form all pairings with exactly one selection from each group. If Group 1 has g₁ selections and Group 2 has g₂, you have g₁ × g₂ Cross Doubles. Settlement and per‑bet payouts use the same multiplication rule; the difference is coverage and the number of bets.</p>
<p>When to use Cross Doubles:</p>
<ul><li>Horse racing across two races: multiple picks in Race 1 and Race 2; only one horse can win per race, so a Double pays if you find the winner in each race.</li><li>Football across two matches: cover several outcomes in Match A and Match B, forming all cross-pairings across matches.</li></ul>
<p>Counting bets and stakes:</p>
<ul><li>Standard Doubles with n selections: C(n, 2) bets; each selection appears in (n − 1) Doubles.</li><li>Cross Doubles with groups g₁ and g₂: g₁ × g₂ bets; each selection in Group 1 appears in g₂ Doubles, and vice versa.</li></ul>
<p>Example (Cross Doubles, unit £2): Race 1 picks R1a @ 4.00, R1b @ 6.00; Race 2 picks R2a @ 3.00, R2b @ 5.00 → 2 × 2 = 4 Doubles:</p>
<ul><li>R1a–R2a, R1a–R2b, R1b–R2a, R1b–R2b.</li><li>If winners are R1b (6.00) and R2a (3.00), only R1b–R2a returns: £2 × 6.00 × 3.00 = £36.00; Total stake £8; Profit £28.00.</li></ul>

<h2>Double vs Treble and related multiples: which calculator should you use?</h2>
<p>A Double multiplies the prices of two independent selections; a Treble multiplies three. Both bets settle at £0 if any leg loses; a void/push/non‑runner (NR) acts as a factor of 1.00 for that leg. Value comes from finding edges on individual legs; compounding increases both potential return and variance. Use a Double calculator for exactly two legs, and switch to a Treble (or Acca) calculator for three or more.</p>
<h3>Double vs Treble: which offers better value?</h3>
<p>Neither is “better” in isolation—the expected value (EV) depends on the quality of each leg. With decimal odds d and true per‑leg win probability p (legs independent), EV on a £1 stake is p²·d² − 1 for a Double and p³·d³ − 1 for a Treble. For equal‑priced legs, the per‑leg break‑even probability is p = 1/d for both Double and Treble; the difference is variance and hit rate. Trebles deliver higher upside when all edges land together, but they cash less often. Doubles pay more frequently and smooth bankroll swings.</p>

If any leg has a high push probability (e.g., whole‑number spread/total), your realized variance drops because pushes reduce the multiplier to 1.00 for that leg.</p>
<h3>Doubles and Trebles calculator: when to switch tools?</h3>
<p>Use a Double calculator for two selections, including settlement cases like voids, dead‑heats, and Rule 4 deductions (when applicable in horse racing). Move to a Treble/Acca calculator when you have three or more legs, when you’re mixing markets with partial pushes (Asian lines), or when you’re handling system bets that include a Treble component.</p>
<ul><li>Exactly two legs (any sports/markets) → Double calculator handles win/loss/push and dead‑heat logic.</li><li>Exactly three legs (pure accumulator) → Treble calculator for win/loss/push and per‑leg adjustments.</li></ul>
<p>System bets containing Doubles and a Treble:</p>
<ul><li>Trixie (3 selections): 4 bets = 3 Doubles + 1 Treble.</li><li>Patent (3 selections): 7 bets = 3 Singles + 3 Doubles + 1 Treble.</li><li>Yankee (4 selections): 11 bets = 6 Doubles + 4 Trebles + 1 Fourfold.</li></ul>
<p>In these cases, use a system/multiples calculator that itemises each component (including the Treble) and totals the return.</p>
<ul><li>Each Way (E/W) across three legs (rare, racing only) → dedicated E/W Acca calculator to separate Win and Place parts per leg before compounding.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Rule of thumb: if your slip shows any Treble or higher‑fold, switch from a Double‑only tool to a Treble/Acca or System calculator to ensure full settlement accuracy.</li></ul>
<h3>2 singles and a Double: how does the combined bet pay out?</h3>
<p>“2 Singles and a Double” is three bets on two selections A and B: Single A, Single B, and the Double AB (often staked at the same unit). It’s a simple way to keep a return if one leg wins while still compounding upside if both win.</p>
<ul><li>Stake structure: total stake = 3 × unit stake (one per Single and one for the Double).</li></ul>
<p>Settlement:</p>
<ul><li>Both win → both Singles win + the Double wins (all three returns are added).</li><li>Exactly one wins → only the winning Single returns; the other Single and the Double lose.</li><li>Void/push on one leg → affected Single returns stake; the Double becomes a Single on the other leg at its odds.</li><li>Both void/push → all three stakes returned.</li></ul>
<p>Worked example (unit £10; total £30): A @ 2.10, B @ 1.80</p>
<ul><li>Both win: Single A = £10 × 2.10 = £21.00; Single B = £10 × 1.80 = £18.00; Double AB = £10 × 2.10 × 1.80 = £37.80; Total return = £76.80; Profit = £46.80.</li><li>Only A wins: Return = £21.00; Profit = £21.00 − £30.00 = −£9.00.</li><li>Only B wins: Return = £18.00; Profit = £18.00 − £30.00 = −£12.00.</li><li>B void, A wins: Single A = £21.00; Single B stake returned £10; Double becomes Single on A = £21.00; Total return = £52.00; Profit = £22.00.</li></ul>
<h2>Expert corner: evolution, app options and the strongest counterargument</h2>
<p>Double bet calculators have moved from manual arithmetic to instant, on-device tools that handle mixed odds formats, dead-heats, pushes, and Each Way logic. Today, you can calculate returns offline with a native app or a Progressive Web App (PWA), and verify settlement edge cases that once required rulebooks. Below we trace that evolution, explain offline options, and examine a key risk: whether slick calculators can foster overconfidence.</p>
<h3>From pen-and-paper to live tools: how did calculators evolve?</h3>
<p>Before online tools, bettors converted fractional prices by hand, multiplied leg-by-leg, and adjusted for voids or dead-heats using shop rules. Early web pages in the late 1990s–2000s introduced simple server-side calculators. By the 2010s, client-side JavaScript made instant, interactive calculators standard. Modern PWAs, documented on [MDN](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/Progressive_web_apps), use service workers ([Service Worker API](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/API/Service_Worker_API)) to enable offline use and background updates.</p>
<ul><li>Manual era: paper coupons and pocket calculators; fractional-to-decimal conversion and multiplication done by hand; Rule 4 deductions applied from shop charts in horse racing.</li><li>Early web: basic forms posted to a server; returned totals but offered little help for special cases.</li><li>Client-side calculators: instant conversion (fractional/decimal/American), push logic for handicaps/totals, and dead-heat handling.</li><li>PWAs and native apps: offline-first arithmetic, installable experiences, and support for Each Way doubles, cross-doubles, and system bets.</li></ul>
<p>What modern tools compute precisely:</p>
<ul><li>Mixed-odds multiplication: decimal = Stake × d₁ × d₂ (void/push = 1.00 factor).</li><li>Dead-heats: effective factor per tied position (e.g., decimal ÷ n for a 2-way dead-heat on the win market).</li><li>Each Way place terms: apply 1/5 or 1/4 fractions per race type and field size; multiply place parts separately.</li><li>Rule 4 (horse racing): winnings-only deduction per affected leg before compounding with the other leg.</li></ul>

<h3>Devil’s advocate: do calculators encourage overconfidence or risk?</h3>
<p>They can, if users mistake precision for edge. Calculators make compounding returns look effortless, which can mask the steep drop in combined hit rate as legs are added. Even with “fair” prices, a Double or Treble has a lower chance of success than a Single; without a genuine edge in each leg, the expected value is negative after the margin.</p>
<p>Main risks:</p>
<ul><li>Illusion of control: clean outputs may nudge larger stakes without improving selection quality.</li><li>Compounding volatility: a small error per leg is magnified across multiple legs.</li><li>Misread pushes: assuming a push “saves” a bet, while it still erodes upside compared to a full win.</li></ul>
<p>Built-in guardrails that help:</p>
<ul><li>Show combined implied probability and break-even per leg alongside returns.</li><li>Flag pushes and dead-heats clearly, separating return and profit.</li><li>Offer stake sizing presets (e.g., fixed unit, percentage) without suggesting amounts.</li><li>Link to independent safer gambling resources such as the [UK Gambling Commission](https://www.gamblingcommission.gov.uk/).</li></ul>
<p>Example of context that tempers overconfidence:</p>
<ul><li>Two legs at 1.80 decimal each → combined 3.24 → implied hit rate ≈ 30.86%.</li><li>Three legs at 1.80 → combined 5.832 → implied hit rate ≈ 17.15%.</li><li>A calculator that shows these probabilities next to returns makes risk transparent.</li></ul>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>How do I calculate a Double with a free bet (stake not returned)?</h3>
<p>Treat the free bet as removing the stake from the final return: Return = Free‑bet stake × (d1 × d2 − 1), where d1 and d2 are decimal odds after any settlements. Example: £10 free bet at 2.50 and 1.80 gives combined 4.50, return = £10 × (4.50 − 1) = £35.00; if one leg is void so combined becomes 2.50, return = £10 × (2.50 − 1) = £15.00; if both legs void so combined is 1.00, return = £0.00.</p>
<h3>Does the order of selections change a Double’s payout or settlement?</h3>
<p>No, order does not change the payout because multiplication is commutative and the same factors apply. If Leg 1 settles first the stage return is Stake × d1 and then multiplied by d2, if Leg 2 settles first it is Stake × d2 then multiplied by d1; for Stake £20 with 1.80 and 2.10 both ways give £20 × 1.80 × 2.10 = £75.60.</p>
<h3>How are Asian handicaps and quarter lines applied inside a Double?</h3>
<p>Split the leg into equal sub‑stakes per quarter component, apply each outcome factor, then recombine before multiplying by the other leg. For −0.25, a draw gives half push and half lose so the effective factor is 0.50; with £20 on −0.25 @ 1.95 and Over 2.5 @ 1.90 that draws, the combined factor is 0.50 × 1.90 = 0.95 and return is £19.00, while for −0.75 winning by exactly 1 the factor is 0.5 × 1.95 + 0.5 × 1.00 = 1.475 which then multiplies the other leg.</p>
<h3>What happens to a Double if one or both legs are void or push?</h3>
<p>One void or push turns the Double into a Single on the other leg with factor 1.00 for the voided leg; two voids or pushes return the stake. Example: £15 on 2.20 and 1.80 where the first leg is void and the second wins returns £15 × 1.00 × 1.80 = £27.00, while both void return £15.00.</p>
<h3>How are Each Way Doubles settled when one leg wins and the other only places or dead‑heats?</h3>
<p>An E/W Double is two equal stakes, a Win Double and a Place Double; the win part pays only if both win and the place part uses place odds per race terms, with dead‑heats dividing the affected decimal by the tie count. Example: £10 E/W total £20 on A 8/1 (place fraction 1/5 so place decimal 2.60) and B 3/1 (place decimal 1.60), if A places in a two‑way dead‑heat and B wins then Win Double is £0.00 and Place Double returns £10 × (2.60 ÷ 2) × 1.60 = £20.80, a net profit of £0.80.</p>
<h3>What is a Rule 4 deduction and how does it change a Double return?</h3>
<p>Rule 4 reduces only the winnings part on the affected horse by a fixed pence‑per‑pound rate, so the adjusted decimal is 1 + (1 − r) × (d − 1) where r is the deduction rate. Example: £10 Double with A 3.50 subject to 15p/£ (r = 0.15) and B 2.20 gives adjusted A = 1 + 0.85 × 2.50 = 3.125 and combined 3.125 × 2.20 = 6.875 so return £68.75, versus £77.00 without the deduction, a reduction of £8.25.</p>
<h3>Are Double winnings taxed in the UK in 2026?</h3>
<p>No, UK bettors pay 0% tax on gambling winnings; betting duty is levied on operators, not customers. Since 2001, UK customers have not paid income or capital gains tax on bet winnings, so a calculator’s return figure is the amount you receive before any optional fees like payment withdrawals.</p>
<h3>How do UK bookmakers round Double returns and deductions?</h3>
<p>Returns are settled to two decimal places to the nearest penny (£0.01), with intermediate steps sometimes rounded at leg level per house rules. For example, £10 × 1.9091 × 2.10 = £40.091 is paid as £40.09; if a bookmaker rounds each leg first to four decimals and then multiplies, the total can differ by 1p, so always check stated rounding rules.</p>
<h3>What is a fair cash‑out estimate for a Double and how can I compute it?</h3>
<p>Multiply the current combined decimal of all unresolved parts by the live stage stake; if one leg has already won, use Stage return × remaining leg decimal. Example: £20 Double after the first leg wins at 1.80 gives a stage return of £36.00; if the remaining leg is trading at 1.91, a fair, fee‑free estimate is £36.00 × 1.91 = £68.76, noting that bookmaker offers may discount this for margin and risk.</p>
<h3>Can I place a Double on correlated outcomes from the same match?</h3>
<p>Standard Doubles on related contingencies are usually restricted because the events are not independent; only a same‑game builder or parlay priced for correlation is typically allowed. For example, Team A to win and Over 2.5 goals in the same match is often blocked as a standard Double but may be offered as a priced same‑game parlay where the combined decimal already embeds correlation.</p>
<h3>How many Doubles are in n selections and how should I split a total stake?</h3>
<p>The count is n × (n − 1) ÷ 2 and an even split per Double is Total stake ÷ count. Example: with n = 5 there are 10 Doubles, so a £50 total becomes £5 per Double; with n = 8 there are 28 Doubles, so a £56 total becomes £2 per Double.</p>
<h3>Do in‑play or live odds change how a Double is calculated?</h3>
<p>No, the formula is unchanged; you still multiply the live decimal factors after applying any pushes or voids. The only practical differences are that legs may settle at different times and push‑prone markets like whole‑number spreads or totals still apply a factor of 1.00, so £25 × 1.75 (live) × 1.00 on a push returns £43.75.</p>
<h3>How do odds boosts and accumulator bonuses apply to a Double?</h3>
<p>A boost on a leg replaces that leg’s decimal before multiplication, while a Double bonus usually adds a percentage to the winnings portion only. Example: £20 at 2.10 and 1.80 with a 10% winnings bonus returns £75.60 + 10% of £55.60 = £75.60 + £5.56 = £81.16; boosting the 2.10 leg to 2.30 increases the return by £20 × (2.30 − 2.10) × 1.80 = £7.20.</p>
