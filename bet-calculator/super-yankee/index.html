Can I factor in non-runners, dead-heats and each-way terms?

Yes. The calculator models UK settlement accurately:

  • **Non‑runner/Void:** Any line containing a non‑runner is settled with that leg at 1.00 (void). Doubles with one non‑runner become singles; trebles become doubles; fourfolds become trebles; the fivefold becomes a fourfold.
  • **Dead‑heat (win part):** Stake on the tied selection is divided by the number of tied runners. In multiples, you can treat the affected leg’s decimal factor as divided by the number of ties (for a two‑way dead‑heat, divide by 2).
  • **Each‑way terms (place part):** Place odds = original fractional odds × place fraction (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4). Winners count as placed for the place part.
  • **Dead‑heat (place part):** If runners tie for the final place(s), the place return is proportionally reduced (e.g., two‑way dead‑heat for one remaining place → half returns on the place part).

What is a Super Yankee (Canadian) bet and how does it work?

A Super Yankee, also called a Canadian, is a full-coverage multiple built from five selections that excludes singles. It spreads your stake across all doubles, trebles, fourfolds and the fivefold so you are not relying on one accumulator to land. Returns are calculated line by line: each winning line multiplies the decimal odds of its legs, and void legs (e.g., non-runners) settle at 1.00 within any affected line. If placed each-way, the bet duplicates into win and place parts with the place odds derived from the stated each-way terms (for example, 1/5 or 1/4 the win odds), and both parts settle independently.

How many bets are in a Super Yankee?

A Super Yankee consists of 26 separate bets formed from five selections. The breakdown follows combinations of selections taken 2, 3, 4 and 5 at a time. Each stake unit is placed on every line; a total stake is simply the unit stake multiplied by the number of lines.

  • 10 doubles: C(5,2) = 10
  • 10 trebles: C(5,3) = 10
  • 5 fourfolds: C(5,4) = 5
  • 1 fivefold: C(5,5) = 1
  • Total lines: 10 + 10 + 5 + 1 = 26
  • Win-only stake example: £1 per line = £26 total stake.
  • Each-way example: £1 win + £1 place per line = 52 lines, £52 total stake.
  • Minimum winners for any return: 2 winners (to land at least one double). Profit depends on prices.

Football vs horse racing: settlement nuances

Settlement rules vary by sport and market. The core multiple mechanics are the same, but how each leg is graded can differ. Below are common UK-facing conventions; always check your bookmaker’s specific rules before placing a bet.

**Horse racing**

  • Each-way widely available; standard place terms depend on race type and field size. Typical industry terms: non-handicaps with 8+ runners pay 1/5 odds 1–2–3 places; 5–7 runners pay 1/4 odds 1–2; fewer than 5 runners win-only. Handicaps typically pay 1/5 odds 1–2–3 for 8–11 runners, 1/4 odds 1–2–3 for 12–15, and 1/4 odds 1–2–3–4 for 16+.
  • Rule 4 deductions apply to winning returns when a horse is withdrawn after final declarations and the market shortens. The deduction is a pence-in-the-pound scale based on the withdrawn horse’s price at time of withdrawal.
  • Dead-heats: stake is divided by the number of tied horses for the affected place or win, then settled at full odds.
  • Non-runner: leg is void (factor 1.00) and affected lines reduce in fold count accordingly.

**Football**

  • Most 1X2 markets settle on 90 minutes plus stoppage time, excluding extra time and penalties unless otherwise stated.
  • Asian handicaps and totals can “push” (tie with the line), which voids that leg; quarter lines may split the stake across two adjacent lines.
  • Player props often require participation for action (e.g., must start or must play); otherwise the leg is void.
  • Postponements: if not played within the bookmaker’s stated window, the leg is void; if rescheduled within the window, it usually stands.
  • Dead-heats are rare but can occur in outright or seasonal markets (e.g., Top Goalscorer), applying proportional reductions similar to racing.

Key multiple takeaway: in both sports, a void leg settles at 1.00 within any multiple, so a treble with one void becomes a double on the remaining winners. From paper slips to live bet calculators: how we got here

Bet settlement moved from manual tallies on paper and shop till rolls to real-time engines that model every line in a multiple with accurate rounding, rule handling and sport-specific conventions. Historically, clerks combined odds by hand, applied Rule 4 deductions from printed scales, and worked through dead-heats and void legs across dozens or even hundreds of lines—an error-prone and time-consuming process. Modern calculators automate combinations, convert odds precisely across formats, and apply UK-facing settlement rules consistently, improving accuracy and transparency for bettors and operators alike.

Old-school settlement methods and common pitfalls

Traditional settlement involved working line by line with a calculator and printed rule sheets. The workflow and where errors commonly crept in are outlined below.

  • List selections and prices, usually in fractional odds.
  • Generate combinations (e.g., for a Canadian: 10 doubles, 10 trebles, 5 fourfolds, 1 fivefold).
  • Convert odds where needed (fractional to decimal) and multiply factors per line.
  • Apply each-way place fractions (e.g., 1/5 or 1/4) and duplicate lines for win/place parts.
  • Adjust for non-runners (void legs), dead-heats and Rule 4 deductions where applicable.
  • Round each line to the nearest penny; sum totals for return and profit.

Common pitfalls:

  • Missing a combination or double-counting a line in large perms (e.g., Heinz, Goliath).
  • Incorrect application of dead-heats (should divide stake by number of ties, not halve odds arbitrarily).
  • Forgetting that void legs reduce fold depth (treble with one void becomes a double).
  • Applying place terms incorrectly (confusing 1/5 vs 1/4 or wrong place counts by race type/field size).
  • Mishandling Rule 4 (deduction applies to winnings only; decimals adjust by 1 + (D − 1) × (1 − deduction)).
  • Rounding inconsistencies (per-line rounding vs aggregate rounding can differ by a penny or more).
  • Sport-specific settlement differences (e.g., football “90 mins only” vs markets that include extra time).

Dead-end approaches: static tables and manual prorating

Before dynamic tools, bettors relied on printed payout tables for fixed odds and fold counts, or improvised spreadsheets. These approaches break down when any leg deviates from the template—different prices, each-way terms, non-runners, Rule 4, or sport-specific pushes—forcing manual prorating that introduces rounding drift and inconsistent logic across lines. Static tables cannot encode all permutations and quickly become obsolete as market practices evolve.

  • Static payout tables assume uniform prices and no adjustments; they cannot handle live Rule 4 or dead-heats.
  • Manual prorating (e.g., “halve the odds” in a dead-heat) often misapplies correct method (should split stake).
  • One-size spreadsheets hide assumptions (rounding order, stake allocation), leading to penny errors compounded across dozens of lines.
  • Different bookmakers’ terms (settlement windows, place terms, push rules) render static references unreliable.
  • Outcome: inconsistent settlements, slower reconciliation, and poor transparency for customers and shops.

The other side of the coin: strongest argument against Super Yankees

The strongest counterargument to a Super Yankee (Canadian) is that a singles-first approach often makes better risk-adjusted sense for many bettors. Because a Super Yankee splits your bankroll across 26 lines and excludes singles, you need at least two winners to see any return and typically three to four winners (depending on prices) to show a profit. If your edge per selection is modest or uncertain, concentrating stake on singles can reduce variance, improve capital efficiency, and make results easier to audit against your pricing. Complex multiples amplify correlation risk between selections and can mask negative expected value behind occasional large payouts.

The singles-first strategy: pros and context

A singles-first plan prioritises clarity, control, and lower variance. It is widely preferred by risk-managed bettors, particularly when edges are small or markets are efficient.

  • Lower variance and smoother bankroll curve: each selection stands alone, so one winner still returns cash.
  • Transparent performance tracking: easier to attribute profit/loss to specific edges and closing line value.
  • Flexible staking: you can size each bet to confidence and price without being locked into equal unit stakes across 26 lines.
  • Reduced correlation exposure: related outcomes (e.g., same meeting going, same team news cycle) don’t cascade through many combined lines.
  • Operational simplicity: fewer settlement edge cases (dead-heats, voids, Rule 4) to propagate through dozens of lines.

Best contexts:

  • Limited bankroll or strict risk limits.
  • Small but frequent edges where compounding over time matters more than rare windfalls.
  • Sports with high variance per selection (e.g., football props) where strings of results can be choppy.
  • When you need clear auditability for compliance or personal record-keeping.

Risk concentration, variance and stake dilution

In a Super Yankee, your total outlay is dispersed across 26 lines. You earn nothing from a single winner and rely on doubles or better, which concentrates risk into combination outcomes. If picks are correlated (same jockey, same trainer form, same weather assumptions), poor information can torpedo many lines at once. Conversely, when only one pick wins, you realise no benefit despite being partially “right.” Stake dilution also reduces your effective stake on the best ideas compared to targeted singles or a smaller perm.

  • Stake dispersion: each line receives the same unit stake; your highest-confidence picks cannot be upweighted within the structure.
  • Correlation drag: adverse news on a card or team can sink multiple legs, wiping out many lines simultaneously.
  • Return threshold: two winners are required for any return; three or more typically needed to approach breakeven at shorter odds.
  • Rounding and deductions: Rule 4, dead-heats and voids propagate across numerous lines, compounding penny differences.

When should you avoid complex multiples?

Complex perms can be powerful when you expect multiple winners at value prices, but there are clear cases where they are counterproductive.

  • Your assessed edges are small or uncertain; capital is better deployed on carefully sized singles.
  • Bankroll is tight or subject to daily/weekly limits; higher variance from perms can accelerate drawdowns.
  • Selections are positively correlated (same meeting conditions, team news, or market drivers), magnifying downside.
  • You require predictable cash flow or strict maximum liability (e.g., trading around positions).
  • Events prone to Rule 4 and dead-heats (large-field handicaps) where adjustments proliferate across many lines.
  • You need granular record-keeping for self-assessment or safer gambling commitments.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many winners do I need in a Super Yankee to show a profit at common odds?

With uniform decimal odds of 3.00 (2/1), profit typically starts at 3 winners; with evens (2.00), you generally need 4 winners. For £1 per line (total £26), three winners at 3.00 return about £54 (profit +£28), while three winners at 2.00 return about £20 (loss −£6) and four winners at 2.00 return about £72 (profit +£46).

How does the Super Yankee calculator split a total stake across 26 or 52 lines?

Your total stake is divided equally across all lines, so unit stake = total stake ÷ 26 (win‑only) or ÷ 52 (each‑way). For example, a £20 win‑only total implies £20 ÷ 26 = £0.77 per line to the nearest penny; because £0.77 × 26 = £20.02, the effective spend can differ by up to a penny or two after per‑line rounding, and the calculator reports ROI from the actual spend shown.

How are Rule 4 deductions applied across doubles, trebles and bigger folds?

Apply the deduction to each affected leg independently, then multiply the adjusted decimal factors within the line. Using the UK convention, adjusted decimal = 1 + (original − 1) × (1 − deduction), so a 20p Rule 4 turns 4.00 into 3.40 and 3.00 into 2.60; a £1 double that was 12.00 becomes 8.84, a 26.3% reduction in that line’s return.

How do you settle a two‑way or three‑way dead‑heat inside a Super Yankee?

Divide the stake on the tied leg by the number of ties, then settle at full odds; in decimal terms this is equivalent to dividing that leg’s factor by the number of ties. For example, a £1 single at 5.00 in a two‑way dead‑heat returns (£1 ÷ 2) × 5.00 = £2.50; a treble with a two‑way dead‑heat leg 5.00 and other legs 2.50 and 3.00 pays 2.50 × 2.50 × 3.00 = £18.75 per £1.

Does the calculator handle football pushes, Asian handicaps and postponements?

Yes; a push voids that leg at 1.00 and the affected line reduces in fold depth. For instance, a double where AH 0 at 1.95 pushes and the other leg at 2.10 wins returns £2.10 per £1; a treble with one push and winners at 1.80 and 2.20 settles as a double paying 1.80 × 2.20 = £3.96 per £1, while postponed matches outside the bookmaker’s settlement window void that leg.

Are Super Yankee winnings taxed in the UK?

No; UK punters pay 0% tax on betting winnings, and operators, not customers, are liable for Remote Gaming Duty. Since 2001, the old punter betting duty has not applied to customer payouts, so your calculated returns are gross to you.

Can I weight certain selections with higher stakes inside a Super Yankee?

Not within the structure itself; all 26 lines carry the same unit stake by definition. To overweight a selection you must add separate bets that include it, for example extra singles or additional perms such as targeted doubles or a Trixie involving that selection, thereby increasing its total staked exposure by a known amount.

How does each‑way settlement work in a Super Yankee with winners and placers mixed?

The win part pays only when all legs in the line are winners, while the place part pays when all legs are placed or winners, with winners also counting as placed. For example, at place terms 1/5 on 3/1 (decimal win 4.00), the place decimal is 1.60, so two placed selections return 1.60 × 1.60 = £2.56 per £1 on each place‑part double even if neither won.

How is ROI calculated on the results screen and what do the percentages mean?

ROI% is computed as (Total return − Total stake) ÷ Total stake × 100. Using the worked examples, ROI for Example A is (301.00 − 26.00) ÷ 26.00 × 100 = 1,157.7%, while ROI for Example B each‑way with Rule 4 is (22.17 − 52.00) ÷ 52.00 × 100 = −57.4%.

What happens to each‑way place terms if the field size changes after non‑runners?

Settlement uses the bookmaker’s final terms at the off, so both the place fraction and the number of places can change with withdrawals. A common case is a 16‑runner handicap at 1/4 odds, 4 places becoming 15 runners at 1/4 odds, 3 places; your place part is then settled at the revised 3‑place terms across all affected lines.

Can the calculator model a dead‑heat for the final place on the each‑way part?

Yes; if two tie for one remaining place, the place return for that leg is halved in each affected line. For example, if a leg’s place decimal is 2.00 and it dead‑heats two‑way for the last paying place, its effective factor becomes 1.00, so a place double with another leg at 1.80 returns 1.00 × 1.80 = £1.80 per £1.

How should returns be rounded to match UK bookmaker settlement?

Round each line to the nearest penny before summing totals, which can create a 1p difference versus rounding only at the end. For instance, three lines at £1.335 each round to £1.34 and sum to £4.02, while the unrounded sum £4.005 would round to £4.01; per‑line rounding mirrors shop settlement.

Super Yankee vs Yankee: what changes in line count, stake and return profile?

A Super Yankee uses 5 selections and 26 lines, while a Yankee uses 4 selections and 11 lines; at £1 per line the win‑only stakes are £26 versus £11. Both need at least 2 winners for any return, but the Super Yankee concentrates more stake into higher folds, increasing variance and upside when 3–5 legs win compared with a smaller 4‑selection Yankee.

How are quarter‑line Asian handicaps (±0.25, ±0.75) treated in multiples?

A quarter line splits the line stake across two adjacent handicaps, and each sub‑line settles independently before recombining. For example, AH −0.25 splits into AH 0 and AH −0.5; if the match is drawn, half the stake pushes and half loses, so a double with a winning partner at 2.10 returns 0.5 × 2.10 = £1.05 per £1 unit on that line.
