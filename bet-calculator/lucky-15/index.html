How do I use the Lucky 15 Calculator?

A Lucky 15 is a 15-line multiple built from four selections: 4 singles, 6 doubles, 4 trebles, and 1 fourfold. To use the calculator, enter your odds and stake, choose Each-Way (if applicable), set place terms for horse racing, and record each selection’s result (Win, Place, Lose, Dead-Heat, Void). The tool then settles every line, applies any Rule 4 deductions you specify to the affected races, and outputs total stake, total return, and profit.

What steps calculate returns, each-way, and totals?

Follow this sequence to mirror how UK bookies settle a Lucky 15 and to ensure each line is correctly evaluated.

Choose bet type and stake:

  • Select Lucky 15 and enter your unit stake (e.g., £0.50).
  • Tick Each-Way for horse racing (splits the unit stake 50/50 between Win and Place on every line).

Enter odds and format:

  • Input each selection’s odds in fractional (e.g., 3/1), decimal (e.g., 4.00), or American (e.g., +300) format.
  • If Each-Way, specify the place terms for each race (e.g., 1/5 odds, places 1–3).

Set settlement factors per selection:

  • Result: Win, Place, Lose, Dead-Heat, Void.
  • Rule 4 (if any): enter the deduction in pence per £1 (e.g., 20p = 0.20).

Calculate singles:

  • Win single return (fractional a/b with stake s): return = s × (a/b) + s. Decimal d: return = s × d.

Each-Way single splits stake into Win (s/2) and Place (s/2). Place odds use the place factor f (e.g., 1/5 = 0.20):

  • Place fractional = (a/b) × f; place decimal = 1 + (d − 1) × f.
  • Winners pay on both Win and Place parts; a placer pays only on the Place part; a loser pays neither.

Calculate multiples (doubles, trebles, fourfold):

  • Win part: every leg must win.
  • Place part: every leg must place (a winner counts as placed).
  • Accumulator return = stake × product of decimal odds across legs (use Win decimals for the Win part; Place decimals for the Place part).

Totals and profit:

  • Total stake (Each-Way Lucky 15) = 15 lines × 2 parts × unit stake.
  • Total return = sum of all winning lines (Win and Place parts).
  • Profit = Total return − Total stake.

Which odds formats, Rule 4, dead heats, and voids are supported?

The calculator supports UK settlement conventions for horse racing and other sports. Enter odds in your preferred format, and the tool converts and settles accordingly, with options for Rule 4, dead-heats, and voids.

Odds formats:

  • Fractional (e.g., 3/1), Decimal (e.g., 4.00), American (e.g., +300 or −200).
  • Conversions: decimal = 1 + (a/b); American = +100 × (a/b) if decimal ≥ 2.00, else −100 ÷ (a/b).

Rule 4 deductions (UK/Tattersalls):

  • Applied when a selection is withdrawn after betting, reducing the winnings (not the stake) on affected races.
  • Input the deduction as pence in the pound (e.g., 20p = 0.20). Adjusted decimal = 1 + (original decimal − 1) × (1 − deduction).
  • Typical range: 0.05 to 0.90 depending on the withdrawn favourite’s price at the time of withdrawal.

Dead-heat settlement:

  • Win market: if n runners tie, the winning stake is divided by n and settled at full odds; the remaining (n−1)/n of the stake is lost.
  • Place market: divide the stake by the number of runners sharing the final place(s) and settle at place odds.
  • Example (win DH, 2-way, 6/1, £1 stake): return = (£1/2 × 7.00) = £3.50 decimal (or £0.50 × 6 + £0.50).

Voids:

  • A void selection settles at 1.00 (stake returned for that leg). In multiples, the affected line becomes a bet on the remaining selections.
  • Example: a double with one void becomes a single on the other leg for both Win and Place parts.

What is a Lucky 15 bet and how does it work?

A Lucky 15 is a full-coverage multiple built from four selections, producing 15 separate lines: 4 singles, 6 doubles, 4 trebles, and 1 fourfold. Each line is settled on its own merits and all winning lines are added together to form your total return. If placed Each-Way (commonly used in horse racing and greyhounds), every line is duplicated into a Win part and a Place part, doubling the number of lines from 15 to 30. Place terms (for example, 1/5 the odds, places 1–3) apply to the Place part only, while the Win part requires the selection to win.

Do UK bookies pay one-winner and all-correct bonuses?

Many UK bookmakers run promotional bonuses on Lucky 15s, but they are not universal and can vary by brand, sport, market, stake type, and date. Common formats include an enhanced-settlement bonus when exactly one selection wins (“one-winner” bonus) and a percentage uplift to the total return when all four selections win (“all-correct” bonus). Always check the current terms on the bookmaker’s site before betting; these promotions can be changed, limited, or withdrawn.

One-winner bonus (typical concept):

  • Applies only when exactly one of the four selections wins and the others lose.
  • Usually enhances the winning single (for example, by settling that single at a multiple of the odds) rather than paying on multiples that do not exist.
  • May exclude certain bet types or apply only to designated sports (often horse racing and greyhounds).

All-correct bonus (typical concept):

  • Applies when all four selections win, adding a percentage bonus to the return.
  • Frequently calculated as a percentage of “winnings” (return minus stake) rather than the full return, but methods differ by bookmaker.
  • For Each-Way bets, some terms apply the bonus to the Win part only; others define qualifying criteria differently.

Key cautions:

  • Promotions are discretionary and subject to eligibility, settling rules, and market restrictions.
  • Dead-heats, Rule 4 deductions, voids, or non-runners can affect whether a bet qualifies.
  • Always confirm qualifying markets, maximum payouts, and how the bonus is calculated in the specific terms.

Edge cases: How do Rule 4 tables and margins affect payout?

Two levers materially change returns on a Lucky 15: Rule 4 deductions and the built-in margin on your odds. Rule 4 reduces the winnings on affected legs only; bookmaker margin is already “baked into” each price and compounds across multiples.

Applying Rule 4 to one winning leg in a multiple:

  • Adjusted decimal formula: adjusted = 1 + (original − 1) × (1 − deduction).
  • Example double (Win-only, £1 unit): Leg 1 at 4.00 with 20p Rule 4 → 1 + 3 × 0.80 = 3.40; Leg 2 at 3.00 → unchanged. Return = £1 × 3.40 × 3.00 = £10.20 (vs £12.00 with no deduction).

Dead-heats vs Each-Way:

  • Win market dead-heat: divide the winning stake by n tied runners and settle at full odds; the remainder of the stake is lost.
  • Place market dead-heat: divide the Place stake among the number sharing the final place(s) and settle at place odds.

Margin compounding:

  • If each leg’s offered decimal is, say, 2% lower than the “fair” no-margin price on the winnings portion, the product across four legs multiplies that haircut four times, noticeably reducing the accumulator’s return.
  • Practical takeaway: marginal price differences per leg can add up substantially in a 15-line multiple.

Always mirror the exact inputs from your slip—odds format, Each-Way status, place fraction and places, Rule 4 (if any), and any dead-heat or voids—to align calculator outputs with bookmaker settlement. Promotions such as one-winner or all-correct bonuses, and brand maximum-payout caps, are applied after the core math and may change the final figure you receive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are Lucky 15 winnings taxed in the UK?

For UK residents, gambling winnings are not taxed; the tax rate on personal betting returns is 0% and no self-assessment entry is required. Operators pay betting duty, not punters. Practical takeaway: a £500 return from a Lucky 15 is paid as £500 net to you, with 0 forms to file and 0 income tax due as of 2026.

How do Best Odds Guaranteed (BOG) affect a Lucky 15 return?

BOG upsizes the price on each winning racing leg if SP exceeds your taken odds, and the higher price then flows through every affected line. Example: a £1 win single at 4/1 (5.00) that drifts to 6/1 (7.00) pays £7.00 instead of £5.00, a 40% uplift; in a double, replacing one 5.00 with 7.00 turns £1 × 5.00 × 3.00 = £15.00 into £1 × 7.00 × 3.00 = £21.00. Calculators show core settlement; apply BOG leg-by-leg where your bookmaker terms say it applies.

Can I place a Lucky 15 with free bet stakes and how do I calculate the return?

If allowed by the bookmaker, SR (stake not returned) free bets pay winnings only on each winning line. To model it, use return = stake × (decimal − 1) for every winning line, not stake × decimal. Example: a £1 unit on a 4.00 winner returns £3.00 with SR (4.00 − 1.00) versus £4.00 with cash; if three lines win at 4.00, the free-bet-funded total is £9.00 instead of £12.00. Many UK operators restrict tokens on full-cover multiples, so check eligibility before staking.

How does partial Cash Out change a Lucky 15 settlement?

Partial Cash Out sells a percentage of the entire bet and scales both remaining stakes and eventual returns by the unsold proportion. If you cash out 40% of a £0.50-unit Lucky 15, the live portion is 60%: each line’s unit becomes £0.30, and any later return is 0.6 × the amount it would have been without cash out. Example: a line that would have paid £20 now pays £12; total outlay recorded is £15 originally, with 40% of its live value realized immediately as cash-out and 60% left to settle.

What’s the difference between a Lucky 15, a Yankee, and a Canadian (Super Yankee)?

A Lucky 15 has 15 lines from 4 selections and includes singles; a Yankee has 11 lines from 4 selections without singles; a Canadian has 26 lines from 5 selections without singles. At £0.50 win-only, costs are £7.50 (Lucky 15), £5.50 (Yankee), and £13.00 (Canadian); at £0.50 each-way they double to £15.00, £11.00, and £26.00 respectively. The inclusion of singles in a Lucky 15 increases resilience because even one winner returns.

Can I include related contingencies in a Lucky 15?

No; UK bookmakers block multiples where outcomes are dependent, so related selections cannot be combined. Typical barred pairs include two markets from the same horse and race that logically overlap, for example Win and Without-Favourite on the same runner, or Scorecast-style football props with the same match result; attempting them results in rejection, or the operator reforming the slip into permitted lines only.

Do ante-post rules change Lucky 15 settlement for racing?

Yes; traditional ante-post bets usually have no Rule 4 and a non-runner is a losing bet, while NRNB markets refund the stake at 1.00 if the horse does not run. Example: in an ante-post Lucky 15, a non-runner leg contributes £0.00 to affected lines; in NRNB the same leg contributes 1.00 as a multiplier, turning, for example, an AB double into a single on B. Always match the market type per selection before computing returns.

How are multiple Rule 4 deductions combined on a Lucky 15?

Rule 4s on the same race add together and are capped at 90p per £1, and they reduce winnings only via adjusted decimal = 1 + (original − 1) × (1 − combined deduction). Example: a 5.00 winner with two withdrawals at 10p and 15p combines to 25p; adjusted = 1 + 4 × 0.75 = 4.00, so a £1 win single pays £4.00 instead of £5.00. If later a further 70p applied, the total would cap at 90p, giving adjusted = 1 + 4 × 0.10 = 1.40.

How are dead-heats for the last place settled when extra places are paid?

You divide the place stake by the number of runners sharing the final qualifying place and settle at place odds. Example: an each-way 1/5 odds, places 1–4 market has a three-way tie for 4th; a £1 place stake at 10/1 has place odds of 10/1 × 1/5 = 2/1 (3.00 decimal), so the return is £1 ÷ 3 × 3.00 = £1.00; two-thirds of the place stake is lost due to the dead-heat split.

Which rounding method should I use to match UK bookmaker payouts to the penny?

Most UK operators round to the nearest penny at settlement, but the rounding point can be per-line or on the final total, creating 1p to 2p differences. Example: three winning lines at £0.333 each round per line to £0.33 + £0.33 + £0.33 = £0.99, but rounding on the total gives £1.00; conversely, a single line at £1.235 rounds to £1.24. If you need exact parity, replicate your bookmaker’s rounding point consistently.

Why does my Lucky 15 calculator result differ from William Hill or Betfred by a few pence?

Small gaps usually come from rounding point, applied promotions, or market settings mismatches. Typical causes include per-line versus total rounding worth 1p to 2p on small stakes, BOG lifting SP on winners which raises affected lines by 10% to 50% when drifts are large, one-winner or all-correct bonuses adding, for example, 10% to winnings, and place-term or Rule 4 differences where a 20p deduction cuts the winnings portion by 20% on that leg and compounds in multiples.

With exactly two selections placed and none winning, how many lines pay on an each-way Lucky 15?

Three place lines pay: two place singles and one place double; all win-part lines lose. If the two placers have place decimals of 2.50 and 3.00 with a £0.50 each-way unit, the returns are £0.50 × 2.50 + £0.50 × 3.00 + £0.50 × 2.50 × 3.00 = £1.25 + £1.50 + £3.75 = £6.50 from those three lines only.

How do I convert negative American odds to fractional for Lucky 15 inputs?

Use fractional = 100/|A| for negative American A and simplify. Examples: −200 converts to 100/200 = 1/2, −150 converts to 100/150 = 2/3 which is commonly expressed as 4/6, and −120 converts to 100/120 = 5/6; the equivalent decimals are 1.50, 1.67, and 1.83 respectively. If you prefer decimals in the calculator, use decimal = 1 + 100/|A| for negative prices.

How can I estimate break-even strike rate and expected value (EV) for a Lucky 15?

Sum EV across the 15 component bets: EV(line) = stake × product of leg probabilities × product of decimals, and break-even occurs when the total EV equals the total stake. With four fair legs at decimal 3.00 each and win probability 1/3, a single’s EV is s × 1/3 × 3.00 = 1.00s, a double’s is s × (1/3)^2 × 3^2 = 1.00s, and likewise for trebles and the fourfold; summing 4 singles + 6 doubles + 4 trebles + 1 fourfold returns 15s against 15s staked. If each leg’s decimal is haircut by 5% on the winnings portion to 2.90, a single’s EV becomes s × 1/3 × 2.90 = 0.967s and the 15-line EV sums to about 14.5s, implying a roughly 3.3% shortfall versus total outlay.
