4:T7f23,<h2>What does the Arbitrage Calculator do for 2-way, 3-way, and 4-way bets?</h2>
<p>An arbitrage calculator splits your total stake across mutually exclusive outcomes so the gross return is equal (or near-equal) regardless of the result. For decimal odds with no commission, arbitrage exists if the sum of inverse odds across the selected outcomes is less than 1. The calculator converts your total stake into outcome stakes using proportional formulas, handles rounding to the nearest penny, and shows expected return, guaranteed profit, and ROI.</p>
<h3>How to calculate 2-way arbitrage stakes step by step</h3>
<p>Use 2-way mode when there are exactly two mutually exclusive outcomes that fully cover the market (e.g., tennis Player A vs Player B, or football Draw No Bet lines across two books). The goal is to equalise gross returns.</p>
<ul><li>Check the arbitrage condition: 1/O1 + 1/O2 &lt; 1 (decimal odds O1 and O2).</li><li>Compute the inverse-odds sum S_inv = (1/O1) + (1/O2).</li><li>Compute equal return R = TotalStake / S_inv.</li><li>Compute stakes: Stake1 = R / O1, Stake2 = R / O2.</li><li>Round stakes to currency precision (e.g., £0.01) and adjust one line by a penny if needed so Stake1 + Stake2 = TotalStake.</li><li>Profit = R − TotalStake. ROI = Profit / TotalStake.</li></ul>

<h3>3-way arbitrage: how do you split stakes across outcomes?</h3>
<p>Use 3-way mode for markets with three mutually exclusive outcomes (e.g., football 1X2: Home–Draw–Away). The same principle applies: allocate by inverse odds to equalise gross returns.</p>
<ul><li>Check the arbitrage condition: 1/O1 + 1/O2 + 1/O3 &lt; 1.</li><li>Let S_inv = (1/O1) + (1/O2) + (1/O3).</li><li>Equal return R = TotalStake / S_inv.</li><li>Stakes: Stake1 = R / O1, Stake2 = R / O2, Stake3 = R / O3.</li><li>Round to the nearest penny; adjust one stake so the sum equals TotalStake.</li><li>Profit = R − TotalStake; ROI = Profit / TotalStake.</li></ul>

<h3>4-way and extra outcomes: when should you use them?</h3>
<p>4-way mode applies when the market has four mutually exclusive outcomes that fully cover all possibilities (common examples: method of victory in boxing/MMA: KO/TKO, Submission, Decision, Draw; tennis match correct score in best-of-three sets: 2–0, 2–1, 1–2, 0–2). The same equal-return logic extends to any number of outcomes n.</p>
<ul><li>Arb condition (no commission): (1/O1 + 1/O2 + 1/O3 + 1/O4) &lt; 1.</li><li>Equal return: R = TotalStake / Σ(1/Oi); Stakes: Stake i = R / Oi for i = 1..4.</li></ul>
<p>When to use:</p>
<ul><li>Method-of-victory markets (four outcomes including Draw/Technical Decision).</li><li>Correct-score in short formats with exactly four outcomes (e.g., best-of-three tennis match scorelines).</li><li>Three-runner “with tie” markets that list a separate “any tie” outcome as a fourth option.</li></ul>
<p>Practical cautions:</p>
<ul><li>Settlement rules: ensure all selections settle on every result (no partial voids or “other” buckets that add hidden outcomes).</li><li>Dead-heat or split-decision rules can change the effective payout; model these before staking.</li><li>Rounding: with more outcomes, cumulative rounding drift increases—apply a final penny adjustment to one outcome.</li></ul>
<p>Illustrative 4-way check (no commission): with odds 2.90, 4.60, 4.80, 6.50, the inverse-odds sum is 0.924398 (arb exists). For a £100 total stake, the theoretical equal return is £108.178, implying an £8.18 profit (8.18% ROI) before rounding. You would size each stake as Stake i = R / Oi and round to the nearest penny, ensuring the stakes sum to £100.</p>
<h2>How to use the formula: margin, implied probability, and stake sizing?</h2>
<p>Arbitrage exists when the sum of implied probabilities from independently sourced prices is less than 100%. In decimal odds, this means the sum of reciprocals is below 1 after adjusting for any fees or commission. Stake sizing then allocates your total stake proportionally to equalise the gross return across outcomes. For n outcomes with effective decimal odds Oi, the break-even check is Σ(1/Oi) &lt; 1 and the equal-return stake for outcome i is Stake i = (TotalStake / Σ(1/Oj)) / Oi.</p>
<h3>What is the arbitrage formula and break-even threshold?</h3>
<p>The arbitrage condition compares the total implied probability against 100%. Using decimal odds, implied probability for outcome i is Pi = 1/Oi. For a market fully covered by n outcomes (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive):</p>
<ul><li>Arbitrage condition (no commission): Σ(1/Oi) &lt; 1.</li><li>Equal-return constant: R = TotalStake / Σ(1/Oi).</li><li>Stake sizing (each outcome): Stake i = R / Oi.</li><li>Guaranteed profit: Profit = R − TotalStake. ROI = (1 / Σ(1/Oi)) − 1.</li></ul>
<p>With platform fees or commission, first convert each quoted price to an effective decimal Oi,eff that reflects net payout after all charges, then apply the same formulas to Oi,eff. The break-even threshold remains Σ(1/Oi,eff) &lt; 1; the margin (or overround) of a set of quotes is Overround = Σ(1/Oi) − 1. Arbitrage margin is ArbEdge = 1 − Σ(1/Oi,eff).</p>
<h3>How do I convert fractional, decimal, and American odds in the UK?</h3>
<p>UK-facing sites often show fractional odds, while exchanges and tools default to decimal. These are exact conversions: decimal = fractional + 1; American odds convert piecewise based on sign. Implied probability is 1/decimal (expressed as a percentage).</p>
<ul><li>Fractional (A/B) → Decimal: 1 + A/B.</li><li>Decimal (D) → Fractional: D − 1, simplified to a fraction.</li></ul>
<p>American (US) → Decimal:</p>
<ul><li>If US ≥ 0: 1 + (US / 100).</li><li>If US &lt; 0: 1 + (100 / |US|).</li></ul>
<p>Decimal (D) → American:</p>
<ul><li>If D ≥ 2.0: US = (D − 1) × 100.</li><li>If 1.01 ≤ D &lt; 2.0: US = −100 / (D − 1).</li></ul>
<ul><li>Implied probability: P = 1 / D.</li></ul>

<h3>How do you account for commission and exchange lay odds?</h3>
<p>Exchanges typically charge commission c on net winnings (e.g., 2%–5%), applied only on the side that wins the market. Adjust odds before running the arbitrage check and stake sizing:</p>
<ul><li>Back bets on an exchange (commission c): effective decimal Oeff(back) = 1 + (O − 1) × (1 − c). Example: O = 3.00 at 5% → Oeff = 1 + 2 × 0.95 = 2.90.</li><li>Bookmaker back bets (UK) usually have no commission; Oeff = O, but still confirm any promo terms or deductions.</li></ul>
<p>Lay bets (exchange) are best modelled by case outcomes. To equalise a bookmaker back with an exchange lay on the same selection:</p>
<ul><li>Let Sb be the bookmaker back stake at odds Ob (no commission).</li><li>Let you lay the same selection at lay odds Ol with commission c.</li><li>Lay liability: Liability = (Ol − 1) × L (where L is lay stake).</li></ul>
<p>Net profits:</p>
<ul><li>If the selection wins: Pwin = Sb × (Ob − 1) − (Ol − 1) × L.</li><li>If the selection loses: Plose = L × (1 − c) − Sb.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Equal-profit lay stake: L = (Sb × Ob) / (Ol − c).</li></ul>
<p>Two-way arb check using a lay as the contra side: convert the lay price on A into an effective “back” price on Not-A, then apply Σ(1/Oeff) &lt; 1:</p>
<ul><li>Equivalent back odds for Not-A from a lay at Ol with commission c: Onot,eff = 1 + (1 − c) / (Ol − 1).</li><li>Then check: 1/Ob + 1/Onot,eff &lt; 1. If true, an arb exists after commission.</li></ul>
<p>Worked example (bookmaker back + exchange lay): Back Team A £100 at Ob = 2.40 (no commission). Lay Team A at Ol = 2.52 with c = 5%. Equal-profit lay stake L = (100 × 2.40) / (2.52 − 0.05) = 240 / 2.47 ≈ £97.17. Liability = (2.52 − 1) × 97.17 ≈ £147.69. Net if A wins: £100 × 1.40 − £147.69 ≈ −£7.69. Net if A loses: £97.17 × 0.95 − £100 ≈ −£7.69. No arb here (a controlled hedge loss); improving either Ob upward or Ol downward can flip this to a sure profit after fees.</p>

<h3>Live vs offline data: how do latency and slippage affect results?</h3>
<p>Arbitrage windows are often measured in seconds. Latency (quote age, network round-trip, order routing) and slippage (fills worse than top-of-book) can erase a theoretical edge. Treat delay and depth as explicit costs and require a buffer above the pure fee-adjusted threshold.</p>
<p>Latency sources:</p>
<ul><li>Data freshness: screen-scrape or delayed APIs can add 1–5+ seconds of staleness.</li><li>Network and matching: routing and queueing can move your fill back in the book.</li></ul>
<p>Slippage modelling:</p>
<ul><li>Estimate expected slippage s per unit (e.g., ticks or basis points) from recent depth.</li><li>Revised condition: Edge &gt; Fees + Transfer + s.</li><li>Prefer limit orders for control; use partial fills logic in the calculator.</li></ul>
<p>Mitigations:</p>
<ul><li>Pre-fund accounts to avoid transfer delays; choose markets with consistent depth.</li><li>Set a minimum target edge (e.g., ≥ 0.50% above total costs) before execution.</li><li>Cap stake size to top-of-book depth and add a time-to-live on quotes.</li><li>Cache profiles for venue fees and auto-apply a slippage buffer from recent trades.</li></ul>

<h2>The other side of the coin: why won’t a calculator guarantee profit?</h2>
<p>An arbitrage calculator equalises theoretical returns under fixed prices and rules. In the UK market, real frictions—palpable error clauses, bet limits or partial acceptance, settlement rules (voids, Rule 4 deductions, dead-heats), exchange commission, and account restrictions—can break those assumptions. Price moves during placement and operational delays introduce slippage, turning a paper edge into a loss. Robust workflow, buffers, and bankroll discipline are required to convert calculations into realised profit.</p>
<h3>What can go wrong: palpable errors, limits, voids, gubbing?</h3>
<p>Even perfect math can fail if operational or rules-based risks materialise. Plan for the following failure modes and build safeguards into your process.</p>
<p>Palpable error (palp):</p>
<ul><li>Definition: a clear pricing mistake; many bookmakers reserve the right to void or adjust such bets per their T&amp;Cs.</li><li>Impact: your hedge may stand while the mispriced leg is voided, leaving unbalanced exposure.</li><li>Mitigation: avoid extreme outliers versus market consensus; use time-stamped screenshots and keep stake sizes conservative on anomalous quotes.</li></ul>
<p>Limits and partial acceptance:</p>
<ul><li>Issue: maximum stake limits, per-account risk flags, or “bet referred” delays; exchanges have depth limits at top-of-book.</li><li>Impact: only part of your intended stake is matched, breaking equal-return sizing.</li><li>Mitigation: query min/max stakes before sizing, cap per-leg stakes, and pre-check exchange depth; allow rebalancing logic.</li></ul>
<p>Voids and settlement mismatches:</p>
<ul><li>Examples: event cancellations; non-runners (horse racing) triggering Rule 4 deductions; sport-specific push/draw rules; dead-heats reducing payout.</li><li>Impact: one side voids while the other settles, or pays less than assumed, eliminating the edge.</li><li>Mitigation: harmonise market rules (1X2 vs DNB vs Asian lines), model deductions, and avoid legs with ambiguous settlement.</li></ul>
<p>Account restrictions (“gubbing”):</p>
<ul><li>Issue: sustained arbing or promo extraction can trigger stake restrictions or market exclusions.</li><li>Impact: sudden inability to place hedges at required sizes or prices.</li><li>Mitigation: diversify venues, moderate stake sizes, avoid obvious arbing patterns, and maintain exchange alternatives.</li></ul>
<p>Operational delays:</p>
<ul><li>Sources: stale screen prices, slow page loads, manual entry errors, MFA prompts at checkout.</li><li>Impact: prices drift while you enter stakes, eroding or reversing EV.</li><li>Mitigation: pre-fill profiles, use PWA/mobile workflows, set quote time-to-live, and require minimum edge buffers.</li></ul>

<p>For two-way arbitrage with decimal odds O1 and O2, the fee-adjusted break-even is (1/O1 + 1/O2) &lt; 1. <h3>Bankroll management: what staking caps protect you?</h3>
<p>Although a correctly hedged arb carries low market risk, operational and rules risks remain. Bankroll policy should cap exposure per arb, limit concentration by venue and event, and maintain cash buffers to absorb failed hedges or voids.</p>
<p>Per-arb exposure caps:</p>
<ul><li>Pre-match, stable markets: 2–5% of bankroll per completed arb.</li><li>In-play or thin markets: 0.5–2% due to higher slippage/void risk.</li></ul>
<p>Venue concentration:</p>
<ul><li>Cap exposure to any single bookmaker or exchange at 20–30% of bankroll to reduce counterparty and restriction risk.</li></ul>
<p>Event correlation:</p>
<ul><li>Limit total exposure across correlated markets on the same event (e.g., main line and derivatives) to 5–10% of bankroll.</li></ul>
<p>Operational buffers:</p>
<ul><li>Maintain 10–20% of bankroll as liquid reserve for re-hedging, fees, or unexpected voids.</li><li>Use penny-accurate rounding with final reconciliation to the intended total stake.</li></ul>
<p>Edge thresholds:</p>
<ul><li>Only execute when fee-adjusted ROI exceeds your latency/slippage buffer (e.g., minimum net ROI ≥ 0.75–1.50%).</li></ul>
<h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
<h3>Is arbitrage betting legal in the UK and are profits taxed?</h3>
<p>Arbitrage betting is legal in the UK, but bookmakers can limit or refuse bets under their terms. HMRC does not tax gambling winnings for individuals, whereas non-gambling activities such as crypto arbitrage may be taxable as capital gains or income depending on circumstances. Keep bookmaker arbing and any crypto trading separate, keep records with timestamps and stakes, and seek tax advice if you operate beyond casual betting.</p>
<h3>How can I check a 2-way surebet in under five seconds?</h3>
<p>Add the reciprocals of the two effective decimal odds; if the sum is below 1, a surebet exists and ROI equals (1/sum) − 1. Example: 2.20 and 2.10 give 1/2.20 + 1/2.10 = 0.9307, so ROI ≈ 7.44% and a £100 stake targets about £7.44 profit before rounding and fees.</p>
<h3>What buffer should I require for in-play arbitrage to cover latency and slippage?</h3>
<p>Target a net ROI buffer of about 0.5% to 1.5% above all fee-adjusted costs for live markets. As a quick rule, if recent fills show about 0.3% average slippage per leg and you place two legs, require at least 0.6% extra edge plus a small latency allowance (for example 0.2%), so a minimum net ROI threshold near 0.8%.</p>
<h3>How do I adjust exchange prices for commission without a calculator?</h3>
<p>For a back on an exchange with commission c, use Oeff = 1 + (O − 1) × (1 − c); for a lay at Ol, the effective “Not‑A” back odds are Onot,eff = 1 + (1 − c) / (Ol − 1). Example with c = 2%: an exchange back at 4.00 becomes 3.94; a lay at 2.20 becomes Not‑A odds 1.8167, so a 2-way arb with a bookmaker back at 2.30 has 1/2.30 + 1/1.8167 = 0.9848 and ≈1.55% ROI.</p>
<h3>What rounding rule keeps 3‑way and 4‑way stakes consistent to the penny?</h3>
<p>Round each leg to the nearest £0.01, then reconcile by adjusting a single leg by £0.01 so the stakes sum exactly to your total. The resulting return spread stays within a few pence; the worst‑case deviation from equal return is approximately max_odds × £0.01, for example with max odds 6.50 the spread is roughly £0.065.</p>
<h3>Can I use a stake‑not‑returned free bet in an arbitrage and how do I size it?</h3>
<p>Yes; treat the free bet as profit‑only with effective odds Oeff = O − 1 and pair it with a lay sized for equal profit. Example: a £25 SNR free bet at 5.0 vs a lay at 5.2 with 2% commission gives L = FreeStake × (O − 1) / (Ol − c) = 25 × 4 / 5.18 ≈ £19.31, which locks about £18.9 on either outcome, a ≈75.6% return on the free bet’s face value.</p>
<h3>Which settlement rules most often break surebets and how do I protect myself?</h3>
<p>Rule 4 deductions, dead‑heats, draw/push mismatches, and palpable errors are the common failure modes, so align rules before staking and avoid obvious misprices. As a quant check, a Rule 4 deduction of 25p per pound reduces the winnings component by 25% so decimal 5.00 becomes 1 + (5.00 − 1) × 0.75 = 4.00, which can erase a marginal edge if not modelled.</p>
<h3>Does the calculator work with UK odds formats and can I mix them in one calc?</h3>
<p>Yes; convert all quotes to decimal before the arb check, since conversion is exact. For example 6/4 equals 2.50 and +150 American, 1/2 equals 1.50 and −200, and implied probability is always 1/decimal.</p>
<h3>How do I cap stakes to exchange depth and estimate ROI impact if I slip to the next price level?</h3>
<p>Limit each exchange leg to the size available at the top of book and estimate the ROI change from any portion filled at worse odds by recomputing the reciprocal sum with a weighted average. Example: if half a leg slips from 2.50 to 2.48, 1/odds rises from 0.4000 to 0.4032, adding about 0.0016 to the 2‑way sum and trimming ROI by roughly 0.16%.</p>
<h3>Can I combine crypto and bookmaker legs in the UK?</h3>
<p>UK‑licensed bookmakers generally do not accept direct crypto deposits, so treat crypto arbitrage as a separate exchange‑to‑exchange workflow. In crypto mode, require the sell price after fees to exceed the buy price after fees plus transfer cost per unit, and add a practical buffer of at least 0.20% to 0.50% for slippage during transfers or hedges.</p>
<h3>What’s the fastest way to compare two candidate arbs and pick the better one?</h3>
<p>Compute S = Σ(1/Oi,eff) for each and select the smaller S because ROI = (1/S) − 1. If Arb A has S = 0.972 (ROI ≈ 2.88%) and Arb B has S = 0.965 (ROI ≈ 3.63%), B is superior before rounding, limits, and slippage checks.</p>
