How do I convert decimal, fractional, and American odds to probability?

Implied probability is the chance of an outcome as implied by the quoted price. Converting odds to an implied probability lets you benchmark prices, find value, and compare books consistently. The formulas below return the bookmaker-implied probability (they include margin if present); to estimate “true” probability you would de‑vig a market using all sides of the book.

What formulas convert each format to implied probability?

The core relationships are simple and reversible. Use the table as a quick reference; multiply p by 100 to get a percentage.

  • For multi‑outcome markets (e.g., 1X2), sum all implied probabilities to see the overround.

How does UK rounding work for 11/8, 7/4, and other fractions?

UK displays favour a “ladder” of familiar fractional quotes rather than arbitrary fractions. Traders convert their model price (often kept internally in decimal) to the nearest display fraction on this ladder, so the screen shows human-friendly numbers that bettors recognise.

Start from decimal or probability:

  • Given decimal d, fractional ratio r = d − 1.
  • Given probability p, r = (1 − p) / p.
  • Map r to the nearest standard UK fraction. Common rungs include: 1/4 (1.25), 2/5 (1.40), 4/7 (1.571), 8/11 (1.727), 10/11 (1.909), Evens 1/1 (2.00), 11/10 (2.10), 6/5 (2.20), 5/4 (2.25), 11/8 (2.375), 13/8 (2.625), 7/4 (2.75), 15/8 (2.875), 2/1 (3.00), 9/4 (3.25), 5/2 (3.50), 3/1 (4.00), 7/2 (4.50), 4/1 (5.00), 9/2 (5.50), 5/1 (6.00).

Tie-breaking and house style:

  • If a model price sits exactly between two rungs, firms use internal style guides to choose one (conventions differ by operator).
  • For display clarity, equivalent simplified forms may be preferred (e.g., show 4/6 instead of 2/3; show 6/4 instead of 3/2).
  • 11/8: decimal = 1 + 11/8 = 2.375; implied probability = 8 ÷ (11 + 8) = 8/19 ≈ 42.105%.
  • 7/4: decimal = 1 + 7/4 = 2.75; implied probability = 4 ÷ (7 + 4) = 4/11 ≈ 36.364%.
  • 10/11: decimal = 1 + 10/11 ≈ 1.909; implied probability = 11 ÷ (10 + 11) = 11/21 ≈ 52.381%.
  • 4/6 (often shown instead of 2/3): decimal = 1 + 4/6 ≈ 1.667; implied probability = 6 ÷ (4 + 6) = 60%.

Practical workflow: convert your internal decimal to the nearest ladder price for display, but keep the precise decimal for risk, settlement, and audit. This preserves user-friendly screens without losing accuracy in back-end calculations.

Evolution of odds formats: from UK fractional to global decimal

Odds formats evolved with trading practice, media conventions, and regulation. The UK built a fraction-first culture around on‑course bookmaking and pre‑decimal currency, while continental Europe and the internet era standardised on decimals for clarity and computation. Asian formats (Hong Kong, Malay, Indonesian) remain accurate and useful regionally, but have seen limited global uptake due to usability and interoperability frictions.

Why did fractional prices dominate UK books and Tote pools?

Fractional prices reflect net profit “to stake” and matched how British racecourse layers quoted and settled for more than a century. This convention persisted across shop boards, newspapers, and broadcast commentary, shaping bettor intuition.

  • On‑course trading workflow: chalkboards and tic‑tac signals used simple fractional “ladders” (e.g., 4/6, 10/11, Evens, 11/10, 6/4, 7/4, 9/4) that could be updated quickly and memorised.
  • Pre‑1971 currency intuition: pounds–shillings–pence encouraged thinking in halves, quarters, eighths—exactly the denominators used in fractional quotes (e.g., 11/8, 13/8).
  • Settlement clarity for bettors: a/b communicates “win a for every b staked,” keeping profit separate from returned stake—useful when hedging each‑way or dutching.
  • Media reinforcement: form guides, price shows, and commentary normalised fractions (EVS, 6/4, 7/2), so bettors learned mental benchmarks rather than decimals.
  • Tote influence: while pool dividends were often published as a return to a £1 unit (a decimal‑like figure), race‑day culture and newspapers frequently referenced or compared them against familiar fractional prices, reinforcing fraction‑first thinking among UK bettors.

How did decimal odds spread across Europe and online books?

Decimal odds simplify probability math (p = 1/d), reduce input errors in software, and localise better across languages and currencies. As betting digitised, these traits made decimals the default for pan‑European and online platforms.

  • Continental heritage: state and monopoly operators in Europe (e.g., Scandinavia, Benelux) used decimal‑style returns aligned with metric currency, priming customers to read “1.80” or “2.10.”
  • Internet platforms (late 1990s–2000s): online sportsbooks serving multiple jurisdictions adopted decimals to avoid per‑market format toggles; exchanges standardised on decimal for matching engines and API simplicity.
  • UI/UX and compliance: decimals fit small screens, export cleanly to CSV/feeds, and make RG/compliance messaging (e.g., break‑even, implied probability) straightforward.
  • Cross‑market quoting: decimals avoid negative signs and large magnitudes, reducing confusion when books show secondary formats (e.g., moneyline) alongside a primary global default.

Which formats (HK, Malay, Indo) failed and why?

These formats have not “failed” in their home regions; they remain actively used. However, they saw limited adoption outside Asia because decimals deliver the same information with lower cognitive load for global audiences, and integrate more smoothly with European and US products.

  • Hong Kong (HK) odds: show net profit per 1 staked (h = d − 1). Simple for traders, but the difference from decimals is subtle, so most global UIs prefer decimals to avoid duplicate education.
  • Malay odds: range between −1 and +1; negative numbers indicate stake required to win 1. Negative signs and magnitudes below 1 confused recreational users in non‑Asian markets.
  • Indonesian odds: like American but scaled to a 1‑unit base rather than 100. Large absolute values (e.g., −3.33) proved harder to parse outside specialist audiences.
  • Interoperability: global oddsmaking stacks, risk, and data feeds converged on decimal and fractional; extra conversion layers added QA surface area without user benefit.
  • Regulatory disclosure: many regulators and consumer protection standards emphasise clear, prominent implied probability and returns; decimals make those disclosures trivial.

How do conversions differ on Pinnacle, exchanges, and Kalshi?

Converting a single price to implied probability is straightforward, but the trading venue changes what that probability means. A low‑vig sportsbook compresses the market margin, exchanges apply commission to net winnings (altering break‑even), and event‑contract venues like Kalshi price contracts directly as probabilities (before fees). Understanding these mechanics helps you turn “screen prices” into fair probabilities and venue‑specific break‑evens.

How does Pinnacle’s low‑vig model affect conversions?

On any sportsbook, a one‑price conversion p = 1/d includes margin. What distinguishes low‑vig books is a smaller overround (sum of implied probabilities across all outcomes), so each single conversion sits closer to the “fair” number. To remove margin, de‑vig using all outcomes:

  • Two‑way market: given decimal odds d1 and d2, raw implied p1 = 1/d1, p2 = 1/d2, overround O = p1 + p2. Fair probabilities: p̂1 = p1 / O, p̂2 = p2 / O.
  • Three‑way (1X2): compute p1 = 1/d1, px = 1/dx, p2 = 1/d2, then O = p1 + px + p2 and p̂i = pi / O for each i ∈ {1, X, 2}.
  • Effect of low vig: smaller O (closer to 1.00) means p̂i are closer to pi, so conversions require less adjustment. Always de‑vig before comparing to your model.

Exchange vs sportsbook: how does commission change break-evens?

Exchanges charge commission c on net winnings. This lowers your effective price and raises your break‑even probability on backs, and changes the lay break‑even as well. Let d be the matched decimal price.

  • Back bet (stake s): net profit if the selection wins is (d − 1) × s × (1 − c). Effective decimal deff = 1 + (d − 1) × (1 − c); break‑even probability pBE = 1 / deff.
  • Lay bet (you lay backer stake s): your liability is (d − 1) × s. If the selection loses you win s × (1 − c). Break‑even true probability for the selection to win is qBE = (1 − c) / (d − c).
  • Compared with sportsbooks: there’s no built‑in vig across outcomes on an exchange book; instead, commission bites only on wins, so account for c in every conversion.

How do I convert Kalshi contract prices to probability and payout?

On event‑contract venues like Kalshi (a CFTC‑regulated Designated Contract Market; see [https://www.cftc.gov/](https://www.cftc.gov/)), each Yes/No contract settles to $1 (Yes) or $0 (No). Ignoring fees, the mid‑price in dollars is the implied probability. Let y be the Yes price in dollars (0.01–0.99) and n = 1 − y the No price.

  • Implied probability (no fees): P(Yes) ≈ y; P(No) ≈ 1 − y.

Payoffs per $1 contract:

  • Buy Yes at y: profit if Yes = 1 − y; loss if No = y.
  • Buy No at n: profit if No = 1 − n = y; loss if Yes = n = 1 − y.
  • Returns: ROI(Yes win) = (1 − y) / y; ROI(No win) = y / (1 − y). Apply any venue fees to net winnings to obtain net ROI and adjusted break‑even.

The other side of the coin: Can odds converters mislead bettors?

An odds converter is only as good as its inputs and context. It translates formats into implied probability, but it does not guarantee the number reflects current information, venue fees, settlement rules, or the amount of money standing behind a price. This section highlights where conversions can mislead, how to read limits and depth as “error bars,” and what extra modeling you should add for robust decisions.

Why can stale lines and survivorship bias distort outputs?

Converters assume prices are current and representative. If the feed is stale or the sample is biased, the implied probability can be badly wrong even though the math is correct.

Stale lines:

  • Latency and suspensions: after news (injury, weather, red card), some books suspend and reprice while others briefly display old numbers. A converter turns that old number into a precise—but obsolete—probability.
  • Feed caching: third‑party odds screens may cache data or throttle updates. A “best price” can be minutes old, leading to phantom edges that vanish when you click through.
  • Different cut‑off rules: some operators void or adjust; others keep markets live, so quoted prices are not comparable across books at that instant.

Survivorship bias:

  • Filtering to “available” prices only: if you exclude books that moved or limited quickly, the remaining sample skews toward outliers or slow movers.
  • Winner‑only backtests: analyzing only bets that got on (or only winning markets) overstates edge because failed or cancelled attempts are missing.
  • Post‑event drift: pulling closing prices only, without intraday context, masks the path and volatility that determined fill probability and risk.
  • Mitigate by timestamping every quote you convert and recording market state (open, suspended, in‑play).
  • Cross‑check multiple venues; prefer consensus or de‑vigged aggregates over single quotes.
  • Retain failed/declined bets in your dataset to avoid survivorship bias in ROI estimates.

What do limits and market depth say about probability error bars?

A single implied probability p = 1/d says nothing about how confident the market is. Venue signals—max bet limits, quoted size, spread width, and order book depth—act like error bars around p. Thin, low‑limit markets are noisier; high‑limit, tight‑spread markets convey stronger information.

  • Max bet limits: higher limits near event start generally indicate more informed, efficient prices.
  • Depth ladders (exchanges): how much size is queued at best back/lay and one or two ticks away shows the cost to move the price—thin books are easier to push and less reliable.
  • Spread width: a tight back/lay (or A/B price parity on sportsbooks) suggests lower microstructure noise than a wide, asymmetric market.
  • Cross‑venue convergence: when multiple sharp venues cluster tightly after de‑vigging, your uncertainty narrows.

When is an odds converter not enough—and what should I add?

Conversion is step one. Robust pricing and staking require adjustments for margin, fees, and settlement, plus context from market microstructure and event rules.

De‑vig all outcomes:

  • Compute raw pi = 1/di and fair p̂i = pi / Σpi to remove overround before comparing to your model.

Account for venue fees:

  • Exchanges: apply commission to net winnings to get effective decimal and break‑even.
  • Prediction/event contracts: treat price as probability, then subtract fees from wins in EV and Kelly inputs.

Incorporate settlement rules:

  • Dead‑heat: standard method divides the stake (or the winning portion) by the number of tied selections and settles at full odds on that fraction.
  • UK Tattersalls Rule 4 (horseracing): deductions apply when a runner is withdrawn; incorporate pence‑per‑pound schedules in expected returns.
  • Each‑way terms: parameterise place fraction f (e.g., 1/5, 1/4) and places k to compute EV on both win and place parts.
  • Push/void rules: markets like “draw no bet,” Asian handicaps, and totals with whole numbers may void; include push probability in EV.

Add market microstructure:

  • Use limits, spread, and depth to weight prices and set uncertainty bands around implied probabilities.
  • Track line history to avoid acting on transient outliers.

Model‑aware staking:

  • If using fractional Kelly, base inputs on de‑vigged probabilities and effective prices after fees; cap to manage drawdown.
  • Convert and de‑vig to get fair probabilities.
  • Adjust for fees/commission and settlement rules relevant to the sport and venue.
  • Use limits and depth to grade confidence and size positions conservatively.
  • Log timestamps, availability, and declined bets to remove survivorship bias from performance metrics.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate no‑vig probabilities from a two‑way line like -110/-110 or -105/-115?

Divide each raw implied probability by the overround O, where O is the sum of both raw implied probabilities. For -110/-110, each side is 1/1.9091 = 0.52381, O = 1.04762, so fair probabilities are 0.52381/1.04762 = 0.50000 each (fair decimals 2.00). For a split -105/-115, raw p are 1/1.95238 = 0.51220 and 1/1.86957 = 0.53571, O = 1.04791, so fair p are 0.48886 and 0.51114 (fair decimals 2.046 and 1.955).

How do I convert multiple legs into an accumulator’s combined probability and adjust for exchange commission?

Multiply the leg decimals to get the acca decimal D, then back‑convert to probability p = 1/D; with exchange commission c on net wins, the effective decimal is 1 + (D − 1)(1 − c) and break‑even probability is 1 divided by that effective decimal. Example: legs 1.80, 1.95, 2.20 give D = 1.80 × 1.95 × 2.20 = 7.722, p = 12.95%; with c = 2%, d_eff = 1 + 6.722 × 0.98 = 7.5876 and p_BE = 1/7.5876 = 13.18%.

My model says 58% true probability; what minimum odds do I need after 2% exchange commission?

You need an effective decimal of at least 1/0.58 = 1.72414; solving 1 + (d − 1)(1 − 0.02) ≥ 1.72414 gives d ≥ 1.73994. That corresponds to roughly moneyline −135.1 and a fractional equivalent near 37/50.

How do pushes/voids change break‑even probability on Asian handicaps or totals?

If t is the push probability, the back break‑even win probability is w = (1 − t)/[(d − 1)(1 − c) + 1], where d is matched decimal and c is commission on net wins. Example at d = 2.00, c = 0, t = 10% gives w = 0.9/(1 + 1) = 45.00%, which is 5 percentage points lower than the 50% needed with no push chance.

How can I estimate bookmaker margin (hold) and fair odds in a three‑way 1X2 market?

Sum the raw implied probabilities to get the overround O, then divide each by O to get fair probabilities and invert to fair decimals. Example: 2.10, 3.40, 3.60 imply p = 0.47619, 0.29412, 0.27778, so O = 1.04809 (4.81% hold), fair p̂ ≈ 0.4543, 0.2807, 0.2650 and fair decimals ≈ 2.201, 3.563, 3.773.

How do each‑way terms convert into effective returns and EV?

Set place odds to 1 plus the win fractional odds multiplied by the place fraction, then compute outcomes for win, place‑only, and lose. Example: 10/1 each‑way at 1/5 odds, 3 places: win decimal 11.00, place decimal 3.00; with P(win) = 12%, P(place‑only) = 18%, per £1 win + £1 place stake the scenario profits are +£12 (win), +£1 (place‑only), −£2 (lose), giving EV = 0.12×12 + 0.18×1 − 0.70×2 = +£0.22 per £2 staked (ROI 11%).

Is +100 the same as −100 on the moneyline, and what probability do they imply?

Both +100 and −100 map to a 50% implied probability, but sportsbooks conventionally display +100 for evens. Numerically, +100 implies p = 100/(100 + 100) = 50.00% and −100 implies p = 100/(100 + 100) = 50.00%.

What rounding precision keeps implied probability error under 0.05 percentage points?

Round decimal odds to three decimals; the worst‑case probability error is at short odds and stays below about 0.035 percentage points at d = 1.20 because dp ≈ Δd/d² and Δd = 0.0005 gives 0.0005/1.44 = 0.000347. Rounding to two decimals can be 0.347 percentage points off at d = 1.20 since 0.005/1.44 = 0.00347.

What’s the implied bookmaker margin when both sides are −110?

Each side implies p = 1/1.9091 = 52.381%, so the overround is 52.381% + 52.381% = 104.762% and the hold is 4.762%. The no‑vig fair probability on each side is 50.000%.

How can I bound the fair probability if only one side’s price is visible?

Divide the raw implied probability by an assumed overround O to get an estimate; using a realistic O range gives bounds. Example: at 2.00 the raw p is 50%; if O is between 1.02 and 1.10, the fair p lies between 50%/1.10 = 45.45% and 50%/1.02 = 49.02%.

How do I value a stake‑not‑returned (SNR) free bet using implied probability?

An SNR free bet of stake s at decimal d with true probability p has EV = s × p × (d − 1); higher odds increase value linearly with (d − 1). Example: with s = £10, d = 3.50, p = 40%, EV = £10 × 0.40 × 2.50 = £10.00.

How do Kalshi‑style event contract fees change break‑even probability?

If a Yes contract price is y dollars and the venue takes fee rate f on profits, the break‑even win probability is w = y/[y + (1 − y)(1 − f)]. Example: y = $0.62 and f = 10% yield w = 0.62/(0.62 + 0.38×0.90) = 0.644, so you need about 64.4% to break even after fees.

What input ranges are mathematically valid for odds conversion?

Decimal odds must be greater than 1.00, fractional odds require positive numerator and denominator, and American moneyline cannot be 0 (use +100 for evens). Examples: 1.25 is valid, 0.99 is invalid; 11/8 is valid, 11/0 is invalid; +150 and −200 are valid, 0 is invalid.

How do I compare the same market across Pinnacle, an exchange, and a prediction market on an apples‑to‑apples basis?

Convert each to a break‑even probability after margin or fees, then compare those probabilities directly. Example: Pinnacle 1.95 implies 51.282%; an exchange back at 2.00 with 2% commission implies 1/(1 + (2 − 1)×0.98) = 50.505%; a prediction market Yes at $0.50 with 10% profit fee implies 0.50/(0.50 + 0.50×0.90) = 52.632%.

How are dead‑heats and Rule 4 deductions reflected in effective odds and returns?

In a two‑way dead‑heat, half your stake settles at full odds and half loses, so a £1 stake at 6.00 returns a net profit of £2.00 because 0.5×(6 − 1) − 0.5 = 2.0. A Rule 4 deduction of 20p in the pound on a 4.00 price makes the effective decimal 1 + (4.00 − 1)×0.80 = 3.40.
